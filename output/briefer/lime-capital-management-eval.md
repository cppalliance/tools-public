# Lime in Finance: A Brand After Its Founder

**A piece of detached reputational property that has outlived its generative founder; the form persists, the substance is replaced, and the gap is invisible.**

April 2026, by Vinnie Falco

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Mark Gorton's Lime Group built a distinctive low-latency execution franchise between 2000 and 2010. The franchise was sold off in pieces. The hedge fund vehicle (Lime Capital Management LLC) wound down through Bermuda by August 2007.<sup>3</sup> The broker (Lime Brokerage) was sold to Wedbush in 2011. Wedbush merged it with Lightspeed in 2018. Lightspeed sold it to Score Priority in November 2020.<sup>6</sup> Score Priority rebranded itself to Lime Financial in March 2022.<sup>7</sup> The brand has now been bought four times in twenty-two years, while the founder's continuing generative work sits at Tower Research Capital and at non-finance ventures the founder has steered since 2011.

The structural reason is that the institutional content of Lime - the engineering culture, the live-player adaptation, the people who made the original franchise distinctive - was never transferable. Each successive owner acquired the brand and the trademark; none acquired the source. The most recent owner is the unusual case: Score Priority has reinvested in product since 2022 rather than strip-mined the brand,<sup>17</sup> which fits the competent-buyer branch of the brand-resale literature (Tadelis 1999, Mailath and Samuelson 2001). But the September 2024 replacement of CEO Tony Huck (the last named operator carrying any original-firm history) by Eva Sehic, an outsider from Goldman Sachs, Deutsche Bank, and Sberbank New York,<sup>8</sup> completed the personnel-imprint severance that the prior fifteen years had set up.

The single most important finding is that what remains under the Lime brand is a sub-scale active-trader broker in a market that is consolidating against it. Both of its subsidy revenue pillars are under simultaneous regulatory pressure: the EU PFOF ban took effect in March 2024 with phase-out by June 2026,<sup>12</sup> and the SEC's January 2025 cash-sweep enforcement action against Wells Fargo signals a hardening regime around net interest income and the advisory-brokerage seam.<sup>14</sup> Sister-firm contagion is reactivating the categorical Gorton-Lime-Tower link in regulator and institutional awareness with each new event: Tower Research's February 2024 CBOT fine,<sup>9</sup> Tower's September 2025 Chinese customs FPGA-and-ASIC investigation alongside Jump Trading and Optiver,<sup>10</sup> and LimeWire's September 2025 acquisition of Fyre Festival rights for a Web3 revival, which Mark Gorton publicly objected to as creating brand confusion.<sup>19</sup>

The trajectory if nothing changes: the current owner's reinvestment is real but localized, and the structural drains around it are not within the owner's control. The institutional prognosis is Cargo Cult performed by a competent operator. The form persists. The substance is replaced. The gap is invisible to customers, to current operators, and to whichever next owner inherits the brand.

## 2. The Subject

The subject is the Lime brand in finance, treated as a single institution across its lineage. Its constituent legal entities and successor brands include:

- Mark Gorton's Lime Group umbrella, founded around 2000.
- Lime Brokerage, a low-latency direct-market-access broker founded 2000, sold to Wedbush in 2011, merged with Lightspeed in 2018, sold to Score Priority in November 2020 with FINRA approval,<sup>6</sup> rebranded as Lime Financial in March 2022.<sup>7</sup>
- Lime Capital Management LLC, a Delaware hedge-fund vehicle founded around 2005, with a master-feeder structure including Lime Fund LLC (onshore feeder), Lime Overseas Fund Ltd. (Bermuda offshore feeder), Lime Master Fund Ltd. (Bermuda master, EC 36480), and Lime Capital Management Administrators LLC. SEC Schedule 13G filings disclosed concentrated long-equity positions in Solutia Inc. (~7.4 percent in November 2005, amended December 2006)<sup>1</sup> and Star Gas Partners L.P. (~5.3 percent in April 2005).<sup>2</sup> Gregory E. Bylinsky served as president and director of the Bermuda vehicles.<sup>20</sup> The Bermuda master fund was struck off August 28, 2007.<sup>3</sup> Bylinsky moved to Bandera Partners after that date. No further regulatory filings are located under the Lime Capital Management LLC name.
- Tower Research Capital LLC, the founder's HFT firm, which is the surviving primary Gorton asset and is not branded Lime today; it received a $67,493,849 deferred prosecution agreement from the United States Department of Justice on October 24, 2019 for E-mini index futures spoofing 2012-2013.<sup>4</sup>
- LimeWire LLC, a parallel founder venture, found liable for contributory copyright infringement in 2010 and settled with the Recording Industry Association of America for $105 million in 2011;<sup>5</sup> the brand was acquired by separate parties post-bankruptcy and used for AI tools, a Binance Greenfield decentralized file-sharing partnership in November 2024, and the September 2025 acquisition of Fyre Festival rights for a Web3 revival.<sup>19</sup>
- The current Lime Financial group, comprising Lime Advisory Corp (SEC-registered investment adviser), Lime Trading Corp (FINRA, NFA, SIPC member broker-dealer), and Lime Trading International, under CEO Eva Sehic and President Johan Sandblom (both appointed September 2024).<sup>8</sup>

Korean Lime Asset Management and the Shenzhen Qingcheng Capital firm that brands itself as "Lime Capital" share the name but not the lineage. Both are excluded from this Brief.

The stated mission of the current Lime Financial entity is to provide professional-grade, low-latency trading tools and execution to active retail and institutional traders, with brand chronology asserting continuous operation since 2000.<sup>11</sup> The brand's claim of continuity is a load-bearing component of its current value proposition. Whether the institutional content has actually been continuous is the question this Brief answers.

## 3. The Landscape

The current Lime Financial operates in the United States online-broker plus dual-registered RIA market. The competitive landscape consolidated dramatically between 2019 and 2025.

End-2025 client assets: Schwab $11.9 trillion (+18 percent year-over-year, 46.5 million accounts); Robinhood $324 billion (+68 percent year-over-year, 27 million funded accounts); Interactive Brokers $780 billion (+37 percent year-over-year). Schwab gathered $519 billion in net new assets in 2025 alone, more than 1.5 times the entire Robinhood asset base. The average number of financial institutions per US household fell from 2.7 to 2.4 between 2023 and 2025 (Schumpeter 1942 on competitive concentration). Lime Financial's market share is sub-one percent and unmeasured in the trade data.

Upstream dependencies are conventional for the segment: clearing firms, market data vendors, exchanges, FINRA and SEC registration, market makers (the buy side of payment-for-order-flow), and mobile app distribution platforms. Downstream dependencies are active-trader retail and a small managed-account RIA book.

Market structure is a hybrid. The customer-facing side is competitive at the niche level and oligopolistic at the top. The market-maker side is increasingly oligopolistic: Citadel Securities posted record $12.2 billion trading revenue in 2025 (+25 percent year-over-year) and acquired Morgan Stanley's electronic equity-options market-making business in July 2025, including specialist posts at Cboe, Nasdaq, NYSE, and MIAX.<sup>16</sup> The two sides interact through the payment-for-order-flow auction (Rochet and Tirole 2003 on platform competition).

Vertical integration pressure is the salient supply-side change. Citadel Securities launched a white-label trading-desk service in mid-2024, explicitly targeting struggling banks and brokers whose execution stacks could be absorbed (Klein, Crawford, and Alchian 1978 on appropriable quasi-rents).<sup>15</sup> Robinhood Legend, the free advanced desktop platform launched in late 2024 with sub-second data, ninety-plus technical indicators, and AI-driven Cortex indicators, plus the HOOD Summit 2025 additions of futures on forty-plus CME products, overnight options, short selling, and trading ladders, has dragged the differentiation floor of professional-grade tooling down to zero subscription cost. The category that justified mid-tier brokers as a distinct tier between zero-commission and prime-broker has been compressed from above and below at the same time (Porter 1980 on commodity competition).

Both subsidy pillars of the active-trader brokerage model are under simultaneous regulatory pressure. The EU MiFIR Article 39a payment-for-order-flow ban entered into force March 28, 2024 with full Member-State phase-out by June 30, 2026.<sup>12</sup> SEC Rule 605 amendments (March 2024, compliance extended to August 2026) expanded execution-quality disclosure to broker-dealers themselves.<sup>13</sup> SEC release 34-102229 in January 2025 instituted administrative proceedings against Wells Fargo Clearing Services and Wells Fargo Advisors Financial Network for cash-sweep program failures running from 2019 through May 2024,<sup>14</sup> with a parallel class-action wave against multiple brokers along the RIA-and-brokerage seam (Faulhaber 1975 on cross-subsidization).

Sister-firm activity continues to attach to the brand. Tower Research Capital Investments LLC was fined $75,000 by CBOT in February 2024 for supervisory failure in an automated-trading incident.<sup>9</sup> In September 2025, Chinese customs opened a $17 million FPGA-and-ASIC hardware-smuggling investigation alleging that Tower (alongside Jump Trading and Optiver) installed customized servers and switches at the Shanghai Futures Exchange without proper 3C certification.<sup>10</sup> LimeWire's September 2025 acquisition of Fyre Festival rights for $245,000 prompted a public objection from Mark Gorton himself that the revival "creates confusion and falsely uses that brand."<sup>19</sup>

The natural-disaster exposure profile is generic to a US online broker with cloud-based DR and BCP and is not a structural concern at the brand-lineage level. Extralegal operating costs are low in the firm's primary operating jurisdictions and not materially elevated by any documented activity in higher-risk markets.

## 4. Structural Assessment

### 4.1 The Brand Detached from the Founder

The Lime brand was sold separately from Mark Gorton in 2011 (Burja 2020 on great founders). The skill stayed with the founder. What was transferable - trademark, customer list, technology stack of the moment, FINRA registration - was sold. What was not transferable - the engineering culture, the live-player adaptive capacity, the founder's vision and decision-making (Weber 1978 on succession; Stinchcombe 1965 on founder imprinting) - stayed with Gorton at Tower Research.

The post-sale generative output of the founder confirms the location. Gorton has continued to operate Tower Research Capital, has chaired Open Plans (the urban-policy nonprofit) through its 2025 and 2026 livable-city advocacy campaigns, and has publicly distanced himself from the LimeWire revival.<sup>19</sup> No comparable strategic innovation has been issued under the Lime brand chain since 2011. The post-2011 Lime is the form. Tower Research is the function. The September 2025 Chinese customs investigation, however unwelcome, is itself evidence that Tower retains the operational HFT capacity that produced the original Lime franchise; the customized FPGA and ASIC hardware reportedly installed at the Shanghai Futures Exchange<sup>10</sup> is the late-stage form of the same technical edge that founded Lime Brokerage in 2000 (Burja 2020 on functional residue location).

The personnel imprint that connected current Lime Financial to its origins ran through one named carrier: Tony Huck, who was Lime Brokerage's president and chief operating officer under Gorton, became Score Priority's CEO post-acquisition, and brought the brand to Lime Financial through the 2022 rebrand. Lugene Forte succeeded Huck as Score Priority CEO in August 2021. Eva Sehic was appointed CEO of Lime Trading Corp in September 2024, with Johan Sandblom as President.<sup>8</sup> Sehic comes from Goldman Sachs, Deutsche Bank, and Sberbank New York and carries no original Lime Brokerage history. The single named imprint channel from Marquis and Tilcsik 2013 is now closed at the chief-executive level. (medium-high)

### 4.2 The Wrapper Hardens

The brand persists across four ownership transitions while the institutional content under it has been replaced four times. Each transition resets the social technology - the engineering practices, decision-making routines, risk culture, and people-of-the-firm (Katz and Kahn 1966 on institutional entropy; North 1990 on functional institutions). The April 2026 SIFMA membership announcement, which states that Lime is an "agency-only broker-dealer founded in 2000" with "institutional-grade execution technology and low-latency infrastructure for active traders,"<sup>18</sup> is technically true about the trademark and false about the institution. The trademark dates to 2000. The people, capital, governance, and culture do not.

Identity persists along one of the three Albert and Whetten 1985 pillars: the Distinctive (low-latency direct-market-access platform plus active-trader positioning) (Whetten 2006). The Central (people, decision-making, governance) and Enduring (continuous corporate body) pillars do not survive. The Distinctive pillar is also imprinted, not chosen: the 2024 to 2026 product launches - the TakeProfit partnership in May 2024, the Low-Latency Retail API plus AI and LLM support in March 2025, Workspaces, Bulk Order Cancellation, Watchlist Sharing in April 2025, the 2D P-and-L Chart in May 2025, Indicator Constructors in July 2025, Bookmap order-flow visualization integration in March 2026<sup>17</sup> - all reinforce the original niche and constitute no observable strategic pivot. The pivot constraint is the imprint mechanism's tail (Marquis and Tilcsik 2013), not a strategic preference.

The wrapper effect is invisible to participants by construction. Practitioners who perform the brand believe they are continuing distinctive practices. Each new owner treats the brand as the baseline they purchased and lacks the reference point that would be needed to track drift; the tacit knowledge required for that reference was never transmitted (Hannan and Freeman 1977 on organizational identity). Stasis registers as stability. The September 2024 CEO transition is itself the latest expression of the pattern, and the framing of the new mandate around cost containment and operational efficiency, rather than around continuity of any prior social technology,<sup>8</sup> confirms that the new operating leadership is not pretending to continue the older one. The brand will continue to assert the older one anyway. (medium)

### 4.3 Reputation as Drawn-Down Capital

The Lime brand is reputational capital accumulated 2000 through 2010 by Mark Gorton's Lime Brokerage (Mises 1949 on capital consumption). Each successive owner has acquired the right to deploy this stock and has used some portion of it for marketing legitimacy without a corresponding reproduction of original quality (Tadelis 1999 on reputation as a tradeable asset; Mailath and Samuelson 2001 on type-revealing purchases). The brand has been transactable because reputational capital is, in the formal economic sense, a tradeable asset; the tradability is exactly what makes its drawdown invisible.

The Klein and Leffler 1981 mechanism predicts that reputation is sustained only when present-value future quasi-rents above marginal cost exceed the one-shot gain from cheating. As direct-market-access execution commoditized between 2010 and 2026, the Lime brand's quasi-rent stream fell. The predicted decay pattern is acceleration of ownership transitions - 2011, 2018, 2020, 2022 rebrand - which is the observed cadence. Score Priority's 2022 to 2026 hold of the brand at four-plus years under one ownership, with sustained product investment and a single-day execution record of 270 million shares in January 2026,<sup>17</sup> is the local exception to the decay-cadence pattern. It fits the Mailath and Samuelson 2001 competent-buyer branch in which an actor builds an average reputation upward rather than depleting an above-average one. This is the only stable or improving signal in the diagnostic record.

The competent-buyer reinvestment is real and is the most favorable element of the diagnosis, but it does not regenerate what is being drawn down. Score Priority can rebuild the platform; it cannot rebuild Mark Gorton, the engineering team that left in 2011, or the live-player decision-making that the brand originally embodied. The drawdown continues because what is being consumed is not the asset class the current owner is reinvesting in. (medium-high)

### 4.4 The Squeeze on a Mid-Tier Broker

Sub-one-percent market share in a market where the top three players gathered eighteen to sixty-eight percent year-over-year asset growth in 2025 is a structural cost disadvantage rather than a niche position (Baumol 1977 on natural monopoly; Schumpeter 1942). Strong scale economies in compliance, technology, market data, and regulatory capital absorb the fixed costs more efficiently for Schwab, Fidelity, Interactive Brokers, and Robinhood than they do for Lime. SEC Rule 605 amendments raise that fixed-cost floor disproportionately on sub-scale players<sup>13</sup> (Porter 1980 on commodity competition).

The mid-tier residue dynamic (Akerlof 1970 on adverse selection) is intensifying from both directions in 2024 to 2026. Robinhood Legend pulls value-conscious active traders down-market with a free advanced platform. Interactive Brokers added one million net new accounts in 2025 with Q3 daily-average revenue trades up forty-seven percent year-over-year and equity volumes up sixty-seven percent, pulling the margin-rate-sensitive and product-breadth-sensitive segments up-market. The traders who remain are the ones whose switching costs hold them in place. Customer staying is therefore decoupled from customer-quality validation (Suchman 1995 on legitimacy management).

Both subsidy pillars are under simultaneous regulatory pressure. The EU MiFIR Article 39a payment-for-order-flow ban reaches full phase-out in June 2026.<sup>12</sup> The SEC's January 2025 Wells Fargo cash-sweep enforcement<sup>14</sup> and the broader class-action wave at the RIA-and-brokerage seam (Jensen and Meckling 1976 on agency costs) hit net interest income and the advisory side at the same time (Faulhaber 1975 on cross-subsidization). Citadel Securities's mid-2024 white-label trading-desk service<sup>15</sup> is an explicit vertical-integration play to absorb mid-tier broker execution stacks (Klein, Crawford, and Alchian 1978), and its July 2025 acquisition of Morgan Stanley's electronic options market-making business<sup>16</sup> deepens the imbalance between the two sides of the order-flow auction (Rochet and Tirole 2003).

The pivot constraint locks the brand into the squeezed niche. The imprint mechanism cannot easily relocate Lime out of active-trader direct-market-access positioning even when that positioning becomes the worst tier to occupy (Marquis and Tilcsik 2013). (medium-high)

### 4.5 Categorical Contagion in Real Time

The brand carries the Gorton-Lime-Tower categorical legacy whether its current operators and ownership want it to or not. Audiences who retain the categorical link - financial press, regulators, sophisticated institutional counterparties, securities-industry attorneys - read each new sister-firm event as evidence about the category, regardless of legal independence (Jonsson, Greve, and Fujiwara-Greve 2009 on undeserved legitimacy loss; Karpoff and Lott 1993 on reputational penalties).

Three concurrent contagion channels are active in 2024 to 2026. Tower Research generated the February 2024 CBOT $75,000 supervisory-failure fine<sup>9</sup> and the September 2025 Chinese customs $17 million FPGA-and-ASIC hardware-smuggling investigation alongside Jump Trading and Optiver.<sup>10</sup> The 2019 Tower Research deferred prosecution agreement<sup>4</sup> remains in regulator memory and is reactivated by each new event. The LimeWire acquisition of Fyre Festival rights for a Web3 revival, for which Mark Gorton publicly objected on confusion grounds,<sup>19</sup> establishes that name-confusion spillover is real - the founder himself certified the mechanism (Jonsson, Greve, and Fujiwara-Greve 2009 on categorical sophistication scaling).

Spillover scale is asymmetric across audiences. Retail traders generally do not carry the Gorton-Lime-Tower category in their model of the broker they use; spillover at this audience tier is small. Regulators, institutional counterparties, and prime-broker / clearing relationships do carry the category, and spillover at this tier is measurable. The same brand can simultaneously hold net-positive equity in the retail audience and net-negative equity in the institutional audience, and the institutional audience controls the borrowed power on which the brand's existence depends (Grossman and Hart 1986 on ownership and bargaining).

Sub-scale online brokers are politically orphaned compared with the constituency-bearing competitors above them (Mayhew 1974 on electoral incentives). Robinhood has a retail-trader political constituency that mobilizes around its issues. Schwab has institutional weight and a wealth-management lobby. A regulatory or licensing event affecting Lime would not produce a senator's call. The combination of borrowed power, contagion exposure, and absent political defenders means a revocation of any borrowed-power input would not be politically friction-managed and would land at the speed of the licensing process itself. (medium)

## 5. Institutional Durability

The institutional prognosis for the Lime brand-lineage is **Cargo Cult performed by a competent operator** (Burja 2020). The form persists. The substance has been replaced four times. The current operator is unusual in that it is reinvesting in the operating platform rather than strip-mining the brand, but the reinvestment cannot regenerate the institutional content the brand asserts continuity with. The 2026 brand statement that Lime is the broker founded in 2000 is true only of the trademark. The people, the engineering culture, the live-player decision-making, and the founder's generative capacity that made Lime distinctive in 2005 are at Tower Research and at Gorton's other ventures, not at Lime Financial.

The institutional-health diagnostic produced findings on eleven of seventeen baseline tests forming a coherent picture: no live player at the brand-lineage level, succession failure at every transition, borrowed power dominating with thin owned residue, depreciating legitimacy not regenerated by current operations, no unique structural function in the niche, original social technology not preserved, high imitation distance, entropy resistance only in the trademark layer, structural information asymmetry between customers and operators, and absence of institutional self-correction across ownership transitions. The institutional content has not been continuous. The trademark has. Cargo cult is the precise term Burja uses for that arrangement.

The mitigation is genuine but localized. Score Priority's competent-buyer reinvestment branch (Tadelis 1999, Mailath and Samuelson 2001) is the only stable or improving signal in the diagnostic record, and it operates against a background of degrading dynamics on every other axis. If the current owner held the brand for another ten years and continued to reinvest at the 2024 to 2026 cadence, the institution under the brand would over time become its own institution - one that happened to use a heritage trademark but that no longer required the heritage to function. That outcome would convert the cargo-cult diagnosis into a Functional verdict for a new institution that bears the old name. None of the directional evidence in 2024 to 2026 suggests this trajectory is the modal outcome; the squeeze, the subsidy attack, and the contagion channels apply pressure faster than the reinvestment can compound. (medium-high)

## 6. Predictions

**Short term (12 to 24 months).**

If the EU PFOF ban completes its phase-out by June 2026 as scheduled and SEC Rule 605 expansion compliance reaches its August 2026 deadline, then Lime Financial's revenue model faces a cost reset that the brand-marketing budget cannot offset, and the visible margin compression will surface in product-investment cadence; the January 2026 record-volume month becomes the local peak rather than a trend continuation. If the regulatory timeline slips, the squeeze paces more slowly and Score Priority's reinvestment branch has more runway. Confidence: medium-high (regulatory trajectories are observable and on calendar).

If a fifth ownership transition is announced in this window, the Klein-Leffler decay-cadence pattern reasserts and the brand enters the late-stage phase. If not, the four-plus-year hold by the current owner stands as the deviation from the cadence and the competent-buyer interpretation strengthens. Confidence: medium (no transaction signal observed in the 2024 to 2026 directional record; the prediction is residual on the structural pressure).

**Medium term (24 to 60 months).**

If sister-firm contagion produces a new headline event at the cadence the 2024 to 2025 record establishes (Tower CBOT, Tower China, LimeWire-Fyre Festival), institutional-audience legitimacy spillover compounds and the brand's regulator and counterparty relationships harden materially. If the channels go quiet, categorical sophistication scaling keeps the spillover concentrated among informed observers and the brand absorbs the legacy without acute disruption. Confidence: medium (one of these channels is structurally active and producing events at twelve to eighteen month cadence).

If consolidation continues at the 2023 to 2025 pace - Schwab, Robinhood, and IBKR continuing to gather net new assets at eighteen percent year-over-year and above - sub-one-percent brokers contract further and the squeeze becomes existential at the cost-coverage level rather than the strategic level. If consolidation stalls (recession, regulatory action against scale players, antitrust intervention in the market-maker tier), Lime's niche gets a reprieve. Confidence: medium-high (consolidation is the dominant directional signal in the market structure cluster).

If the advisory-and-brokerage seam continues to harden via cash-sweep class actions and SEC enforcement extending beyond Wells Fargo, the dual-registered structure that Lime uses (Lime Advisory Corp plus Lime Trading Corp) absorbs additional fixed compliance cost and the principal-agent misalignment becomes a per-account drag rather than a residual concern. Confidence: medium.

**Long term (5 to 10 years).**

If the brand survives past 2030 under Score Priority/Lime Financial's stewardship without a fifth ownership transition, the most probable steady state is a wrapper around a stagnant operating book whose customers stay for switching costs, with the imprint pivot constraint preventing reallocation toward institutional execution, professional-trader infrastructure, or RIA platform consolidation. Structurally inferred. Confidence: medium (no directional evidence on this timescale; prediction is consequence of the imprint mechanism and squeeze compounding).

If Mark Gorton or Tower Research were to face a major new enforcement action in a Western jurisdiction, the categorical contagion would land hardest on the brand among regulators and institutional counterparties, and the brand might need to be retired or rebranded again - completing one more turn of the brand-aggregator pattern that Polaroid, RadioShack, and other heritage trademarks have already traced through. Confidence: low-medium (depends on contingent enforcement actions and on whether the brand owner concludes that the heritage trademark has become a net liability rather than a net asset).

If the active-trader retail niche commoditizes fully into AI-orchestrated portfolio management plus zero-commission execution, the imprint constraint forces Lime to defend a niche that has stopped existing as a distinct category. The structural inference from imprinting plus commodity-trap plus pivot constraint is convergent on this outcome. Confidence: low-medium.
