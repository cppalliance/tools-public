# Staker: Anthropic, PBC

**Governance architecture captured by the founders it was designed to constrain**

A $965B Public Benefit Corporation whose oversight bodies - a self-perpetuating trust, a PBC charter, and voluntary safety commitments - have never independently checked management on any substantive decision.

June 2026, by Vinnie Falco

---

## 1. Executive Summary

Anthropic is a Delaware Public Benefit Corporation valued at $965B. Revenue reached $47B ARR by May 2026. The company holds 32-40% of enterprise LLM API spending and serves 300K+ business customers. It filed confidentially for IPO on June 1, 2026.

The dominant stakeholder dynamic is the Domestication Circuit. The LTBT reached board majority in April 2026. It has never overruled management. Trustees were selected "in consultation with the company." The board has never prevailed against founder preference. RSP v3.0 made the training pause conditional on competitive position and management self-assessment. No independent body constrains hyperscaler compute dependency, founder concentration risk, or strategic direction. The governance architecture is elaborate, untested, and structurally disposed toward the leadership it nominally oversees.

The PBC charter names "humanity" as the intended beneficiary. Actual primary beneficiaries are the Amodei siblings, who hold founder equity and operational control over a $965B entity with no external constraint on their authority. Amazon has invested $13B+ and booked $16.8B in pre-tax gains on its stake in Q1 2026, exceeding its total cash investment. Google holds 14% equity worth $135B against $10B+ invested. Ninety-four pre-IPO institutional investors hold positions at 15x+ paper gains. The general public has no enforcement mechanism, no standing to sue under PBC law, and no representation in governance.

The trajectory is degrading. Of 48 assessed findings, 26 are worsening, 14 are stable, 4 are improving. Governance capture is stable because it remains untested. Compute dependency is deepening. Vertical foreclosure is accelerating as Claude Code competes with third-party tools built on the Claude API. Safety-brand erosion is a secondary trend affecting talent retention and coalition durability but not the commercial foundation.

## 2. The Organization

Anthropic, PBC was founded January 26, 2021, by seven former OpenAI employees. Delaware Public Benefit Corporation. Headquartered in San Francisco. Headcount 2,500-5,000. Total funding $132B across 18 rounds. Valuation $965B after the May 2026 Series H.

The stated mission is "the responsible development and maintenance of advanced AI for the long-term benefit of humanity." This language is codified in the certificate of incorporation.

The actual purpose is building and selling frontier AI models. Enterprise API generates roughly 80% of $47B ARR. Claude.ai serves approximately 30M consumers. Claude Code produces $2.5B ARR. Thirteen vertical products span coding, design, research, and enterprise workflow automation. The company filed for IPO.

Dario Amodei serves as CEO. PhD biophysics, Princeton. Co-invented RLHF at OpenAI. Controls strategy, research direction, and external policy. Daniela Amodei serves as President and runs all day-to-day operations: product, engineering, sales, marketing. Five other co-founders hold senior technical roles: Jared Kaplan (Chief Science Officer and Responsible Scaling Officer), Tom Brown (Chief Compute Officer), Chris Olah (Interpretability Research Lead), Sam McCandlish (Chief Architect), Jack Clark (Head of Public Benefit, leads the Anthropic Institute).

The board has seven members. The Long-Term Benefit Trust holds Class T shares granting authority to elect a board majority, a threshold reached in April 2026. Three trustees serve: Neil Buddy Shah (Chair), Richard Fontaine, Mariano-Florentino Cuellar. Two seats remain vacant. Trustees hold no financial stake and elect their own successors.

Blau-Scott classification: business organization with commonweal aspirations. Enterprise customers are the prime beneficiary in practice. The divergence between actual purpose and stated mission is a governance-context finding. It explains why the LTBT and PBC structure exist, not the analytical baseline against which findings are assessed.

## 3. The Landscape

### Market position

Anthropic is the most valuable private AI company at $965B, surpassing OpenAI's $852B. Revenue of $47B ARR passed OpenAI in April 2026. Enterprise market share stands at 32-40% of LLM API spending. Claude Code generates $2.5B ARR. Eight of the Fortune 10 are customers. Over 1,000 customers spend more than $1M per year.

Consumer reach lags sharply. Claude.ai has roughly 30M users against ChatGPT's 900M weekly users. The revenue base depends on enterprise API concentration, not consumer breadth.

### Ecosystem dependencies

Three hyperscalers form the compute foundation. Amazon has invested $13B+ and committed to Project Rainier, a cluster of nearly 500,000 Trainium2 chips. Anthropic has committed $100B+ on AWS over the next decade. Google holds 14% equity, has invested $10B+, and provides access to up to 1M TPUs. NVIDIA supplies GPUs and holds roughly $10B in equity at a 2.6% stake.

All three distribute Claude through their cloud marketplaces. All three develop competing models. Amazon builds Nova. Google builds Gemini. Simultaneous dependency and competition span every major infrastructure relationship.

Microsoft invested $5B and distributes Claude on Azure Foundry but remains primarily an OpenAI partner.

### Domain-specific vulnerabilities

Government intervention has materialized. On June 12, 2026, Commerce Department export controls forced a global shutdown of Fable 5 and Mythos 5, affecting hundreds of millions of users. The Department of War designated Anthropic a supply chain risk on March 3, 2026 - the first such designation against an American company.

Copyright litigation exposure exceeds $4.5B. The $1.5B Bartz v. Anthropic settlement covers 500,000 works. Concord/UMG v. Anthropic II seeks $3B+ for 20,000+ songs and names the CEO as an individual defendant.

Safety researcher departures are a secondary risk affecting talent retention and coalition durability. Mrinank Sharma, head of safeguards research, resigned in February 2026 with a public letter reaching 10M+ views. RSP v3.0 drew criticism from safety rating organizations that downgraded the company. These departures erode the safety coalition's internal mass. They do not threaten the commercial foundation.

The IPO filing introduces execution risk at a $965B valuation requiring sustained extraordinary growth. Regulatory shocks, safety incidents, or revenue deceleration during the pre-IPO period carry outsized consequences.

## 4. Structural Assessment

### 4.1 The Domestication Circuit

**Dominant dynamic.** The governance architecture is captured by the founders it was designed to constrain.

Dario Amodei sets strategy, research direction, and external policy. Daniela Amodei runs all operations. No third party holds comparable authority over any organizational function. This concentration matches Michels's iron law of oligarchy: formal democratic structures yield to incumbent control as organizations scale.

The LTBT achieved board majority in April 2026. It has never overruled management on any substantive decision. The three seated trustees were selected "in consultation with the company." Two seats remain vacant. The Trust Agreement is unpublished. No external challenger has displaced an incumbent in any governance body since founding.

Board capture compounds founder concentration. The board has seven members. No member has publicly dissented from a management proposal. No vote against management is documented. The LTBT's authority to elect a board majority is formal. Its exercise of that authority has tracked founder preference without exception.

Safety accountability diffuses across five structures: RSP commitments, LTBT oversight, board governance, the Responsible Scaling Officer, and external evaluators. No single body owns a binding safety decision. When METR evaluates model capabilities, it does so at Anthropic's discretion and on Anthropic's schedule. RSP v3.0 made the training pause conditional on competitive position and management self-assessment. The diffusion is structural, not incidental. It functions as an accountability sink: responsibility fragments until no party bears it.

Consequence: no independent body constrains founder decisions on compute commitments ($100B+ to AWS), competitive strategy, succession planning, or organizational direction. The governance body designed to check founders is structurally disposed toward founder deference. For a $965B enterprise AI company approaching IPO, this is the load-bearing failure.

### 4.2 The Hyperscaler Shadow State

Amazon has invested $13B+ and holds consultation rights over strategic decisions. Google holds 14% equity, has invested $10B+, and maintains access to sensitive company information per FTC filings. Neither holds a board seat. Both control compute infrastructure that Anthropic cannot replicate or replace on any relevant timeline.

The UK CMA assessed Amazon's consultation rights as potentially conferring "material influence" over Anthropic's commercial policy. This assessment identified a governance channel that operates outside formal board structures.

Shadow channels settle strategic constraints before formal governance encounters them. Compute allocation, marketplace placement, infrastructure pricing, and API distribution terms are negotiated bilaterally between Anthropic and each hyperscaler. These negotiations determine which products ship, at what margin, and on what timeline. The LTBT has no visibility into these channels. The board's visibility is uncertain.

Four veto players hold divergent interests. The US government demonstrated enforcement capacity with the June 2026 global model shutdown. Amazon and Google control infrastructure and compete with their tenant. The LTBT holds formal authority it has never exercised. Copyright plaintiffs hold $4.5B+ in claims. The distance between these players entrenches the status quo: change requires simultaneous alignment across parties with incompatible objectives.

Consequence: the entities that control compute infrastructure also set strategic parameters through bilateral channels invisible to formal governance. For an enterprise AI company existentially dependent on hyperscaler compute, the shadow state is the operative governance layer.

### 4.3 The Vertical Squeeze

Claude Code competes directly with third-party developer tools built on the Claude API. It surpassed Cursor and GitHub Copilot in revenue, reaching $2.5B ARR. Anthropic reportedly approached Figma and Canva to partner, then expanded Claude Design to compete in the same space. Figma's CEO stated Anthropic was "not consistently candid" about its intentions.

This is vertical foreclosure. The platform owner enters the downstream market and competes with its own customers. The dynamic is standard in platform economics (Rochet and Tirole 2003). It is also irreversible: once a platform demonstrates willingness to compete downstream, third-party developers price that risk into every integration decision.

Upstream foreclosure operates simultaneously. AWS, Google Cloud, and Azure distribute Claude through their marketplaces while developing Amazon Nova, Google Gemini, and competing models. Anthropic depends on these distributors for enterprise reach. The distributors compete with their tenant for the same enterprise budgets.

Bidirectional lock-in compounds both dynamics. The $100B+ AWS commitment and the million-TPU Google deal create switching costs that prevent exit in either direction. Anthropic cannot leave its compute providers. Its compute providers cannot easily replace the revenue.

Consequence: simultaneous vertical foreclosure downstream and upstream creates an adversarial dynamic with no clean exit. Enterprise customers face platform risk from both directions. Developer trust erodes with each competitive expansion.

### 4.4 The Information Fortress

Anthropic holds model capability information that no external party can independently verify. METR, the primary external evaluator, conducts assessments at Anthropic's discretion. METR's own researchers described the arrangement as "grading its own homework."

No external certification regime exists for frontier AI comparable to the FAA for aviation, the FDA for pharmaceuticals, or the NRC for nuclear energy. This is an industry-wide gap, not an Anthropic-specific failure. Dario Amodei called for FAA-style AI certification in June 2026, acknowledging the gap while operating within it.

Cloud providers add a second information filter. Enterprise customers access Claude through AWS, Google Cloud, or Azure. The cloud provider mediates the relationship. Capability claims, safety disclosures, and performance benchmarks pass through two layers of institutional interest before reaching the customer.

The information asymmetry described by Akerlof (1970) applies with particular force to dual-use technology. The seller knows the capability profile. The buyer, the regulator, and the investor cannot verify it. NIST AI 800-1 identifies this as a structural risk for dual-use AI systems.

Consequence: an information monopoly on capability assessment creates structural risk for regulators, investors, and customers. The risk is not that Anthropic misrepresents capabilities. The risk is that no external institution can confirm or deny the representations.

### 4.5 The Succession Vacuum

No succession plan is publicly documented. Dario Amodei holds all key external relationships: policy engagement, press interaction, research community standing. Daniela Amodei holds all operational relationships: product, engineering, sales, marketing, partnerships. Neither role has a documented second.

Key safety researchers have departed. Mrinank Sharma resigned publicly. Meg Scandinaro and others left without public statements. Each departure removes tacit knowledge that cannot be transferred through documentation. The departures concentrate remaining knowledge in fewer individuals.

All eight co-founders came from a single institution (OpenAI), represent a single age cohort, and remain active in the organization. This demographic concentration creates correlated risk. A single event - regulatory action, health crisis, competitive poaching - could remove multiple founders simultaneously.

The LTBT provides structural continuity in theory. In practice, the three seated trustees lack operational expertise in AI research, product development, or enterprise sales. Two seats remain vacant. The Trust was designed to outlast the founders. It was not designed to replace them.

Consequence: for a company approaching a potential IPO at $965B, the absence of documented succession is a material governance risk. Departure of either Amodei would create a leadership vacuum with no transfer mechanism. The LTBT cannot fill an operational role it was not designed to perform.

### 4.6 Governance Context: The Mission-Commerce Divergence

This subsection is governance context. It is not a pathology.

The PBC charter names "humanity" as the intended beneficiary. Revenue comes from enterprise API customers, who generate roughly 80% of $47B ARR. This gap is arithmetic. Every commercially successful PBC exhibits it. The divergence explains why the LTBT and PBC structure exist, what they were designed to enforce, and why their capture (Section 4.1) matters.

RSP v3.0 made safety commitments conditional on competitive position. Safety researcher Sharma resigned citing values erosion. The Centre for Future Generations downgraded Anthropic's safety rating. These are secondary trends. They affect talent retention, safety coalition durability, and reputational positioning. They do not threaten the commercial operation.

Moral legitimacy erosion is real. It affects enterprise customer trust in regulated industries (healthcare, finance, government). It affects safety community endorsement, which influences researcher recruitment. It affects the credibility of policy positions that rely on safety leadership claims. But the $47B revenue base rests on model quality and API execution, not on safety branding.

The governance architecture - PBC charter, LTBT, RSP - was built around the stated mission. The stated mission was the justification for creating these mechanisms. Without the stated mission, there is no LTBT. The LTBT's capture is pathological not because the mission is being abandoned, but because an elaborate governance system exists that constrains nothing. The mission-commerce divergence is the context in which that capture operates. It is not itself the dominant dynamic.

### 4.7 Domain-Specific Findings

**Amoral drift resilience.** LTBT enforcement rights belong to stockholders, not trustees (Enriques and Gatti 2025). The mechanism is untested. No stockholder has brought a PBC enforcement action against Anthropic. The legal pathway exists in Delaware statute. Its practical viability is unknown. (medium)

**Regulatory capture dynamics.** Anthropic spent $3.1M on lobbying and $20M on political engagement. Dario Amodei's June 2026 call for FAA-style certification proposes a regime that would favor well-capitalized incumbents (Dobusch et al. 2025). Safety advocacy and competitive moat-building produce identical policy outputs. The two motivations are indistinguishable from the outside. (medium)

**Export control compliance.** Demonstrated. The June 2026 global shutdown of Fable 5 and Mythos 5 in response to Commerce Department export controls affected hundreds of millions of users. Compliance was immediate and complete. (high)

**Institutional purpose embedding.** Safety values are weakening as the organization scales (Selznick 1957). Approximately 119 staff hold dedicated safety roles against 2,500+ total headcount. The ratio has declined as commercial hiring outpaced safety hiring. Researcher departures accelerate the dilution. (medium)

**Vertical foreclosure.** Claude Code competes with API customers. Revenue surpassed Cursor and GitHub Copilot at $2.5B ARR. Thirteen vertical products now span coding, design, research, and enterprise workflow automation. Each vertical expansion converts a potential API customer into a competitor. (high)

## 5. Cui Bono

### Stated beneficiary

The certificate of incorporation names "humanity" as the intended beneficiary. The PBC charter obligates the board to balance stockholder financial interests against the public benefit purpose. This is governance context. It explains why the LTBT exists and why its capture matters. It is not the analytical baseline.

### Actual primary beneficiaries

Four parties capture the overwhelming share of economic value.

**Amodei siblings.** Founder equity in a $965B entity. Operational control over all functions. No external constraint on authority. No documented succession mechanism. The governance architecture designed to limit their power has never checked it (Section 4.1). Every dollar of valuation appreciation accrues to their equity position. Every strategic decision reflects their preference.

**Amazon.** $16.8B in pre-tax gains on its Anthropic stake in Q1 2026, exceeding $13B+ in total cash investment. Infrastructure revenue from the $100B+ compute commitment. Distribution revenue from Claude on AWS Marketplace. The investment has already returned more than its cost on paper, before counting recurring compute and distribution income.

**Google.** 14% equity stake worth $135B at the $965B valuation, against $10B+ invested. TPU revenue from up to 1M chips allocated. Cloud marketplace distribution revenue. Paper appreciation alone represents a 13x+ return.

**Pre-IPO institutional investors.** Ninety-four institutional investors hold positions at 15x+ paper appreciation. The Series H at $965B followed rapid valuation increases from $61B in March 2025 to $175B in January 2026 to $965B in May 2026. IPO would convert paper gains into liquid positions.

### The gap

The PBC charter names humanity. The revenue model serves 300K+ enterprise customers. The equity structure serves founders and investors. No public enforcement mechanism exists. Stockholders can bring PBC enforcement actions under Delaware statute. No stockholder has done so. The general public has no standing.

This gap is the expected baseline for a commercially successful PBC, not a pathology. The benefit structure tracks normal commercial incentive alignment. The pathology is the governance capture documented in Section 4.1, which removes the only mechanism designed to enforce the stated purpose.

### Secondary beneficiaries

**Enterprise API customers.** 300K+ businesses access frontier AI capability. Eight of the Fortune 10. Over 1,000 customers spend more than $1M annually. They benefit from model quality. They bear platform risk from vertical foreclosure (Section 4.3).

**Employees.** Pre-IPO equity positions at 2,500-5,000 headcount. Compensation competes with peer frontier labs. Safety-role employees face a secondary risk: values erosion documented by departures may reduce the cohort willing to stay.

**NVIDIA.** $10B invested at 2.6% stake. Hardware demand from Anthropic's compute expansion. Each hyperscaler commitment to Anthropic infrastructure generates GPU and accelerator orders upstream. NVIDIA benefits from the compute dependency it supplies.

### Negative externality bearers

**Creative professionals.** The $1.5B Bartz settlement covers 500,000 pirated books at roughly $3,000 each. Concord/UMG v. Anthropic II seeks $3B+ for 20,000+ songs. Total pending claims exceed $4.5B. The settlement establishes a per-work price for training data extraction. Creative professionals bore the cost of model training before any compensation was negotiated.

**Workers in exposed occupations.** 37.1M workers fall in the top exposure quartile for AI displacement. Anthropic's 13 vertical products target coding, design, research, and enterprise workflow automation. Each product directly substitutes for paid human labor. The displaced workers have no seat in governance and no share in the equity appreciation their displacement enables.

**General public.** Safety risks with no external verification mechanism (Section 4.4). No representation in governance. No standing under PBC law. No enforcement pathway. The stated beneficiary has the least access to the organization's decision structures.

### Analytical frames

Benefits concentrate among a small number of parties with organized interests. Costs diffuse across millions of unorganized individuals (Wilson 1980; Olson 1965). Founders, investors, and hyperscalers gain specific, measurable returns. Creatives, displaced workers, and the general public bear costs that are individually small enough to prevent organized resistance.

Hyperscaler investment subsidizes compute at preferential rates (Faulhaber 1975). The $100B+ AWS commitment and million-TPU Google deal provide infrastructure below standalone market cost. Anthropic cannot operate without this subsidy. The subsidy creates existential dependency. The dependency transfers strategic leverage to the subsidizer.

Amazon and Google capture infrastructure revenue and equity appreciation exceeding any governance role either plays (Coff 1999). Amazon holds no board seat. Google holds no board seat. Both extract value that dwarfs the returns available to the LTBT trustees, who hold no financial stake. The value extraction tracks infrastructure control, not governance participation.

### Structural conflicts

Holden Karnofsky led the RSP v3.0 rewrite. He is married to Daniela Amodei (Davis 1982). Amazon invests $13B+ and competes with Nova. Google invests $10B+ and competes with Gemini. These are structural conflicts in the commercial operation. They arise from the dual roles that hyperscalers and insiders occupy. They do not require bad faith. They produce systematic bias toward the interests of parties who hold multiple roles.

Google develops Gemini, a direct competitor across every product category. The FTC flagged Google's access to sensitive Anthropic information. Both parties maintain the relationship because the financial entanglement makes exit costly. Trajectory: stable tension.

**Anthropic - US Government.** Conflict. The Department of War designated Anthropic a supply chain risk on March 3, 2026. Commerce Department export controls forced a global shutdown of Fable 5 and Mythos 5 on June 12, 2026. The government demonstrated enforcement capacity that exceeded any action by the LTBT or board. Trajectory: escalating. The regulatory surface area expands with each new model release and each new vertical product.

Revenue growth favors Anthropic. Consumer reach favors OpenAI at 900M weekly users against 30M. The competition is structural and zero-sum on enterprise API share. Trajectory: intensifying.

Sharma's resignation letter reached 10M+ views. The Centre for Future Generations downgraded the company. This is a secondary relationship. It affects talent pipeline quality and reputational credibility. It does not affect revenue or compute access. Trajectory: degrading.

### Coalitions

**Commercial coalition.** Amazon, Google, enterprise customers, pre-IPO investors. Bound by financial interest. Total capital commitment exceeds $132B. Members tolerate governance weakness because the commercial operation generates returns. This coalition is stable as long as revenue grows. Revenue deceleration or IPO failure would fracture it.

**Governance coalition.** LTBT, board, Responsible Scaling Officer. Nominally independent. Actually captured by founders (Section 4.1). The coalition has formal authority and no demonstrated willingness to exercise it. Its function is legitimation, not constraint. Stable because untested.

**Government-defense coalition.** Department of War, Commerce Department/BIS, NIST. Bound by national security interest. Demonstrated enforcement capacity exceeds all other coalitions combined. The June 2026 shutdown affected hundreds of millions of users. No other stakeholder has taken an action of comparable scope. Growing in influence.

### Network brokers

The Amodei siblings bridge all three coalitions. Dario Amodei interfaces with the commercial coalition through investor relations, with the governance coalition through board participation, and with the government-defense coalition through direct policy engagement. Daniela Amodei interfaces with the commercial coalition through operational partnerships and with the governance coalition through LTBT coordination.

No other individual spans more than one coalition. This makes the Amodeis a single point of failure for inter-coalition communication. It also concentrates brokerage power. Information that crosses coalition boundaries passes through them. Their departure would sever the connections between coalitions that currently have no independent communication channel.

### Fault lines

**Government vs. company autonomy.** Active conflict. The DoW designation and export controls represent direct assertions of state authority over corporate operations. Anthropic complied immediately with the June 2026 shutdown. The precedent establishes that government enforcement operates on a faster timeline and at greater scale than any internal governance mechanism. This fault line will deepen with each new dual-use capability.

**Hyperscaler vs. company independence.** Structural tension. The $100B+ AWS commitment and million-TPU Google deal create bilateral lock-in. Anthropic cannot exit these relationships. Its compute providers cannot easily replace the revenue and utilization. The tension is stable as long as mutual benefit persists. It becomes acute if either hyperscaler's competing model gains enough share to reduce the strategic value of the tenant relationship.

**Vertical foreclosure upstream and downstream.** Accelerating. Downstream: Claude Code and 13 vertical products compete with API customers. Upstream: AWS, Google Cloud, and Azure distribute Claude while developing competing models. Each new vertical product and each new competing model widens the squeeze. Trajectory is one-directional. No market force or governance mechanism reverses it.

### Hidden influence

**Amazon.** Formal role: investor with no board seat, no governance participation. Actual influence: compute infrastructure control, consultation rights over strategic decisions, marketplace distribution terms, Project Rainier resource allocation. The gap between formal and actual influence is the widest of any stakeholder. The CMA identified this gap. No remedial action followed.

**Google.** Formal role: 14% equity holder with no board seat. Actual influence: TPU supply control, access to sensitive company information (per FTC filings), cloud marketplace distribution, competitive intelligence from the tenant relationship. Google's information access exceeds that of the LTBT, which has no documented access to bilateral hyperscaler negotiations.

**Karnofsky.** Formal role: technical staff. Actual influence: led the RSP v3.0 rewrite, the company's primary safety commitment framework. Married to the President. The structural conflict (Davis 1982) operates regardless of intent. The individual who architected the safety framework holds a personal relationship with a co-founder that no external party can evaluate for independence.

Its board majority authority is formal. Its exercise has tracked founder preference without exception. Its trustees were selected in consultation with the company they oversee.

Direction: stable. The capture is stable because it remains untested. The approaching IPO represents the first genuine test. Public company governance imposes disclosure obligations, fiduciary duties, and shareholder scrutiny that private company governance does not. If the LTBT demonstrates independence during the IPO transition, the assessment changes. If it does not, the capture is confirmed under conditions of maximum visibility.

## 9. Predictions

### Short-term (0-2 years)

**1. LTBT governance test at IPO.** The trust holds board majority authority. The IPO is the single event where exercising that authority - imposing dual-class protections, succession requirements, trustee transparency - would be structurally meaningful. If the LTBT imposes conditions, governance independence exists. If it does not, governance capture becomes permanent and the trust's relevance collapses. The IPO is the trust's load-bearing moment. (medium)

**2. IPO proceeds at $965B+ valuation.** Public-market shareholders introduce a new principal with fiduciary expectations orthogonal to safety mission. Governance capture by founders persists unless public-market dynamics create an independent board faction. If delayed, pre-IPO investors holding $76B+ face liquidity pressure that compounds their incentive to accept capture. (medium-high)

**3. Music copyright litigation forces settlement.** Combined exposure from Concord/UMG II ($3B+) and related actions approaches $5B. Discovery with CEO deposition creates personal liability for a founder filing an IPO. Settlement pressure intensifies proportionally to IPO timeline proximity. The litigation becomes a governance event because its resolution requires founder decision-making under personal financial constraint. (medium-high)

**4. Export control regime formalizes.** Commerce Department faces August 2026 deadline on formalizing the control framework that produced the Fable/Mythos restrictions. Formalization gives Anthropic regulatory predictability and compliance planning capacity. Continued ad hoc directives erode enterprise customer confidence and create model deployment uncertainty that compounds the vertical foreclosure risk. (medium)

**5. Hyperscaler leverage materializes or remains latent.** Amazon ($100B+) and Google (14% equity, TPU supply) possess structural leverage over pricing, marketplace placement, and competitive restrictions. If exercised, the shadow governance dynamic becomes overt conflict. If the relationship remains cooperative, mutual lock-in sustains the status quo. The trigger condition is hyperscaler development of frontier models matching Claude quality. (medium)

### Medium-term (2-5 years)

**1. Compute partnerships shift from collaborative to coercive.** Amazon and Google each develop frontier models. Platform incentives favor their own models over a competitor's. Anthropic's distribution dependency - 80% of revenue through hyperscaler channels - becomes a competitive liability rather than a growth engine. The partnership structure that enabled scaling becomes the constraint that limits it. (medium-high)

**2. Vertical foreclosure contracts developer ecosystem.** Each first-party product (Claude for Enterprise, Claude Code, domain agents) competes with API customers who built on Claude. Enterprise customers face platform risk identical to the dynamic that eroded trust in prior platform ecosystems. If Anthropic restrains first-party competition, the API ecosystem strengthens. Current trajectory favors foreclosure. (medium)

**3. Mandatory external certification creates independent constraint.** EU AI Act implementation and potential US legislation impose compliance requirements independent of internal governance. Certification bodies become the first genuinely independent constraint on Anthropic's operations - a function that captured internal bodies (RSP, LTBT, PBC charter) have never performed. Compliance cost creates barriers but also creates accountability. (medium)

### Long-term (5-10 years)

**1. Founder departure creates governance vacuum.** No documented succession mechanism exists. No institutional process transfers authority, knowledge, or relationships held by the Amodei siblings. The LTBT provides structural continuity only if it has demonstrated independence before that moment. If the trust remains captured at the point of departure, governance vacuum is total. The organization's tacit knowledge concentration in two individuals is a single point of failure. (medium)

**2. Open-source convergence compresses frontier premium.** If open-weight models reach 90%+ of frontier capability, API pricing collapses and the $47B revenue model faces structural compression. Governance capture becomes irrelevant to a commodity business - there is nothing left to capture. If frontier differentiation persists, governance quality determines whether the premium is sustained through genuine value creation or extracted through lock-in. (medium)
