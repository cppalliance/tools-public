# The Vasa - Coherence Report: P2300R10 + P2900R14 - May 2026

## Executive Summary

P2300R10 (std::execution) and P2900R14 (Contracts for C++) are structurally compatible. Their foundational designs do not contradict each other, and several interaction points show positive coherence: P2900's "Concepts Do Not See Contracts" principle (ART-8) hermetically seals P2300's concept-based type machinery from contract interference; implicit const-ness in predicates aligns naturally with P2300's read-only environments (ART-17); the no-implicit-lambda-capture rule (ART-30) protects P2300's zero-allocation sender pipelines from contract-induced closure layout changes; and both papers independently avoid dynamic allocation in core infrastructure (ART-21). Two principles (Rule 6: Type Equality, Rule 22: No Preprocessor Dependence) find no concerns whatsoever. The type system is sound across their boundary, and neither paper forces adoption of the other.

However, the inspection reveals two HIGH-severity structural tensions and a dominant compound dynamic. The most pervasive finding is that P2900's global, monolithic, implementation-defined customization model (link-time violation handler, compiler-flag semantic selection) is fundamentally incommensurable with P2300's composable, per-type, compile-time domain customization mechanism. This single interaction is flagged by 11 of 24 principles. It means execution domain authors - the primary extensibility audience of P2300 - have no programmatic control over contract behavior within their domains. The second structural concern is that contracts designed for synchronous function semantics lose expressiveness when applied to P2300's lazy, async composition model: postconditions bind to opaque sender handles rather than eventual results, coroutine postconditions cannot reference parameters, and preconditions fire at construction time rather than execution time. The third concern, elevated to HIGH by Rule 5 (Visible Unsafety), is that contract violations flowing through sender exception machinery become indistinguishable from ordinary exceptions at consumer call sites, hiding broken invariants behind clean catch syntax. These are not design defects in either paper individually. They are emergent cross-paper tensions visible only when the hull is inspected as a whole.

## Compound Dynamics

### COMPOUND 1: The Monolithic Handler / Implementation-Defined Semantics Complex

**Artifacts:** ART-16, ART-25
**Principles stressed:** Rules 1, 3, 10, 14, 15, 16, 18, 19, 21, 23, 24 (11 of 24)

This is the dominant structural finding. P2300's execution domain mechanism provides composable, per-type, compile-time control over algorithm behavior. P2900's violation handler is global and monolithic (link-time replacement), and its semantic selection is entirely implementation-defined (compiler flags). These two customization models are incommensurable: a domain author who uses `transform_sender` to customize every aspect of algorithm behavior cannot influence how contracts behave within that domain - not the enforcement level, not the violation response, not the handler behavior.

The tension is elevated to HIGH by Rule 23 (Local Verification) because the global handler creates action-at-a-distance and the implementation-defined semantics defeat local reasoning. It is reinforced at MODERATE by Rules 10, 14, 16, 19, 24 across composability, integration, and expressiveness dimensions.

This is not a defect in either paper alone - P2900's global handler follows established C++ precedent (`operator new`/`delete`), and P2300's domain mechanism is a new C++26 pattern. But the combination means the two major C++26 extensibility stories (execution domains for async, replaceable handler for contracts) exist in parallel universes.

### COMPOUND 2: The Lazy/Async Expressiveness Gap

**Artifacts:** ART-3, ART-4, ART-14, ART-20, ART-24
**Principles stressed:** Rules 13, 19, 20, 24

P2900's contracts were designed for synchronous function semantics: preconditions fire at entry, postconditions bind to the return value. P2300's sender-returning functions return opaque lazy descriptions of future work. When postconditions bind to a sender, they check the handle, not the eventual async result. Coroutine postconditions bind to the coroutine handle, not the co_returned value. Preconditions on adaptors check construction-time state, not execution-time correctness.

The "contract" of a sender - what it will do when started - is expressed through compile-time completion signatures, unreachable from runtime contract predicates. The two checking systems are complementary (one compile-time, one runtime) but the runtime system has near-zero expressiveness for the async use case. This affects "Useful Now" (Rule 13) because postconditions on sender-returning functions are useful only to experts who understand the gap, and "Direct Concept Mapping" (Rule 20) because the programmer's mental model of "result" diverges from the language's.

### COMPOUND 3: The Invisible Danger Complex

**Artifacts:** ART-2, ART-5, ART-13, ART-19
**Principles stressed:** Rules 5, 23

Contract violations flowing through P2300's exception machinery become invisible at consumer call sites. The path: violation occurs - throwing handler's exception caught by sender try-catch - routed to `set_error(current_exception())` - `sync_wait` rethrows as ordinary exception. At the `sync_wait` call site, the caller catches a `std::exception` with no indication that program invariants have been breached (not just a normal operational failure).

Additionally, nothrow mandates on core execution operations (`get_env`, scheduler operations) silently convert any contract violation with a throwing handler into unconditional termination - the syntax of the contract looks identical whether on a throwing or nothrow function. The exception scoping question (does the sender's try-catch encompass contract evaluation?) is also invisible at the call site. Rule 5 (Visible Unsafety) rates this HIGH because the rocks have been erased from the chart.

### COMPOUND 4: The Type Erasure Boundary

**Artifacts:** ART-26
**Principles stressed:** Rules 3, 5, 8, 9, 10, 12, 13, 14, 19, 20, 24 (11 principles)

P2900 prohibits contracts on virtual functions (deferred to future). P2300's core is concept-based, but the practical ecosystem requires type-erased senders. At the type-erasure boundary, contracts are lost. Individual severity is low across all 11 principles, but the breadth of impact (11/24 principles at low severity) indicates a pervasive low-grade concern that will intensify as the ecosystem builds type-erased async interfaces. This is explicitly a deferred feature, not an oversight.

### COMPOUND 5: Mixed-Mode ODR Amplification

**Artifacts:** ART-28
**Principles stressed:** Rules 4, 14, 23

P2900 permits different TUs to use different contract evaluation semantics. P2300's sender algorithms are template-heavy and header-only. The combination amplifies ODR risk: the same template instantiated in two TUs with different contract semantics may produce different instantiations if predicates have side effects interacting with constant expressions. Rule 23 rates this HIGH because the hazard is fundamentally non-local - each TU appears correct in isolation. P2900 acknowledges the risk but P2300's nearly-entirely-template design makes the surface area unusually large.

## Tensions by Severity

### HIGH

**1. Global Handler Incommensurable with Execution Domain Customization**

- Artifacts: ART-16, ART-25
- Papers: P2300R10 (domain-based algorithm customization, transform_sender, apply_sender), P2900R14 (link-time replaceable violation handler, implementation-defined semantic selection)
- Principles flagged: Rules 1, 3, 10, 14, 15, 16, 18, 19, 21, 23, 24

P2300R10 introduces execution domains as the composability primitive for customizing async algorithm behavior. A domain author controls algorithm dispatch via `transform_sender`, environment propagation via `transform_env`, and operation application via `apply_sender` - all at compile time, all per-type, all composable. P2900R14's violation handler is a single global function replaced at link time (like `operator new`), and which evaluation semantic applies to any contract assertion is entirely controlled by implementation-defined compiler flags.

An execution domain cannot mandate that contracts within its operations use enforce semantics for safety-critical work, or ignore semantics for performance-critical bulk operations. The violation handler cannot distinguish between violations occurring in different execution domains. A high-assurance domain (medical, automotive) running alongside a best-effort domain (logging, telemetry) in the same program must share a single violation response policy. This defeats Rule 23 (Local Verification) because reasoning about contract behavior requires global knowledge of the violation handler and compiler flags, not just local knowledge of the domain. It defeats Rule 10 (Complementarity) because the two extensibility mechanisms cannot interact. Rule 24 (Generality) is stressed because domain-specific contract behavior is an obvious generalization that the current design precludes.

**2. Contract Violations Laundered Through Error Channel (Invisible Danger)**

- Artifacts: ART-13, ART-2, ART-5
- Papers: P2300R10 (sync_wait rethrows exceptions, exception catching in then/bulk), P2900R14 (throwing violation handler, exception propagation semantics)
- Principles flagged: Rule 5 (HIGH), Rule 23 (moderate)

When a contract violation occurs inside a sender pipeline and the violation handler throws, the sender's exception-catching machinery (which catches exceptions in `then`, `bulk`, etc. and forwards them via `set_error(rcvr, current_exception())`) captures the handler's exception. `sync_wait` then rethrows it as an ordinary exception. At the call site:

```cpp
try {
    auto result = this_thread::sync_wait(my_sender);
} catch (const std::exception& e) {
    // Is this a network timeout? A file-not-found?
    // Or a BREACHED PROGRAM INVARIANT from a contract violation?
    // Impossible to distinguish without dynamic_cast probing.
}
```

The caller has no syntactic or structural indication that what they caught represents a broken invariant rather than a recoverable operational failure. Rule 5 (Visible Unsafety) requires that unsafe situations be "visible and diagnosable without specialist knowledge." Here, the unsafe situation (program invariant breached) has been laundered into the same channel as ordinary operational errors.

Additionally, contracts placed on nothrow execution infrastructure (`get_env`, scheduler operations, stop token operations) with a throwing handler always terminate unconditionally. The syntax of the contract looks identical on a nothrow function as on a throwing one - the unsafety is invisible at the declaration site.

**3. Mixed-Mode ODR Risk Amplified by Template-Heavy Sender Design**

- Artifacts: ART-28
- Papers: P2300R10 (template-heavy, header-only sender algorithms), P2900R14 (different TUs may use different evaluation semantics)
- Principles flagged: Rule 23 (HIGH), Rule 4 (low), Rule 14 (moderate)

P2900R14 explicitly permits different translation units to compile with different contract evaluation semantics. P2300R10's sender algorithms are almost entirely templates, instantiated in user translation units. If a contract predicate has side effects that affect constant expressions (which P2900 acknowledges as a risk), the same template instantiated under different evaluation semantics in different TUs may produce different object code, violating ODR.

The hazard is fundamentally non-local: each translation unit appears correct in isolation. The combination of P2300's "everything is a template" design with P2900's "semantics vary per TU" permission creates an unusually large surface area for this class of defect. Rule 23 (Local Verification) rates this HIGH because diagnosing the resulting undefined behavior requires cross-TU analysis that no single developer at a single call site can perform.

### MEDIUM

**4. Lazy/Async Expressiveness Gap for Postconditions**

- Artifacts: ART-3, ART-4, ART-20
- Papers: P2300R10 (lazy sender model, coroutine integration), P2900R14 (postcondition result binding, coroutine restrictions)
- Principles flagged: Rule 13 (medium), Rule 19 (moderate), Rule 20 (medium), Rule 24 (signal)

Postconditions on sender-returning functions bind `r` to the sender object (an opaque `basic-sender`), not to the eventual async result. The sender's public interface exposes only compile-time properties (completion signatures, tag, domain) - nothing about what it will compute. Similarly, coroutine postconditions bind to the returned handle (e.g., `task<T>`), not to the co_returned value, and P2900 makes it ill-formed to odr-use parameters in coroutine postconditions.

The practical consequence is that postconditions on the sender/async API surface have near-zero expressiveness for checking functional correctness. Preconditions on sender adaptors (`then`, `let_value`) fire at construction time, checking only the construction-time state, not the execution-time invariants that matter.

**5. when_all Branch Cancellation from Contract Violations (Observe+Throw)**

- Artifacts: ART-19
- Papers: P2300R10 (when_all cancels siblings on error/stopped), P2900R14 (observe semantic continues, throwing handler)
- Principles flagged: Rule 5 (medium), Rule 23 (moderate)

With the observe evaluation semantic, a contract violation invokes the handler and continues execution. If the handler throws, the sender's exception machinery catches the throw and routes it to `set_error`, which triggers `when_all` to cancel all sibling branches. A contract violation in one concurrent branch - which the "observe" semantic was supposed to let the program survive - ends up killing all parallel work. The cancellation of siblings is an observable program behavior change caused solely by the presence of a contract assertion, which tensions P2900's Prime Directive ("presence/evaluation of contract assertions should not alter program correctness").

**6. Forward Progress Budget Consumed by Contract Checks in Bulk Operations**

- Artifacts: ART-9, ART-18
- Papers: P2300R10 (bulk parallel invocation, forward progress guarantees), P2900R14 (non-ignore semantics have runtime cost)
- Principles flagged: Rule 1 (medium)

P2300's `bulk` invokes a function across an index space with the forward progress guarantee of the scheduler. Under enforce or observe semantics, each invocation evaluates its contracts, multiplying the checking cost by the bulk shape. For a weakly_parallel scheduler providing minimal forward progress guarantees, contract evaluation in bulk callbacks could consume the entire progress budget. The violation handler (which may allocate, do I/O, or lock) is especially problematic if invoked from a parallel execution agent with restricted capabilities.

**7. Observable Checkpoints Constraining Sender Pipeline Optimization**

- Artifacts: ART-11, ART-12
- Papers: P2300R10 (strictly lazy ordering, domain-based transform_sender optimization), P2900R14 (observable checkpoints conditional on P1494R5, side effect elision)
- Principles flagged: Rule 1 (drift)

Observable checkpoints prevent time-travel optimizations from reordering past a checked contract assertion. If contracts appear within sender callbacks, and the domain's `transform_sender` would otherwise fuse or reorder operations for efficiency, the observable checkpoint anchors prevent those transformations. Side effect duplication (P2900's implementation-defined repetition) in hot sender paths adds further unpredictable overhead. This is conditional on P1494R5 adoption but represents a potential optimization ceiling.

### LOW

**8. Type Erasure Boundary (Deferred Feature)**

- Artifacts: ART-26
- Papers: P2300R10 (concept-based, type-erased wrappers anticipated), P2900R14 (virtual functions ill-formed for contracts)
- Principles flagged: 11 principles at low severity

Contracts on virtual functions are explicitly deferred. Type-erased sender wrappers (e.g., `any_sender`) use virtual dispatch internally. Contracts must be placed before erasure, not on the erased interface. Low severity today, but the ecosystem pressure will grow.

**9. Constant Evaluation and Constexpr Senders**

- Artifacts: ART-27
- Papers: P2300R10 (constexpr-friendly patterns), P2900R14 (trial-then-real evaluation for constexpr)
- Principles flagged: None at medium or above

Trial evaluation ignoring contracts, then re-evaluation with contracts active, applies coherently to constexpr sender operations. A terminating violation makes the expression ill-formed. Minor interaction, no tension.

**10. noexcept Infrastructure Forces Binary Outcome**

- Artifacts: ART-5
- Papers: P2300R10 (nothrow mandates on core operations), P2900R14 (noexcept + throwing handler = terminate)
- Principles flagged: Rule 5 (component of HIGH finding), Rule 21 (low)

Contracts on nothrow execution infrastructure with a throwing handler always terminate. Non-throwing handlers work fine. The binary is clear once understood, but invisible at the declaration site.

**11. Postconditions on connect() Bind to Non-Movable Operation State**

- Artifacts: ART-14
- Papers: P2300R10 (operation_state non-movable, non-copyable), P2900R14 (result binding)
- Principles flagged: Rule 24 (signal)

Postconditions on `connect` can only check structural properties of the returned operation_state (type, address), not behavioral properties (what will happen on start). Limited utility but not harmful.

**12. stop_callback Blocking Destructor and Contract Termination**

- Artifacts: ART-15
- Papers: P2300R10 (inplace_stop_callback destructor blocks), P2900R14 (contract termination)
- Principles flagged: None at medium or above

Contract termination kills threads blocked in stop_callback destructors. This is a general consequence of program termination, not specific to contracts.

## Positive Coherence

The following interaction points demonstrate the two proposals reinforcing each other:

**ART-8: Concepts Don't See Contracts.** P2900's Principle 3 explicitly excludes contract predicates from SFINAE, concept satisfaction, `if constexpr`, and the `noexcept` operator. This hermetically seals P2300's entire concept hierarchy (`sender`, `receiver`, `scheduler`, `operation_state`, `queryable`, `stoppable_token`) from any interference by contract annotations. A type satisfying `sender` with always-failing preconditions still satisfies `sender`. The two type-checking systems occupy orthogonal spaces by deliberate design.

**ART-17: Implicit Const-ness Aligns with Read-Only Environments.** P2300's environments are conceptually read-only key/value stores. P2900 makes all external variables implicitly const (shallow) within contract predicates. Both papers independently converge on restricting mutation in contexts where it would be problematic. Environment queries (`get_env`, `get_scheduler`, `get_stop_token`, `get_allocator`) are nothrow and const-compatible, making them natural targets for use within contract predicates.

**ART-21: Zero-Allocation Philosophy Shared.** P2300 achieves zero-allocation for structured patterns through intrusive linked lists in operation states. P2900 specifies that `contract_violation` object memory is not allocated via `operator new`. Both papers avoid dynamic allocation in their core mechanisms, supporting resource-constrained deployment scenarios.

**ART-30: No Implicit Lambda Captures Protects Sender Pipelines.** P2300's sender pipelines are built from lambdas passed to adaptors (`then`, `let_value`, `bulk`). P2900's rule that programs are ill-formed if all references to a local entity occur only within contract assertions of a lambda prevents contracts from silently growing closure objects. This protects the zero-allocation property of structured sender pipeline construction.

**ART-10: Freestanding Contracts Available for Execution Primitives.** P2900's `<contracts>` header is freestanding, enabling contract annotations in the embedded/kernel environments where P2300's zero-allocation patterns are most valuable.

**ART-23: Receiver Environments Accessible from Predicates.** Environment queries work naturally within the implicit-const context of contract predicates, enabling rich preconditions on receiver operations (e.g., checking whether a stop token is stoppable).

**ART-24: Completion Signatures and Contracts Are Complementary.** Compile-time completion signatures express the type-level "contract" of a sender (what completions it can produce). Runtime contract assertions express behavioral conditions. The two systems are complementary and non-overlapping - neither interferes with the other.
