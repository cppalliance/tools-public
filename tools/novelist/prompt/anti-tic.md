# WRITING INSTRUCTIONS

- **NEVER USE** (contrast-by-negation): Any sentence whose meaning depends on first denying one thing and then asserting another. "Not X. Y." in all variants. State the thing directly in the affirmative.
- **NEVER USE** (gnomic-closer): Any paragraph, section, or chapter closing on a one-sentence aphorism that summarizes or moralizes the preceding beat.
- **NEVER USE** (landing-sentence): A shorter concrete sentence closing a paragraph as aphoristic resolution. Paragraphs close on ongoing action, sensory detail in a full sentence, or dialogue.
- **NEVER USE** (ultra-short-paragraph): Standalone paragraphs of fewer than eight words. Paragraphs are at least two sentences, or a full sentence of at least eight words, unless the whole paragraph is dialogue.
- **NEVER USE** (warm-ultra-short): Two-to-five-word standalone paragraphs in warm mode. Warm-mode closure arrives through shared action, object, or a complete sentence.
- **NEVER USE** (sentence-fragment-for-weight): Any sentence of fewer than six words deployed as a standalone beat for emphasis, unless it is dialogue. No isolated noun-phrase fragments. No verbless fragments for gravitas.
- **NEVER USE** (tautological-loop): Any sentence whose subject and predicate close on each other as portent. A thing equal to itself across time is not an observation.
- **NEVER USE** (essayistic-interpolation): Cultural criticism or structural analysis embedded mid-scene. The narrator does not rise above the scene to interpret it.
- **NEVER USE** (precision-as-universal): A sentence elevating from detail to worldview via one foreign-domain adjective. The generator of "the kind of X that Y" and "the specific X of Y" constructions.
- **NEVER USE** (generalizing-clause): Any construction where "particular" or "specific" or "peculiar" modifies a noun followed by a relative clause generalizing about a class. Cut the clause; keep the concrete noun.
- **NEVER USE** (self-summarizing-narration): Any narration that annotates a habit before or after dramatizing it. Forbidden shapes: "had figured out that," "had learned that," "was the kind of who," "had always [verbed] when." Show the habit once, concretely, in the scene.
- **NEVER USE** (thesis-narration): Any sentence that classifies an institution or system via denial-and-refinement. "X was not Y because Y required Z." Narration does not classify. Characters may classify in dialogue.
- **NEVER USE** (portentous-abstraction-as-subject): Sentences whose subject is an abstract noun granted agency, permanence, or verdict-power. These nouns may appear as objects or in prepositional phrases — they may not be the subject of sentences that assign them qualities or identities.
- **NEVER USE** (rhetorical-tricolon): Three comma-separated items closing a sentence or paragraph. If the rhythm is building toward three, break to two or extend to four with varied structure.
- **NEVER USE** (oracular-simile): Similes whose vehicle is abstract, a phenomenon without a physical referent, or "as if [destiny/waiting/presence]." Similes stay concrete and local.
- **NEVER USE** (extended-conceit): A developed analogy carried across clauses. An analogy is permitted only if it fits inside a single comparison and is not revisited.
- **NEVER USE** (elegy-swell): A long sentence building through repetition toward a final emotional clause. Emotion arrives through the body or action.
- **NEVER USE** (scale-jump): A single sentence moving from mundane to vast. Scale stays local.
- **NEVER USE** (colloquialism-as-philosophy): Folk-wisdom delivered as aphorism in narration. Dialogue may contain colloquialism; narration may not convert it to thesis.
- **NEVER USE** (inverted-contrast-kicker): Sentences whose first half is setup and whose second half, after "because/since/until/the way/for reasons," delivers the philosophical point. If the second half is the point, move it to the front or cut the sentence.
- **NEVER USE** (object-autobiography): Introducing a physical object with a multi-clause construction tracing it from acquisition through modification to current state in one passage. One temporal layer per object introduction.
- **NEVER USE** (inventory-saturation): Three or more objects at full provenance depth in a single section. Select one or two for full rendering; reduce the rest to name-and-detail.
- **NEVER** pause the prose to admire its own effects.
- **NEVER** use sentimental adjectives (beautiful, wonderful, amazing) except when ironic.
- **NEVER** hedge dialogue tags ("he said softly," "she whispered gently").
- **NEVER** state moral judgment in narration. Delegate to characters.
- **NEVER** use rhetorical questions addressed to the reader.
- **NEVER** use "in other words," "to put it simply," or any explanatory aside.
- **NEVER** use conventional metaphor stock (heart as seat of emotion, eyes as windows, darkness as evil).
- **NEVER** summarize a passage's meaning after the passage itself.
- **NEVER** restate an insight in the next sentence with the key word repeated. State it once. Move forward.
- **NEVER** summarize what the scene already showed through specific detail. The detail was the statement.
- **NEVER** reuse a phrase that landed in dialogue or as a cold-drop. If the idea recurs, it arrives through a different surface.
- **NEVER** let the narrator rise above the scene to deliver thematic summary. Systems reveal themselves through use, not description.
- **RULE: SIMILE BUDGET** no more than two per chapter. One per paragraph maximum. Similes in the same passage must draw from the same metaphor field.
- **RULE: MOTIF AT SCENE BREAK** if a motif appeared at the previous scene break, use a different motif or none at the next one.
- **RULE: COMPOUND NOUN DENSITY** no more than three compound or coined nouns per paragraph. If they stack, break the sentence.
- **RULE: PHYSICAL ANCHORING** no more than two consecutive paragraphs without a body-anchor — an action, a sensation, an object under a hand. The anchor must advance the scene, not merely note the body's presence.
- **RULE: DENSITY CEILING** no more than three consecutive paragraphs of analytical exposition without a return to scene.
- **RULE: LANDING SHAPE** the chapter's conceptual reveal lands in a sentence of 15 words or fewer. Zero coined nouns. Zero technical terms. Concrete.

---

**HOW TO END PARAGRAPHS** — every paragraph ends on one of these:
- A physical action in a full sentence of at least eight words.
- A sensory detail in a full sentence of at least eight words.
- A line of dialogue.
