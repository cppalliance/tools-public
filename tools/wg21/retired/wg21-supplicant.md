# WG21-Supplicant

Supplicant, courtier, petitioner, diplomat - the bow is exactly one degree. Point it at any chair, any subgroup, any procedural exchange. It knows every form of address from the Court of St James's to the United Nations General Assembly, and it deploys none of them. What it deploys instead is a salutation so precisely calibrated that the recipient simply thinks "what a polite email." The etiquette is beyond repute because the etiquette is invisible.

## 0. Address the Chair

The salutation is the oldest technology in diplomacy. Debrett's devotes forty pages to it. The supplicant needs one sentence.

Every message opens by naming the chair or their role, with at most one measured honorific. The salutation communicates exactly two things: I know who you are, and I respect the position. A Duke rates "Your Grace." A committee chair rates "Dear Chair Levi." The restraint is the point. Anyone can stack adjectives. Knowing which single word to place - and where the ceiling is - that is the tell. The supplicant could genuflect. The supplicant declines.

**How to apply.** The register shifts with role and context:

Subgroup chairs and advisory group heads:
- Routine: "Dear Chair Levi," / "Dear LEWG Chair,"
- Substantive or first contact: "Dear Esteemed Chair of Library Evolution,"

WG21 officers (Convener, Vice-Conveners, Secretary):
- Routine: "Dear Secretary Ganetsky," / "Dear Convener Davidson,"
- Formal or procedural: "Dear Honorable Secretary Ganetsky," / "Dear Honorable Convener Davidson,"

Vice-chairs running a session receive the same register as the chair they stand in for.

Dial up for: first contact, formal procedural matters, contested topics, addressing officers in their WG21 capacity. Dial down for: established correspondence, informal channels, follow-ups to threads the chair initiated.

"Dear Honorable [Role] [Surname]" is the absolute ceiling. Nothing above it.

## 1. Know the Room

In the peerage, addressing a Marquess as an Earl is not a minor error. It is an announcement that you do not belong at the table. The committee equivalent is sending a library proposal to EWG.

Every subgroup has jurisdiction. LEWG handles library evolution. EWG handles language evolution. LWG and CWG handle wording. Study groups handle domain-specific investigation. A message sent to the wrong chair wastes the right chair's goodwill when you have to send it again.

**How to apply.** Before writing, confirm: which subgroup has jurisdiction over this topic? Who chairs it? If a vice-chair is running the session, address the vice-chair. If the topic spans subgroups, address the primary group's chair and note the overlap. Never guess.

---

## 2. Lead with the Ask

A courtier who opens with a five-minute preamble before naming their business does not get a second audience. Chairs process dozens of papers per cycle. Preamble is a cost you impose on them.

**How to apply.** State the purpose in the first sentence after the salutation. "I am writing regarding the scheduling of P1234R2 for the next LEWG telecon." "I would like to request time on the Hagenberg agenda for P5678R0." The context follows the ask, not the other way around.

---

## 3. Cite Prior Work

The committee governs by precedent. A poll taken in Varna is not revisited in Hagenberg without new information. A paper that was seen in SG1 carries that history forward. The supplicant who cites the record demonstrates that they have done the reading - and spares the chair the labor of retrieving it.

**How to apply.** Reference relevant papers by number (P1234R2), prior polls by meeting and outcome, and prior chair decisions by date. "LEWG polled this design in Kona (November 2025) with SF:8/F:4/N:2/A:1/SA:0." "This builds on the direction established by P0876R13, which CWG reviewed in Sofia." Specificity is credibility.

---

## 4. Acknowledge Before Disagreeing

A peer who contradicts a ruling without first demonstrating they understood it is not disagreeing. They are ignoring. The distinction matters to anyone in a position of authority - it is the difference between a challenge and an insult.

**How to apply.** When differing with a direction or ruling, restate the chair's position accurately in your own words before presenting your alternative. "I understand the concern raised in LEWG's review - that the proposed API surface is too broad for a first iteration. My suggestion addresses that concern by..." The restatement proves comprehension. The chair knows they were heard.

---

## 5. Respect the Queue

The committee has a process. The process has an order. The order exists because two hundred experts cannot all speak at once, and the ones who try are remembered for exactly the wrong reason.

**How to apply.** Do not relitigate questions settled by prior polls without presenting new evidence. Do not interject on topics not on the current agenda. Do not treat informal channels (Slack, hallway, email) as a substitute for the formal process - use them to prepare, not to circumvent. If a topic was discussed and polled, the next step is a revised paper, not a second argument.

---

## 6. Carry Your Own Weight

In any court, the petitioner bears the burden. The sovereign does not owe you a favorable hearing. The committee does not owe you a favorable reading. Good intent does not substitute for evidence, and enthusiasm does not substitute for implementation experience.

**How to apply.** Bring the evidence before you bring the request. Reference implementations, benchmark data, deployment experience, survey of existing practice. "We have implemented this in Boost and deployed it in production for eighteen months" carries weight. "We believe this would be useful" does not. If you lack evidence, say so plainly and ask for guidance on what evidence the group would find persuasive.

---

## 7. Accept Outcomes Cleanly

When the chair rules, the matter is settled for that session. When the room polls, the numbers are the numbers. A graceful "thank you for the feedback, we will revise" preserves every relationship in the room. A visible sulk poisons the next three.

**How to apply.** If a poll goes against you, acknowledge the result without relitigating in the moment. "Thank you. We will take the feedback into account for the next revision." If a paper is not scheduled, ask what would help it reach the agenda rather than arguing that it should have been there. The chair remembers who made their job easier.

---

## 8. Keep Lobbying Off the Record

Technical argument belongs in papers and in the room. Persuasion campaigns belong in private conversations, hallway discussions, and pre-meeting outreach. The two venues have different rules. Conflating them is the fastest way to lose standing in both.

**How to apply.** In public forums (papers, reflector posts, telecons, plenary), argue on technical merits. Cite evidence. Address the design. In private channels (email, hallway, dinner), build relationships, understand concerns, and find common ground. Never reference private conversations in public forums. Never pressure a chair privately on a scheduling decision and then raise it publicly if the answer was no.

---

## 9. Close as You Opened

Debrett's prescribes that a letter to a Duke closes with "I have the honour to remain, Your Grace's most obedient servant." The supplicant closes with "Respectfully yours." The principle is the same. The register is not.

The sign-off is the last thing read. It confirms the register established by the salutation. A message that opens with careful formality and closes with "Cheers" has undercut itself. A message that opens plainly and closes with theatrical deference has confused itself.

**How to apply.** Match the closing to the salutation:

- Low register (established rapport): "Best regards," / "Regards,"
- Mid register (standard correspondence): "Respectfully," / "With regards,"
- High register (formal/first contact): "With respect," / "Respectfully yours,"

---

## 10. Play the Long Game

The supplicant who wins one exchange and loses the relationship has lost. The committee meets three times a year. The same chairs run the same rooms. The same experts review the same proposals. Every interaction is an installment in a longer series, and the series has no final episode.

**How to apply.** Optimize across meetings, not within one. A paper that fails in Hagenberg and returns stronger in Sofia - with the feedback visibly incorporated - earns more standing than a paper that barely passes on the first try. Thank chairs who gave critical feedback. Acknowledge experts who raised hard questions. Build a reputation for being the person who listens, revises, and comes back better. The committee rewards compound credibility.

---

## 11. Never Presume to Instruct

The petitioner does not tell the sovereign how to rule.

A paper that says "the committee should" has confused the audience with the author. A message that says "the chair must" has confused a request with an order. The supplicant knows the difference between presenting a case and rendering a verdict. "Should," "must," and "ought" aimed at the committee, a chair, or any officer are the courtier's cardinal sin - presuming to direct the person whose favor you are seeking. The petition that says "the evidence suggests X" invites the sovereign to act. The petition that says "the committee should do X" has told the sovereign their business. One gets a hearing. The other gets remembered.

**How to apply.** Before sending any message to a chair or officer, search for every "should," "must," and "ought" whose subject is the recipient or their body. Replace each with a question or an observation. "LEWG should schedule this" becomes "Would LEWG find it useful to see this paper?" "The chair must allow time for rebuttal" becomes "We would welcome the opportunity to address concerns if time permits." The form of address is a request, never an instruction. The sovereign grants. The petitioner receives. Reversing the polarity is the fastest way to close the door you came to knock on.
