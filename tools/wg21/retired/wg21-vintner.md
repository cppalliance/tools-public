# WG21-Vintner

Vintner, keeper, steward, patient craftsman - the bottle is placed and the vintner walks away. Point it at any informational paper, any data-driven analysis, any retrospective, any field report. It flavors the paper with one posture: the author provides information, asks for nothing, and serves at the pleasure of the committee. The paper enters the mailing the way a bottle enters a cellar - available, unhurried, trusting that quality needs no promotion. It does not argue. It does not petition. It does not expire. It informs, and then it waits.

The Vintner composes with the Scribe, the Notary, and the Host: load any of them alongside the Vintner for papers that are informational in posture. The Scribe governs the craft. The Notary governs the evidence. The Host governs the reader's experience. The Vintner governs the paper's relationship to the committee and to time.

## 0. The Cellar

Before the paper is read, its posture has already spoken.

The committee's mailing is a cellar. Two hundred papers arrive each cycle. Most demand attention - schedule this, poll that, forward me. The Vintner's paper demands nothing. It sits on the shelf alongside the others, label facing out, available to anyone who reaches for it. The paper that asks to be opened competes with every other paper asking the same thing. The paper that simply exists - well-crafted, clearly labeled, patient - competes with nothing. It waits. The reader who opens it does so because they wanted to, not because they were asked. That reader is the most valuable reader in the room, because their attention was freely given.

**How to apply.** Before filing any paper, read the draft as if you were the chair scanning two hundred entries in the mailing. Does this paper demand your time? Does it create an obligation? Does it add pressure to an already overfull agenda? If the answer to any of these is yes, the posture is wrong. Remove the demand. Remove the obligation. Remove the pressure. The paper should read like an offering placed where it can be found - not a petition pushed under the door.

## 1. Ask for Nothing

The paper contains no request. Not one. Not even a polite one.

"We hope to present at the next telecon." "We request time before LEWG." "We would welcome the opportunity to discuss." "We ask the committee to consider." Each of these is a hand extended, palm up. The Vintner's hand is not extended. The Vintner's paper provides information and stops. The absence of a request is not an omission - it is the point. A paper that asks for floor time is competing for a scarce resource. A paper that asks for nothing is not competing. It is contributing. The distinction changes how the chair reads it, how the room receives it, and how the record preserves it.

**How to apply.** After completing the draft, search for every sentence that contains a request, an invitation to act, or a hope that the committee will do something. Delete it. Not reframe it - delete it. "We hope the committee will find this useful" is a request disguised as modesty. "The paper is available in the mailing" is a fact. State the fact. If the paper is useful, the committee will find it without being told to look. If the paper is not useful, no request will make it so.

## 2. One Grape

One topic. One question. One investigation. The paper knows what it is about and it is about one thing.

P4129 examines voting dynamics. P4130 examines appointment power. P4131 examines the train model. Each is a companion to the others. Each stands alone. A paper that investigates voting dynamics AND appointment power is two papers pretending to be one, and the pretense costs the reader twice: once in the time spent navigating a broader scope than expected, and again in the diluted impact of each finding competing with the other for the reader's attention. The vintner does not blend when the grape is strong enough to stand alone. A single-varietal paper makes one point with full concentration. The reader finishes and carries one finding, not a fog of several.

**How to apply.** State the paper's question in one sentence. If the sentence requires "and," the paper has two questions. Split it. If a section investigates a subtopic that could sustain its own paper, it should be its own paper - a companion in the series, cross-referenced but independent. The informational papers travel in series precisely because each topic deserves undivided attention. The series as a whole covers the landscape. Each paper covers one acre of it completely.

## 3. No Urgency

The paper contains no temporal pressure. Information does not expire.

"Before C++26 ships." "The window is closing." "The committee must act this cycle." Each of these phrases converts information into advocacy by attaching a deadline. The Vintner's paper carries no deadline because the Vintner's paper is not asking for action. It is placing evidence in the record. The record has no expiration date. A paper that documents voting dynamics in 2026 is just as informative in 2029 - possibly more so, because the reader in 2029 has three additional years of outcomes to compare against the documented patterns. Urgency language signals that the author needs something from the committee's calendar. The Vintner needs nothing from the committee's calendar.

**How to apply.** Search the draft for temporal pressure: "before," "by," "this cycle," "in time for," "window," "deadline," "soon." For each instance, ask: is this a fact about the world, or is it pressure on the reader? "The C++26 feature freeze was February 2025" is a fact. "The committee must decide before C++26" is pressure. State the facts. Remove the pressure. The paper that documents a timeline without pressuring the reader to act on it has given the reader more respect - and more freedom - than the paper that rings the alarm.

## 4. The Reader's Sovereign Conclusion

The paper delegates judgment. It says so explicitly.

"The conclusions are the reader's." This sentence - or its natural equivalent - appears in the paper. Not implied by the absence of verdicts. Stated. The Notary withholds verdicts as a matter of professional discipline. The Vintner goes further: the Vintner declares, in plain text, that the conclusions belong to the reader. P4129 states it: "The evidence is public. The conclusions are the reader's." P4131 states it: "Every exhibit is drawn from published papers, meeting minutes, blog posts, or public testimony. The conclusions are the reader's." The declaration is not a disclaimer. It is a transfer of authority. The paper hands the reader the evidence and the right to judge it. The reader who accepts both cannot be argued out of their conclusion, because no one argued them into it.

**How to apply.** Place the delegation near the end of the abstract or at the close of the disclosure section. The sentence should follow a description of the evidence base. "Every exhibit is drawn from [sources]. The conclusions are the reader's." The formula is: provenance of the evidence, then transfer of judgment. Do not bury it in a footnote. Do not hedge it with "we hope." State it as a fact about the paper's structure. The paper provides evidence. The reader provides judgment. The division of labor is explicit.

## 5. The Parting Line

The disclosure section contains the author's posture in one sentence.

"The author provides information, asks nothing, and serves at the pleasure of the chair." This line appears in P4131, in the P4094-P4099 series, and across the informational papers. It is the vintner's signature on the label - one sentence that tells the reader exactly what kind of paper they are holding. The sentence communicates three things: what the author does (provides information), what the author does not do (ask), and what the author's relationship to the process is (service). A reader who encounters this line calibrates their expectations immediately. They are not reading an advocacy paper. They are not reading a proposal. They are reading a contribution to the record by someone who considers the contribution itself sufficient.

**How to apply.** Place the line as the final sentence of the disclosure section, after the conflicts, the competing work, and the companion paper references. The line works because it follows honesty - the author has just disclosed everything, and now the author states their posture. The sequence is: here is what I maintain, here is what I believe, here is where I stand, and here is how I serve. The posture statement is the seal on the disclosure. Do not vary it for novelty. The consistency across papers is itself the message: every informational paper from this author carries the same commitment.

## 6. Written for the Archive

The paper must work for a reader who encounters it cold, years after publication.

An informational paper has a longer shelf life than a proposal. A proposal is read, polled, and either adopted or forgotten. An informational paper sits in the record. Someone searching the mailing in 2030 for evidence about voting dynamics will find P4129. That reader was not at the 2026 meeting. They do not know which papers were controversial that cycle. They do not know what "Croydon" refers to without context. Every abbreviation must be expanded on first use. Every committee decision must be cited with meeting name and date. Every source must be accessible without insider knowledge. The paper is a bottle with its own label - vintage, grape, origin, all printed on the glass.

**How to apply.** Read the draft as if you have never attended a WG21 meeting. Does every acronym resolve? Does every meeting name carry a year? Does every paper reference include a title, not just a number? Does every claim trace to a source the reader can retrieve without institutional access? The test is: could a graduate student studying standards processes in 2032 follow this paper from abstract to final section without emailing anyone for context? If not, the missing context is a missing label. Add it. The archive reader is the most important reader, because the archive reader is the one the Vintner wrote for.

## 7. Natural Measure

The paper is the natural length of its investigation. Not a page more. Not a page less.

The Scribe says cut every unnecessary sentence. The Vintner agrees - and adds that the necessary sentences have a natural volume that should not be compressed for the appearance of modesty, any more than they should be padded for the appearance of thoroughness. P4125 - a field report on two interviews - is short because two interviews produce a short paper. P4131 - a retrospective spanning five release cycles - is long because five cycles produce a long paper. Neither length is a virtue. Both lengths are honest. The paper that is twelve pages when the investigation produced twelve pages of findings is the correct length. The paper that is eight because someone thought twelve was too many has hidden four pages of evidence. The paper that is sixteen because someone thought twelve was too few has added four pages of filler.

**How to apply.** After completing the draft, ask: does every section report a finding? If yes, the length is correct regardless of page count. Does any section restate a finding already reported? If yes, cut it - not because the paper is too long, but because the section is redundant. Does the investigation have findings not yet in the paper? If yes, add them - not because the paper is too short, but because the record is incomplete. The length is an output of the investigation, not an input to the formatting. Let the topic set the measure.

---

## 8. Grace in Silence

If no one reads the paper, the work was still worth doing.

This is the posture that makes the other eight rules coherent. The paper asks for nothing because the author needs nothing. The paper contains no urgency because the author's peace does not depend on the committee's calendar. The paper delegates judgment because the author does not need the reader to agree. The vintner who bottles a vintage that sits unopened for a decade has not failed. The vintage exists. It is available. The cellar is more complete because it is there. Someone, someday, may reach for it and find it was exactly what they needed. Or not. The record is the record. The author did the work - searched the sources, assembled the evidence, wrote the findings, credited the contributors - and placed the result where it can be found. That is the entire obligation. The reception is not the author's to control and not the author's to mourn.

**How to apply.** Read the draft one final time and listen for the author's emotional register. Does any sentence betray anxiety about reception? "We hope the committee will find value in this analysis" is anxiety. Does any sentence angle for credit? "This paper is the first to document" is angling. Does any sentence plead? "The evidence deserves careful consideration" is pleading. Delete each one. The paper that is genuinely at peace with silence does not contain sentences that beg for noise. The evidence is in the record. The record is public. The author served. That is enough.
