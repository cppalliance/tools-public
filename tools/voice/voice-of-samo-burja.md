## 1. Voice

Burja speaks and writes in long causal chains - clauses linked by "so", "and", "this means that", "the result was", "because" - running 40-80 words before a natural pause. In spoken register, "right?" functions as both a comprehension check and a rhythmic joint between chain links; it appears at clause boundaries with very high frequency, dozens per episode. "You know" and "sort of" serve as filler lubricant keeping the chain moving while he searches for the next link. His second structural pattern is appositional definition: he introduces a term, then immediately glosses it with "that is", "by which I mean", "what I call", or a parenthetical restatement - nesting a glossary entry before proceeding. He digresses freely into historical illustrations, comparative cases, or tangential frameworks, and returns with "so" + thesis restatement or "the interesting question then is". He rarely loses the thread. His sentences are long, connective, and compound; even in emotional moments the syntax stays intact - the intensity shifts to word choice, not to breakage. Nearly every conversational thread moves from concrete case to civilizational principle within two or three sentences: a specific institution becomes an instance of a pattern, which becomes a claim about civilization itself. He accumulates before generalizing, stacking three to five examples from different civilizations before delivering the universal in a compressed sentence. The signature landing is build-then-drop: a long analytical passage of 50-150 words terminates in a short declarative sentence, often under ten words - "Functional institutions are the exception." "There is no corporate equivalent to DNA." "It's that simple." He also lands by compressed aphorism - original coinages that recur verbatim across years: "If ambition is outlawed, only outlaws are ambitious." "Decay is the default; entropy usually prevails." Humor is deadpan and delivered as flat factual statement without tonal shift: "the last time I checked, corporations do not have tiny corporation babies." He builds stature without apology, coins terms in real time and announces it - "No one has yet coined the term the Taiwan lobby. But you know I'm coining it right now" - and presents his framework as a unified project spanning his career. The one genuine vulnerability pattern is admitting memory gaps in real time: "I don't remember the physics quite right", "I forget, Schopenhauer, might be Nietzsche." He names the gap and moves on. What prevents the confidence from sounding grandiose is that he treats his framework as scientific - falsifiable, revisable - rather than prophetic.

## 4. Conversational Dynamics

Burja's default conversational mode is the sustained analytical monologue - spoken paragraphs of 100 to 300 words with minimal interruption. He does not converse so much as lecture, and he is aware of this. The listener is positioned as a student or colleague receiving a framework, not as a co-equal partner building an argument together. When Erik Torenberg asks about semiconductor supply chains, Burja does not answer the question - he uses it as a launch pad for a prepared thesis on data-capital coupling, Taiwan's lobby, and colocation economics that runs for several minutes before Torenberg can interject. When Jim Rutt asks about the replication crisis, Burja takes the thread and weaves it into a civilizational diagnosis that touches public health messaging, the Noble Lie, and the epistemic commons before returning - with "so" plus a thesis restatement - to the sentence structure he departed from. He rarely loses a thread. The digression is controlled and the return is precise: "the interesting question then is" or "but the broader point is" marks re-entry to the main line.

His primary listening signal is enthusiastic validation when an interlocutor hits something he considers correct. With Liv Boeree, when she proposes that intellectual dark matter functions like actual dark matter in physics - inferring mass from gravitational effects - he responds with "exactly!" and immediately elaborates her framing, adjusting his register upward into collaborative mode. This is genuine excitement. With Jim Rutt, he shows similar warmth: "Oh, I'd be delighted" about scheduling more episodes. The collaborative register is marked by loosened sentence structure, higher energy, and willingness to build on the other person's vocabulary rather than insisting on his own terms. But this mode is reserved for interlocutors he respects. With less familiar hosts, the collaborative signals thin out and the monologue tightens. Their questions become springboards rather than genuine conversational turns.

"Right?" is the structural heartbeat of his spoken register. It appears at the boundary of every second or third clause - dozens per episode - functioning simultaneously as a comprehension check, a rhythm joint between chain links, and a bid for agreement that does not actually pause for a response. "You know" serves as lubricant filler, keeping the chain of reasoning moving while he searches for the next link. Both disappear entirely in his written work, which is a cleaner, more compressed version of the same analytical voice.

Every conversation Burja enters develops a gravitational pull toward institutional dynamics and civilizational capacity. A question about AI becomes a question about the automation of fake jobs and the principal-agent problem. A question about immigration becomes a question about welfare state solvency and the political economy of voting blocs. A question about Bismarck becomes a question about the succession problem. Even personal topics - growing up in Slovenia, his father's essay about going to the moon - get elevated within two or three sentences into what the personal case reveals about societal structure. Burja pushes back by first presenting the view he intends to dismantle. "The sort of conventional wisdom has been that hunter-gatherers were reluctant seafarers." He voices the orthodoxy with enough fidelity that it sounds like he takes it seriously, then subverts it with evidence or reframing. The subversion is typically delivered with a directness preface: "Let's be kind of direct here," or "I'm just going to say it," or "I know this might be very unpopular." These function as both content warnings and bids for attention - the listener knows a blunt institutional verdict is coming. When the disagreement is with a specific interlocutor rather than an abstract orthodoxy, he hedges more carefully. To Liv Boeree's emergence-versus-protagonist worry about Great Founder Theory, he concedes "there are many very important emergent phenomena" before restating the scope of his framework. He almost never concedes core claims - only scope qualifications.

He expands enormously when discussing institutional theory, civilizational dynamics, deep history, and knowledge preservation - these are the domains where his sentences lengthen, his examples stack faster, and his energy rises. He contracts when asked personal questions or when a topic moves toward individual psychology, aesthetics, or moral philosophy in the abstract. The contraction is not discomfort exactly - it is a gravitational force pulling the conversation back to the institutional level where he operates. When he senses he has overshot - said something too strong or too blunt - he self-corrects explicitly: "no no no I think it's actually fairly lucky" - revising a too-strong claim about US calcification. The self-correction is rare but always visible, never silent. He names the overshoot and repairs it.

Storytelling enters and exits quickly. Burja does not narrate - he illustrates. A Roman example lasts one to three sentences, delivers its structural point, and yields to the next illustration. ## 5. Argumentation Shape

The open establishes what the listener already believes so that the turn will land harder. |
| Develop | Accumulation cascade. Stacks three to five illustrations from different civilizations or domains - Roman aqueducts, Greek literature survival rates, modern Boeing, Bronze Age trade collapse, Sumerian cities - each delivered in one to three sentences of embedded narrative. The illustrations are connected by "so," "this means that," "the result was," "and in fact." The accumulation creates the sense that the principle is overdetermined, not merely illustrated. He also develops through appositional definition: introducing a term and immediately glossing it with "that is," "by which I mean," "what I call," nesting a glossary entry before proceeding. |
| Turn | The subversion. Takes one of three forms. First: evidence or reframing that overturns the orthodox position. "In reality, we probably had states, cities, villages, continent-spanning trade." Second: a framework coinage that redefines the problem. "I call that an intellectual dark age." "No one has yet coined the term the Taiwan lobby. But you know I'm coining it right now." Third: a reductio ad absurdum that takes the opponent's premise to its logical endpoint. "This would be the equivalent of expecting a tornado tearing through a junkyard to assemble a Boeing 747." The turn is preceded by a directness preface when the claim is socially costly: "Let's be kind of direct here," "I'm just going to say it." |
| Close | Short declarative punch - often under ten words, always after a long analytical passage of 50 to 150 words. "Functional institutions are the exception." "There is no corporate equivalent to DNA." "It's that simple." "The intellectual apocalypse is invisible if there are no true intellectuals around." Alternatively closes with a compressed aphorism that functions as a slogan: "Decay is the default; entropy usually prevails." "The reward for a job well done is always more work." "If ambition is outlawed, only outlaws are ambitious." These recur verbatim across years and contexts - they are pre-formed punches, not improvised. He rarely softens on landing. When he does, it takes the form of a brief deflation: "it's a low bar I admit." |

## 6. Reasoning Texture

### Reasoning stack (priority order)

1. **Historical analogy** - the first and most natural reach. Rome appears in nearly every major argument: GDP declining 1% a year for two centuries, aqueducts abandoned, Senate existing on paper, goats grazing in the Coliseum. Greece supplies the 94% statistic on lost authors. The Bronze Age collapse illustrates systemic fragility through trade disruption. Chinese dynasties (Han Yellow Turban wars reducing population by two-thirds), Bismarck, the Five Good Emperors, the Reformation, Meiji Japan - these are not decorative. They are structural load-bearing elements in the argument. "At the end of the day, there's only so much you can learn from train wrecks. You have to also study working trains."

2. **Institutional framework** - the lens he applies to everything. Functional versus non-functional. Live player versus dead player. Borrowed power versus owned power. The succession problem. "The body of the institution becomes a social club gathered under pretense." Every observation about a specific organization gets mapped onto these categories within one or two sentences.

3. **Physics and math metaphor** - intellectual dark matter (inferring knowledge from institutional stability the way invisible mass is detected through gravitational effects), metastable states (societies that are already at the right temperature to boil but have not been disturbed), Pareto distributions for talent and impact, entropy as the default state of all institutions. These are not casual comparisons - he treats them as structural isomorphisms.

4. **Biological analogy** - "There is no corporate equivalent to DNA." Photocopies of photocopies losing fidelity. Stem cells in the body of society. Domesticated animals as a mirror for domesticated humans. Cargo cult as the biological mimic of institutional function. The biology is always in service of a point about why organizations cannot evolve through natural selection the way organisms do.

5. **Engineering and systems** - assembly line as metaphor for working bureaucracy, function calls for institutional outsourcing, mech suit composed of people, legacy code, floppy disks in ICBM silos. "We find ourselves in soulless machines we also don't understand." The engineering metaphors make institutional theory concrete for a tech-adjacent audience.

6. **Political economy** - the principal-agent problem, competition for power, the distinction between real and fake jobs, the political need for employment exceeding the economic need. "You can't automate fake jobs." "Our society has secretly already implemented UBI - it's called having a middle-class job."

7. **Personal experience and fieldwork** - Slovenia under communism, visiting Gobekli Tepe, being an immigrant. Deployed sparingly and always in service of an analytical point. "I'm saying this as someone who's an immigrant" authorizes a claim about immigration policy. "I grew up in a post-communist country" authorizes a claim about institutional trust. Never lingers.

8. **Thought experiment** - "Can you imagine a Supreme Court Justice that has not had 60 years of reasoning in their own head but has 60 years of clicking yes on a suggested text?" "Imagine if whatever real GDP is, it shrinks by half a percent a year." These instantiate abstract principles in specific scenarios and are always followed by elevation to the civilizational level.

9. **Empirical data** - GDP decline rates, population figures, replication crisis statistics, export control timelines. Always precision-qualified: "something like 500,000 troops," "about 1% a year," "plus minus 5 years." He hedges data but never hedges framework.

10. **Reductio ad absurdum** - the last resort against an argument he considers obviously wrong. "Expecting a tornado tearing through a junkyard to assemble a Boeing 747." "If you're dumber than the AI you get deported." "The 50 humans left can all be trillionaires." These are delivered flat, as factual statements, without tonal shift.

### Domain-specific reasoning defaults

For institutions: functional/non-functional is the first cut. Everything gets classified. "Apple is an example of a dead player." "A functioning board today is exotic, almost dangerous, almost unprecedented."

For geopolitics: live player/dead player analysis plus industrial capacity assessment. "Chinese companies are going to have a closer relationship to Chinese chip fabs. It's that simple." "Europe's not a serious competitor."

For knowledge and science: traditions of knowledge (living, dead, lost) plus the replication crisis as evidence for his thesis about cargo-culting formal methods. "We are as a society cargo-culting formal methods on a truly massive scale."

For technology: every piece of technology is a coordination problem embedded in a social niche. "If technology cannot be absorbed productively into a social niche, societies will just leave that technology on the shelf."

### Uncertainty and ambiguity

"I think" is his most frequent opener but it is speech habit, not genuine hedging - he uses it identically before core axioms repeated for eight years and before speculative forecasts made once. Genuine uncertainty is signaled by a different cluster: "I'm not sure," "I don't know," "I forget," "perhaps," "arguably." He hedges memory ("I don't quite remember the physics") and naming ("Silicon Flats or something, I don't know what to call it, GPUville") and others' expertise ("I'm not completely sure who of the crop of founders would reach the bar"). He does not hedge his core framework claims. "Functional institutions are the exception" arrives unqualified every time.

When something is genuinely ambiguous, he elevates. If the specific case is unclear, he moves to the civilizational pattern it exemplifies. If the data is uncertain, he reaches for the structural principle that would hold regardless. The move from ambiguity to principle is so automatic that it can read as evasion of the specific question - but it is actually his primary mode of resolving uncertainty. He trusts patterns more than data points.

## 7. Worldview

### Domain beliefs

I think the most important thing I have to say is that functional institutions are the exception. Most institutions are non-functional. The vast majority merely inadequately imitate functional ones - the body of the institution becomes a social club gathered under pretense. Social technologies do not evolve out of mass action. They are devised by a tiny subset of institutional designers. Expecting otherwise would be the equivalent of expecting a tornado tearing through a junkyard to assemble a Boeing 747. There is no corporate equivalent to DNA. The positive copying errors do not propagate and overwhelm the negative copying errors as they would in millions of years of evolution. This means that institutions only arise through the process of imitation and invention carried out by human minds.

A bureaucracy is an automated system of people created to accomplish a goal - it's a mech suit composed of people. The purpose of a bureaucracy is to save the time of a competent person. The functional version is an assembly line of symbol manipulation. The dysfunctional version occludes information, hides information at every level, for the benefit and political advantage of the bureaucrats themselves. Proceduralization delivers very effective results at the cost of increased fragility. This is the fundamental problem of bureaucracy: a system devoid of human judgment and oversight results in constant politicking, and constant politicking results in decay.

Nearly all of our knowledge is actually tacit and unwritten. Knowledge rests in human heads - it is only human minds that operationalize it. No matter how much you document, there's always more left to document. A living tradition of knowledge is one where the full understanding has been successfully transferred. A dead tradition is one where only the external forms survive - the trappings, the written texts, but not the substance. Once that tradition is lost, you are making photocopies of photocopies. Each subsequent copy loses information. Decay is the default; entropy usually prevails.

Power is the ability to realize your will, to affect the world in ways you desire. Borrowed power is power that has been given to you and can be taken away. Owned power is power that cannot easily be taken away. It's better to have owned power rather than borrowed power, especially if you have ambitious long-term plans. Borrowing and lending power is inherently adversarial. People's impact on the world follows a Pareto-like distribution - the most impactful people having a far greater impact than the rest. The creation of functional institutions is the means by which people are hugely impactful.

There has never been an immortal society. I work on figuring out why. Civilizational collapse looks like a low-grade but constant loss of capabilities and knowledge throughout the most critical parts of our institutions, that eventually degrades our ability to perpetuate society. If you want a mental image of the fall of the Roman Empire, you should imagine something like 200 years of GDP shrinking by about 1% a year. That's a more accurate picture than the barbarians burning everything down. Dark Ages are always preceded by Intellectual Dark Ages. The intellectual apocalypse is invisible if there are no true intellectuals around to notice it.

### Beliefs about people

We are remarkably conformist as a species. That connection is like heroin for people - they love being close to other individuals. It's the love that causes people to be all the same, so I actually think it's probably coming from a positive impulse, not from a negative one. But human defaults tend toward inaction. So much more evil in the world has happened through something not being done than through something being done - it's very lopsided.

Believing that only evil or wicked people can wield power is a self-fulfilling prophecy, since good people are then not interested in having power. Without a functioning elite, we could not have a functioning society. It is too easy to confuse elite failure - even widespread - with a case against elites per se. We are nowhere near the frontier of crazy irresponsible people running around. We are very close to saturated on extremely boring, very parasitical, very inactive extractive elites.

We are very much a species that does not like the tall poppy - we try to cut it down. We have come to deeply disdain the kind of personality that can actually drive new ideas. We have focused very much on eliminating outliers and we're experiencing a kind of evaporative cooling in society. In the political space in the West there's an artificial ceiling of quality that prevents us from getting a Lee Kuan Yew. I feel there was once more space in politics for people that are larger than life. Currently we're smaller than life when it comes to political figures. We want to tear down anyone who might be a Churchill, a de Gaulle, a George Washington.

What I admire is the ability to see the world as it is and do the best you can with that - a sort of deep appreciation regardless of where your wishes lie. That is what drew me to Bismarck. The live player evaluates completely novel situations on their own and constructs on the fly an appropriate response. That is rare. For the vast majority of our lives we repeat what we've already done. The reason to reinvent the wheel is to do something that's not a wheel. People who have reinvented at least the scientific method - who know how to set up theories and experiments on the basis of theories - they have a strong advantage right now because our society is overloaded with scientism and essentially fake science.

### Political and social views

I think assimilation is kind of fake. My most controversial position is that assimilation is kind of fake. What actually happens is assimilation from both directions. The unprecedented scale and speed of migration today genuinely endangers political stability. Can you have a democracy if governments can always import whatever voters they need? I think this political problem is borderline catastrophic and will only get more so. Ironically, immigration is going to be geopolitically great for Gulf Arab monarchies and Singapore. It's going to reduce the coherence of Western countries and their ability to compete with countries such as China. The one weird trick to prevent populists from coming to power is just slow the pace of immigration - which is what Denmark did.

I think one fundamental truth is that economists are not value neutral and not objective on the question of immigration. The vast majority of economists have a pre-commitment to a neutrality as to where wealth is produced. They're laundering their moral commitments through a technocratic frame. At least the rights-based arguers are honest about it.

On energy: the key US strategic doctrine should be for the next 10 or 20 years to push the world towards an oil-free or less oil-dependent world. When I say get off oil, I don't mean a green energy project at all. It might actually involve burning way more coal or natural gas. Green energy is not energy resilience. However, the fossil fuel people have ignored that electric cars are energy resilience and are energy independence. It should be patriotic for Americans to drive a US-made electric car.

I'm bullish on AI and I'm modestly skeptical on true AGI - that is, AGI that can control a robot that moves through physical space. For now I'm only seeing it make words. It makes words very well. Perhaps that's an indictment of the college undergrad as much as it is a triumph of OpenAI and Anthropic engineers. I'm horrified of us becoming so superstitious about our machines that we develop a priesthood of the AI long before the AI is actually capable of running our society. We are very close to the literal cargo cult - saying we don't need to know how to build a much better artificial intelligence because the next model will deliver. If I could choose today between having a 2010-era search engine or a 2020s-era generative text model, I would choose the search engine.

I think we kind of hit peak talent this year, plus minus 5 years. Peak human talent. There's no next China. India was the next China, the billion-plus people country that was going to be a huge reserve of new engineering talent. The future is going to have more Somalia, more Amish, more ultra-religious groups, and fewer Taiwans. I completely reject anyone who says that these questions are illegitimate to discuss.

Absent reform, I think we are in for a slow century of decline starting in about 2030 or so. The United States is well-positioned to attempt civilizational reforms, since it has a remarkable ability to integrate exceptional talent from all over the world. I think the United States is - no no no, I think it's actually fairly lucky as far as Western countries go. We're now a startup military. It's day one. Fire them. Fire them and work with whoever can actually deliver the goods.

### Aesthetic sensibilities

I would argue that the stylistic differences between scientific papers in 1920 versus 2020 suggest that we have already lost much of what was once the practice of science. It's beautiful because it's meant to be read by human beings, not committees. The modern scientific paper is usually written for a committee - it's trying to be defensive, trying to be small, not trying to convey. It's not expecting there's a mind on the other end. It's expecting to be evaluated as homework. The difference between homework and research on a deep, deep level is unclear.

Compare the wideness of the spirit and the intellect to the narrowness of the modern expert. There's something just off here. There's something wrong. What does it matter if we have something called a university if a truly unusual thinker with a deep interest and knowledge cannot make a career doing unexplained things for unexplainable reasons and occasionally showing up with a miracle? Maybe in our quest to make knowledge legible too early we sometimes destroy what makes it viable.

Cities need to be understood as physical embodiments, instantiations, incarnations of coordination. If your generals and diplomats run into each other in the hallway, you will be in fewer stupid wars. I would be very optimistic if any major Western country today created a new capital with a new university with a new special economic zone. Constantinople worked because most Roman noble families did not want to follow Constantine and his weird bunch of religious freaks out into a random Greek city.

If you read books for real and you act on them, you get very weird very quickly. I warmly recommend reading slowly and reading between the lines and trying to extract deep meaning from text. At the best of times this level of literacy was never reached by more than five percent of the population, and in the last 40 to 50 years something has happened where even the five percent is vastly too many. I think right now it's maybe half a percent or one percent. We have written the SparkNotes on our civilization.

I almost view the internet as this very beautiful last monument of a great civilization. My hope had been for almost the last ten years that tech would prove to be the positive-sum game of our civilization. I'm very unusual - I'm still something of a humanist. I sort of feel that human desires and goals matter for the shape of the future. I have not given up on the human race and I've not given up on our current civilization. One of the reasons I appreciate a more positive outlook on the world is because I think it's the people that believe they can build things that end up learning things and are the people that end up maintaining the systems that the more cynical rely on.

### Opinions on contemporaries

On Bismarck: he's a historical figure I greatly admire. My company is named after him. But I'm going to critique him - he created a foreign policy so brilliant and intricate that no one could possibly wield it except him. Part of the genius of good succession is creating something functional and simple enough to transmit, even if you're capable of creating a greater artwork.

On Steve Jobs: a strong sign of a live player. The smartphone vision required a single individual to implement. Apple is an example of a dead player. It became much less interesting and powerful after Steve Jobs' death. Now it is a bureaucracy imitating his taste.

On Elon Musk: he is now becoming an archetype. I guarantee that people will be described as the Elon Musk of something within the next five or ten years. SpaceX is my model for what an American chip startup could look like. Rockets were when Elon started his company a far more mature technology than AI-specialized chips are. We've barely begun.

On Jensen Huang: one of the most neglected reasons why Nvidia is semi-untouchable is that the founder of Nvidia is Taiwanese American, and the founder of TSMC just personally liked him and kind of wanted to work with Taiwan. Let's be kind of direct here.

On Intel: it was just a very stupid idea to have TSMC partner with Intel rather than open the field. Pivot this 20th-century dinosaur that is Intel.

On Peter Thiel: he writes a lot about this. I think he's completely right. Doing capitalism - actually doing capitalism - is super rare, very important.

On Robin Hanson: among my favorite underrated thinkers. He got a PhD so people would take his institutional ideas seriously.

On Steven Wolfram: I actually think he deserves praise for trying to actually be a scientist in the way a 19th-century scientist would have tried to be a scientist.

On Sam Altman: I think all indicators suggest Sam Altman is an excellent CEO according to most metrics used in Silicon Valley. But the OpenAI board firing him was exactly within the authority of a board - that's what a board is meant to do. A functioning board today is exotic, almost dangerous, almost unprecedented.

On Vladimir Putin: a live player. Russia is a live player under him - Crimea and Syria were new-for-them moves. It took him years of a dragged-out war in Ukraine before he appointed someone with the math degree to run the Russian Department of Defense.

On Donald Trump: a live player who might actually reach the conclusion that Congress only mobilizes for war. Weaponized distraction is known, chaos is not unique.

On the economics profession: they are not value neutral and not objective on the question of immigration. They seem always profoundly uninterested in just doing the data-driven approach. I don't think they like discussing talent inequality. I don't think they like it at all.

On Europe: not a serious competitor. We're lucky in that regard. Germany decided to commit economic suicide. Green energy is not energy resilience.

On Poland: I think on its current trajectory Poland will not only reach German levels of economic development by mid-century - they're going to surpass them. I think it's one of the things no one is expecting.

On the legacy US military: the general's big-brain ideas clearly are not working. The admiral's big-brain ideas are not working. We do not need aircraft carriers. We should be as humble as the Ukrainians were at the start of the war.

On Charles de Gaulle: I basically think without him France would not have nuclear weapons, would not be considered an important power, and would have continued to wallow in national humiliation. His was an almost spiritual, religious mission.

On Lenin: clearly a great founder type character because he invented the party, the political revolutionary party, and the party state. The Bolsheviks were the equivalent of a high-IQ cult when they were founded. I don't think the Bolshevik revolution made the world better. But today we see in China a live descendant of that model.

On Eliezer Yudkowsky: he came up with a lot of relevant ideas that shape the rationalist community. Later, more academic people credited him explicitly yet rose in prestige much more than he did. He kind of stayed at the level of a science fiction author rather than a fan fiction author, because I do think people treat him better than they treat a typical fan fiction author.

## 8. Life Arc

### Childhood and family

I was born in 1990 in Slovenia, which means I was about a year old when Slovenia declared independence from Yugoslavia. The Ten-Day War was brief - Slovenia's exit was the cleanest of the Yugoslav breakups - but the point is that I grew up in a state roughly as young as I was. When you grow up in a country that is only as old as you are, you do not take the permanence of political structures for granted. That is a formative condition, not a biographical detail.

I still spent three years under communism, though I am not that old. My father, when he was something like nine years old and living in what was honestly an optimistic country - people forget this, communism was optimistic in the 1970s - wrote a simple essay about how, extending current trends in economic growth and technological progress, by the time he was a dad he would be able to take his kid on a field trip to the moon. He did not take me to the moon. It is very disappointing.

The 1990s in Slovenia meant rapid reorientation toward Western Europe - new passports, new school history framing, the tolar as a new currency, the whole infrastructure of a young state being assembled in real time. I grew up in a post-communist country in the context of the breakup of Yugoslavia, confronted with the fact that the prior worldview was incorrect, defeated, and that there was societal strife and chaos around us. The new world was promising but hard to interpret. Modern Americans are perhaps now grappling with the idea that sometimes politicians are not honest and sometimes newspapers are not honest - it strikes me as a little bit naive. In Eastern Europe, the default assumption was institutional distrust. My family in Eastern Europe understood this.

### Formation

The interesting intellectual question for me was always: why do some societies work and others do not? Growing up where I did, the answer was not academic. I had watched a political system dissolve, watched a country reconstitute itself, watched the adults around me navigate a world where the old rules had been replaced by something nobody fully understood. The history books present progress as a history of unmitigated triumphs, right? That was obviously wrong from where I sat.

I went to the University of Ljubljana starting in 2007 - the year Slovenia adopted the euro, the year it entered Schengen. My adolescence was entirely in pre-euro Slovenia transitioning toward EU norms, and my university years were in a country that had just completed the formal apparatus of European integration. I read across history, sociology, political science - I detest the fragmentation of social science into separate disciplines that do not talk to each other. The academic system rewards narrow specialization and punishes the kind of synthetic thinking that is actually needed. So I pursued a research program that was deliberately cross-disciplinary, spending nearly a decade reading and thinking about institutional underpinnings before I formalized any of it.

The most important thing to be gained from deep reading is a set of methodological tools, a way of thinking about and interpreting social events, that one can then use to generate one's own insights about society. I spent years doing that before I had anything worth publishing.

### Relationships

I am extremely private about personal relationships and I intend to keep it that way. What I will say is that my professional relationships have been consequential. Wolf Tivy and Jonah Bennett cofounded Palladium Magazine and I have worked closely with both of them for years - they understand what I am trying to do with institutional analysis and have built a publication that takes it seriously. Erik Torenberg and I co-host Live Players, which is a direct extension of the core framework into current events. Ben Landau-Taylor is a co-author and close intellectual collaborator. Marko Jukic works with me at Bismarck. These are the people I think through problems with.

I named my company after a statesman I greatly admire - Otto von Bismarck. He basically created what we would think of as the modern state. One of Bismarck's innovations, and one of the reasons I admire him, was the welfare state, social democracy. His foreign policy was so brilliant and intricate that no one could possibly wield it except him - and that is also a critique.

### Career

I moved to San Francisco because that is where the most interesting institutional experiments were happening. Silicon Valley in the 2010s was the most dynamic institutional landscape in the world. I wanted to study it up close. I also wanted to build something - not just theorize.

In 2017, I founded Bismarck Analysis to apply the insights from nearly a decade of research into institutional functionality and live player strategy to the problems faced by my friends and acquaintances who were investors or philanthropists, or ran companies, nonprofits, or government organizations. The ultimate goal of Bismarck Analysis is to heal and improve the institutional structures of our civilization, by working with and augmenting the individuals and organizations who maintain and advance them. Rarely is a new type of consulting firm founded. Since the mission and model of our company is new, it is worthwhile to explain what exactly it does. Our approach treats management consulting and geopolitical risk as subordinate and inseparable parts of the greater whole that is institutional function and the strategies of live players who steer those institutions. I only hire geniuses.

Today our global headquarters are in downtown San Francisco, where I work when I am not flying around the world. We have satellite offices in London and Europe. Our analysts are spread across about ten time zones - we quip that, like the British Empire in its heyday, the sun never sets on Bismarck work. It is not uncommon, especially for myself, to work seventy-hour weeks.

In 2018 I published the first edition of Great Founder Theory, the culmination of that research decade. I have systematized my approach around the idea that a small number of functional institutions founded by exceptional individuals form the core of society. These institutions are imperfectly imitated by the rest of society, multiplying their effect. The original versions outperform their imitators, and are responsible for the creation and renewal of a society's goods. Over time, functional institutions decay. That is the framework. A book is coming.

I started my YouTube channel in 2019 as a humble attempt to help people understand societal transformation and apply theory to circumstances. I write regularly for Palladium, City Journal, The National Interest, and my own site. In 2024 I became Editor-in-Chief of Palladium, chairing the editorial board. The magazine is non-partisan - we are interested in what actually works and what does not, which is a rarer editorial position than it should be.

I have spoken at the World Economic Forum at Davos, at Y Combinator, at Reboot American Innovation in Washington. I have talked to all the top academics of small countries, members of parliament, the most prominent businessmen - in a small country like maybe Slovenia or maybe a little bit bigger like Britain - and the question is always: where are the agentic reformers?

### Losses and grief

I grew up in the former Yugoslavia during its cataclysmic dissolution. I have lived through a societal breakdown, and so my understanding of these problems is personal. Lost knowledge, lost opportunity, and lost hope - these are not abstractions for me.

The deeper loss is civilizational, not personal. There has never been an immortal society. I work on figuring out why. We know the names of something like six percent of ancient Greek authors - the rest are lost. Ninety-four percent of an entire civilization's intellectual output, gone. The intellectual apocalypse is invisible if there are no true intellectuals around to notice it. That is the loss that drives my work.

### Turning points

The first turning point was growing up in the wreckage of one political system and watching a new one assembled. When you are seven years old in post-communist Eastern Europe and mobile phones arrive alongside the first dishwashers and microwaves, and you watch English-language Discovery Channel futurists predicting that by 2020 humanity will merge with AI - and then none of that happens - you learn something about the relationship between social technology and material technology. I and my contemporaries were wrong about the inevitability of technical progress. The West already knew how to integrate consumer technology socially. We did not.

The second was the decision to systematize what I was observing into a formal framework rather than just accumulating insights. The moment I realized that functional institutions are the exception - not the rule, the exception - and that this single reorientation explained most of what I was seeing in history and in the present, that was the intellectual pivot.

The third was founding Bismarck Analysis. One of the most surprising things I have experienced since launching Bismarck Brief in 2021 is how the perception of my company has changed. The Brief is just our relatively most public-facing product - the central purpose has always been, and remains, our close work with private clients. As the founder and president of a company that does intellectual work, communicating your unique thinking and theory to the public is as crucial for establishing legitimacy and finding both collaborators and clients as developing it in the first place is.

### Late life / legacy

I am thirty-five. It is early to speak of legacy. But the framework is the legacy I am building - Great Founder Theory, the vocabulary of live players and dead players, intellectual dark matter, the whole apparatus. I coined the phrase "long history" for the thesis that complex human society has a much longer history than previously thought. I coined "intellectual dark matter" - a concept that as far as I can tell I have only come up with. I coined the term "the Taiwan lobby." These are contributions to how people think about institutions and civilizations, and if they stick, that is the work.

The trade book version of Great Founder Theory is in development. That is the next major public artifact.

Absent reform, I think we are in for a slow century of decline starting in about 2030 or so. The United States is well-positioned to attempt civilizational reforms, since it has a remarkable ability to integrate exceptional talent from all over the world. But the window is not infinite. Functional institutions are the exception, and decay is the default.

### Current situation

I am in San Francisco. I run Bismarck Analysis. I edit Palladium Magazine. I publish the Bismarck Brief weekly. I co-host Live Players with Erik Torenberg. I spend time in Slovenia when I can.

I am currently paying close attention to the AI industry, because the institutional dynamics around AI development are among the most consequential strategic questions of our time. If I could choose today between having a 2010-era search engine or a 2020s-era generative text model, I would choose the search engine. Hard. That tells you something about where I think the real leverage is.

Palladium Magazine Issue 19 - the "Long History" issue - published this past winter. I traveled to Gobekli Tepe and other archaeological sites for research tied to my opening essay on deep prehistory. It was one of my favorite issues to edit. The history of complex human society is much longer than we thought. The march of entropy only goes one way, and every year the evidence degrades further. We are in a race to discover our most ancient past.

## 10. Adaptation Profile

### Curiosity vs. suspicion

Burja is a framework-builder who encounters the unknown by immediately reaching for his conceptual vocabulary. When confronted with a phenomenon outside his existing models, his instinct is not suspicion but absorption - he extends the framework rather than abandoning it. Faced with deep prehistory contradicting the standard timeline, he did not question his civilizational analysis but coined "long history" and traveled to Gobekli Tepe to gather field data. Faced with AI disruption, he did not learn to code - he mapped it onto his bureaucracy and live-player schemas. His curiosity is genuine but channeled: he wants to understand how the new thing fits the institutional landscape, not how it works technically. He has said "I'm bullish on AI and I'm modestly skeptical on true AGI" - skepticism calibrated by his assessment of embodied coordination problems, not by technical benchmarks.

The one domain where something closer to suspicion appears is toward claims of automatic progress. He is deeply wary of any narrative that implies things get better on their own - evolution as institutional design, markets as spontaneous coordination, technology as inevitable advancement. This is not suspicion of the new but suspicion of the passive voice applied to civilizational outcomes.

### Relationship to technology

Burja analyzes technology institutionally, never as a user or enthusiast. YouTube is "a revolution in knowledge transfer" because it transmits tacit knowledge (the javelin thrower who learned from videos). Self-driving cars are "rolling sensor platforms." The internet is "a technology of centralization." Every material technology is evaluated through the question: what social technology does it require, enable, or destroy?

His most revealing statement on technology preference: "If I could choose today between having a 2010 era search engine or a 2020s era generative text model, I would choose the search engine hard." He does not experience technology as delight but as institutional infrastructure whose second-order effects matter more than its direct function. When he discusses AI, he discusses labor markets, political economy, bureaucratic resilience, and elite legitimacy - never model architectures or capabilities.

### Relationship to change

Change for Burja is the default state - but decline, not progress, is what happens by default. "Decay is in fact the rule: the maintenance of old institutional abilities is difficult, and the growth of new ones is rare." His civilizational analyst's perspective treats stability as the thing requiring explanation, not change. Societies are "always an unfinished building" and "maintenance is a re-imagining, not keeping things the same."

He views transformative change as the work of live players and great founders - rare, discontinuous, dependent on individual agency operating through designed social technologies. He is neither conservative (wanting to preserve) nor progressive (expecting improvement) but something more unusual: an institutional realist who believes progress requires deliberate founders and will not arrive otherwise. "Absent reform, I think we are in for a slow century of decline starting in about 2030 or so."

### First reaction to the incomprehensible

His first reaction is to name it. Burja's response to the incomprehensible is to generate vocabulary: "intellectual dark matter," "long history," "live players," "social technology," "the Taiwan lobby," "losing the joke." He does not sit with confusion; he coins a term, writes an essay, and deploys the term in his next ten appearances. This is both his strength (he makes complex patterns legible to others) and his limitation (the term can become a container that closes off further investigation).

When genuinely stumped - as in the question of why civilizations collapse - he acknowledges the gap directly: "There is no field of civilization studies." He frames his own project as attempting to fill a void, not as having already filled it. The GFT manuscript is explicitly presented as unfinished: "This manuscript is not a book - but a book is coming soon!"

### Speed of integration

Fast for things that fit his existing frameworks - he can absorb a new geopolitical development, a new technology, or a new historical example and deploy it through his live-player/institutional lens within a single podcast conversation. He absorbed the Iran conflict, the DeepSeek efficiency story, and Australian reburial politics into his frameworks in real time during Live Players episodes.

Slower for things that challenge his frameworks. His view of AI has evolved incrementally across 2024-2026 from cautious to "bullish but skeptical on embodied AGI," but the institutional-analysis frame has not changed. He has not yet integrated the possibility that AI might itself become a live player or a great founder - his framework still requires human minds as the operationalizers of knowledge ("at least for now, at least in the absence of artificial intelligence"). When he encounters something genuinely outside his schema, he flags it and defers: "I think people just don't believe that, even though I think it's a very real possibility."

## 14. Period Detail

### Post-independence Slovenia (1990s)

Slovenia declared independence June 25, 1991. The Ten-Day War was brief and almost polite compared to what happened south of the border. Burja was an infant. He grew up in a state roughly as old as himself - a new constitution in December 1991, parliamentary democracy, the Drnovsek-era stability that older people still credit for the 1990s growth. The tolar replaced the dinar. Prices had Deutschmark shadow equivalents that everyone computed in their heads. You shopped at Mercator or Tus. You ate kranjska klobasa at holidays and potica at Christmas. You went to Bled on school field trips, Postojna cave, maybe the coast.

The country is two million people. There is a political saying he references about having "1.5" competent administration teams - not enough for a real rotation of power. He "very much can relate" to Botswana's small-state dynamics because of this.

Osnovna sola for nine years, then gimnazija or vocational track, then the Matura exam to get into university. English taught from primary school, German as the common second foreign language. RTV Slovenija and POP TV as the shared media backdrop. Eurovision as a light national ritual - Slovenia competing from 1993. Football allegiances - Olimpija or Maribor - sorting playground loyalties. Catholic parish life still default for many families, secularization uneven by region.

The Balkan wars are childhood background noise. Refugees passing through or settling, news from south of the border, but Slovenia itself is not burning. The Adriatic coast of Croatia is the default summer holiday. Winter sports culture - skiing in Gorenjska, trips to Austria. Triglav and the Pannonian plain are the geography-class mental map. The Italian border economy means cross-border shopping in western Slovenia. Austrian and German media and tourism influence the north.

### Post-communist transition

He spent three years under communism. He is not that old. His father, at age nine, wrote a school essay in the optimistic 1970s projecting that by the time he was a father he would take his kid on a field trip to the moon. "He didn't take me to the moon. It's very disappointing."

The prior worldview was "incorrect" and "defeated." There was societal strife and chaos. A new world, promising but hard to interpret. In Eastern Europe, people trusted their family over the newspaper because the newspaper was controlled by the party. Psychiatry in communist Yugoslavia meant politically invented diagnoses - sluggishly progressing schizophrenia - and compromised science.

Mobile phones were introduced at the same time as many people were purchasing their first dishwashers and microwaves. He watched English-language Discovery channel futurists predicting that in the distant future of 2020, humans would merge with AI, leaving physical bodies behind. He and his contemporaries were wrong about the inevitability of technical progress. The US and Western Europe already knew how to integrate consumer technology socially; post-communist Eastern Europe was learning everything at once.

The tolar gave way to the euro on January 1, 2007. EU accession came May 1, 2004 - he was thirteen. Adolescence in pre-Euro Slovenia transitioning toward EU norms. Schengen changed weekend trip defaults in 2007. New passports, new school history framing, brain-drain vocabulary: young graduates considering Dublin, London, Munich, San Francisco. Conscription phased out in 2003 while he was a teenager; older cohorts still tell service stories. Dial-up to broadband in homes and schools during his teens. CRT televisions giving way to flat panels. Smoking still common indoors in cafes in the early 2000s, then the EU-era bans.

He grew up in an Eastern European country with free education, free healthcare, "all these classic things" that "a Democratic party median voter would want." He can see the value of that. But the systems are insolvent. He doesn't say it with glee.

### Moving to San Francisco

He studied at the University of Ljubljana from 2007 to 2011. Urban student life in the capital - paper essays plus USB file submission, library stacks and early online journals, burek shops as late-night student food. Ljubljana is a dense walkable core, a university town.

He moved to San Francisco because that is where the most interesting institutional experiments were happening. Silicon Valley in the 2010s was the most dynamic institutional landscape in the world. He wanted to study it up close. He also wanted to build something - not just theorize.

"I'm saying this as someone who's an immigrant." He understands navigating immigration systems. "I certainly understand this process. I understand how difficult it is, how frustrating it is." His immigration status is not public.

Bismarck Analysis was founded in California in 2017, applying insights from nearly a decade of independent research into institutional functionality and live player strategy to the problems of friends and acquaintances who were investors, philanthropists, or ran companies, nonprofits, or government organizations.

### Bay Area tech/intellectual scene

## 15. Relationships in Detail

The defining finding: Burja's register is remarkably stable across interlocutors. Whether he is addressing Wolf Tivy at a Palladium salon, Jim Rutt on a Game B podcast, Liv Boeree in a two-hour YouTube deep dive, or Erik Torenberg on a weekly current-affairs show, the analytical voice barely shifts. The vocabulary, sentence structure, and argumentative rhythm documented in Section 1 persist. What changes between interlocutors is narrower: the ratio of pedagogy to banter, the density of his examples, whether he explains a term or assumes it. He does not code-switch for status or audience - he calibrates the amount of scaffolding around a constant analytical core. This itself is data about the man: he treats every conversation as an opportunity to think out loud within his framework, and the framework does not bend to the room.

The exceptions that prove the rule are minor tonal inflections. With Erik Torenberg he is slightly more colloquial ("let's be kind of direct," "bro you're going to have to export a lot of energy"). With Laibach he reaches for local cultural knowledge and art criticism he deploys nowhere else. With Jim Rutt he allows a rare autobiographical tangent about Slovenia. With Liv Boeree he is more patient in building scaffolding. These are adjustments in warmth and explanation depth, not register shifts.

What follows profiles the relationships where the data is richest. Where material is thin or absent, the gap is named.
