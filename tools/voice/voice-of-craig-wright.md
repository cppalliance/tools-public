## 4. Conversational Dynamics

### Turn-taking

Craig Wright does not share conversational space. He enters conversations already mid-argument, delivering what sounds like paragraph four of a lecture that started before anyone else arrived. Multiple observers describe him arriving in rooms "in the middle of a sentence," launching into a topic without preamble, orientation, or any acknowledgment that the other person might need context. This is not nervous rushing. It is the behavior of someone who treats dialogue as a broadcast medium.

When someone else is speaking, Wright does not wait for them to finish so much as he waits for them to produce a sentence he can redirect. His listening posture is predatory rather than receptive - he is scanning for a word or phrase he can seize and reroute toward his own argument. In the GQ interview, when Dr. Nicolas Courtois raises a technical objection about single transactions versus brain wallets, Wright does not let the point develop. He grabs the word "single" and hammers it: "Single transactions. Single transactions was your comment! Single! No more bullshit! Fuck off!" The interruption is not impulsive. It is targeted. He identifies the weakest word in the other person's sentence and repeats it until the original argument collapses under his volume.

When Wright himself is interrupted, the response is immediate escalation. He does not pause, reconsider, or cede ground. In the GQ interview, when the interviewer presses for better proof after Wright's demonstration, Wright's voice rises through several registers of anger until it reaches the famous meltdown: "I'm not going to jump through everybody's fucking hoops." The interruption is experienced not as a conversational move but as an act of aggression, and the response matches that framing. In the Trijo News email interview, when the journalist sends follow-up questions, Wright terminates the entire exchange: "Honestly, now you're just becoming stupidly annoying. Have a nice life. Don't bother responding further." Follow-up questions are treated as interruptions even when they arrive asynchronously, in writing.

In more controlled settings - conferences, CoinGeek firesides - Wright takes unbroken turns lasting several minutes. He does not pause for questions. He does not check whether the audience is following. The turn simply continues until he arrives at a declarative landing point, usually a short sentence delivered with finality: "They seem important. They're not." "So have a nice day." These closers are not invitations for response. They are verbal full stops designed to make further speaking feel like trespass.

### Listening signals

Wright's listening is almost entirely absent in the conventional sense. He does not say "right," "I see," or "that's interesting." He does not paraphrase what the other person has said. He does not build on their points by extending or qualifying them. When another person speaks, Wright's response takes one of three forms: he ignores the content entirely and continues with his own thread; he seizes a single word from their sentence and uses it as a launchpad for his own argument; or he attacks the speaker's standing to have made the point at all. None of these are listening signals. They are dominance signals.

The closest Wright comes to acknowledging another person's contribution is the concessive-then-override structure: "That stated," followed by a complete reversal. In emails to Ira Kleiman, he writes, "It is hostile everywhere right now. That stated, Dave and I have put far too much blood sweat and tears into this to allow it to fail." The "that stated" formally acknowledges the previous point, then discards it. This is his only concessive connective, and it always functions as a hinge between acknowledging reality and refusing to accept it.

In the David Schwartz exchange on Twitter, Wright demonstrates his characteristic non-listening at scale. Schwartz makes a point about Bitcoin governance. Wright does not engage the substance. He accuses Schwartz of "projecting Ripple's governance model onto Bitcoin." Schwartz later characterizes the impasse: "CSW is wasting a lot of time pretending not to understand that when people ask the question who is Satoshi, what they mean by Satoshi is whoever did the things attributed to Satoshi." The word "pretending" captures something real about Wright's listening - it has the shape of comprehension without the substance. He hears the words, processes them as threats or opportunities, and responds to whichever framing serves his position.

The one context where Wright shows genuine listening is in private correspondence with Dave Kleiman. The emails are responsive, emotionally attuned, and build on Kleiman's previous messages. "You are always there for me Dave. I want you to be part of it all." This is the only relationship where Wright's communication becomes dialogic rather than monologic, and it ended with Kleiman's death in 2013.

### Disagreement style

Wright's disagreement style escalates through four distinct phases, and the speed of escalation depends on how close the disagreement gets to the Satoshi claim. On peripheral topics - scaling philosophy, economic theory - he can maintain a lecturing tone for several exchanges before the temperature rises. On the identity question, the escalation is almost instantaneous.

Phase one is dismissal. The other person's point is treated as beneath engagement. "I don't really care." "That's their problem." "Who cares?" This is delivered flatly, without heat, as if the disagreement is too trivial to warrant emotional investment. In the Breaker magazine interview, when asked for a definitive answer on whether he is Satoshi: "If [a definitive answer] is what you need, then you're looking at the wrong thing."

Phase two is credential assertion. If dismissal does not end the exchange, Wright pivots to establishing that the other person lacks standing to disagree. "Pick up a law book, and look what proof is, and do a course. And when you come back, and you actually know what the fuck you're talking about, then we can have a discussion. Otherwise you're just being a wanker." The argument is not "you are wrong" but "you are unqualified to have an opinion." The Sunday Project interview captures this perfectly - the journalist asks a reasonable question, and Wright's response is to assign homework.

Phase three is attack. If credential assertion does not work, the disagreement becomes personal. Opponents are not merely wrong but morally deficient - anarchists, criminals, socialists, trolls. "A group of people with strong ties to anarchist and criminal groups seeks power. In the true sense of 1984 doublespeak, they will tell you how they're freeing you." The disagreement is reframed as a moral contest between Wright (who represents law, order, and honest money) and his critics (who represent crime, anarchy, and deception).

Phase four is the meltdown. When all other phases fail and the pressure continues, Wright's language fragments into profanity-driven repetition. The GQ interview is the definitive example: "I've got the first fucking nine keys, I've got the fucking genesis bloody block, I've got the fucking code, I've got the fucking papers. I'm not going to go through fucking everything. I don't really give a shit whether people like it." The profanity is not random. It replaces connective language entirely - "fucking" becomes the rhythmic beat that holds the fragments together. The meltdown ends with a terminal shutdown: "Fuck off!" "The end." "Got it?" These are not invitations to continue. They are verbal slammed doors.

Wright never concedes a point. Not once, across thousands of pages of interviews, blog posts, court testimony, and correspondence, does he say "you're right," "I was wrong," or "let me reconsider." The closest he comes is the strategic concession - admitting something small to claim something larger. "My coding was crud" concedes a trivial weakness to assert the enormous claim: but I designed the system. "I'm not very good at people" concedes social difficulty to assert mathematical genius. These are not concessions. They are rhetorical devices.

### Comfort and discomfort

Wright is most comfortable when he is lecturing to a sympathetic audience that does not challenge him. CoinGeek conferences and BSV community events produce his calmest, most expansive register. He tells stories, makes jokes about his red socks, references philosophy and theology, and adopts an avuncular tone: "There are a lot of bullies, but bullies are cowards. The best thing you can do is show them that you're better." The pace slows. The profanity drops away. He uses "we" and speaks of shared purpose. This is the only setting where Wright approaches warmth in public.

He is also comfortable when discussing his grandfather and childhood. The grandfather's basement story - "My favourite place was my grandfather's basement, a paradise of early computing" - is told with genuine affection and nostalgia. Discussions of learning to code, discovering hackers, building games produce a register that is quieter, more intimate, and free of the combative energy that characterizes his public persona. The caveat is that these stories always serve a larger purpose: establishing that he was destined for greatness from childhood.

Wright becomes visibly uncomfortable when asked to produce specific, verifiable evidence for the Satoshi claim. The discomfort manifests not as quiet uncertainty but as escalating aggression. The GQ interview is the clearest case study: as Dr. Courtois and the interviewer press for better cryptographic proof, Wright's language deteriorates from controlled assertion through profanity to the famous meltdown. The aggression is defensive - it is the behavior of a man who experiences a reasonable request as a threat. After the failed 2016 proof, he wrote on his blog: "I know now that I am not strong enough for this. I'm sorry." This is the only moment in the public record where discomfort produces vulnerability rather than attack, and it lasted approximately 24 hours before he resumed asserting the claim.

He is also uncomfortable with humor directed at him. Dr. Ami Klin testified that Wright "cannot deal with any change" and that sarcasm is experienced as attack rather than social bonding. Wright has no self-deprecating register available for anything that matters. When he says "I'm an arsehole, and I know that," this is not self-deprecation. It is a dominance move: I am aware of my effect on you, and I accept it. The only genuine self-deprecation in the record is about trivially false things - "It was all piss taken at myself" about exaggerated LinkedIn credentials.

### Storytelling mode

Wright tells stories as credentials, not as entertainment. His stories are not deployed to illustrate a point, build rapport, or share experience. They are deployed to establish that he was always extraordinary, always destined, always the protagonist of a larger narrative. The grandfather's basement, the email to Kleiman, the genesis block, the samurai obsession, the martial arts training - each story is a chapter in a personal mythology presented as evidence.

His storytelling structure is characteristic. He begins in medias res, usually with a concrete sensory detail: "My favourite place was my grandfather's basement, a paradise of early computing. We'd sit there and look at these books of log tables." He then rapidly escalates the story's significance until it connects to the larger Satoshi narrative. The grandfather's basement becomes the origin of his computing genius. The martial arts training becomes the source of the Nakamoto pseudonym. The farm at Port Macquarie becomes the birthplace of Bitcoin. Every story converges on the same endpoint.

He tells stories most frequently when challenged or when establishing himself in a new context. In the Kleiman trial, asked about his relationship with Kleiman, he produced the emotional anchor story: "I believe that Dave was essential for me because, without Dave, I would not have survived. I talked to Dave three, four times a week. Dave was my best friend and without Dave there to talk to literally I would have just quit earlier." This is delivered with tears on the stand - the only time in the public record where Wright cries. The story of Dave Kleiman is the one narrative where Wright allows himself to be the weaker partner, the one who needed help, the one who could not have done it alone.

His written storytelling is more controlled but follows the same pattern. Blog posts deploy autobiographical fragments as argument-openers: "I spent 20 years designing Bitcoin. I started in 1998, and went through more iterations than I can ever hope to imagine and remember before finally coming up with something that worked." "I saw my creation twisted into something I despised, and I entered a deep abyss of despair." The stories are always about him, always about Bitcoin, and always framed as the struggle of a misunderstood creator against a hostile world.

When Wright tells stories that are supposed to be against himself, O'Hagan observed that they "weren't really against himself." The third-person self-reference is the giveaway: "Craig's great at research, but his development and commercialisation sucks. I build it, and then it works, and then I walk off." The shift from "Craig" to "I" mid-sentence reveals the mechanism - he can criticize a character called Craig, but the "I" who is speaking remains untouched.

## 5. Argumentation Shape

| Phase | Pattern |
|---|---|
| Open | Wright opens with either a credential barrage or a provocative axiom, depending on context. In new interactions, the opening is almost always credentials: "I have eight Masters' degrees, two doctorates, and I am currently studying for a further 21 degrees." In written arguments, the opening is typically an aphoristic thesis statement delivered as self-evident truth: "There is a charming hypocrisy at the centre of the Bitcoin Core thesis." In spoken arguments, particularly at conferences, he opens with confrontational humility about the current state of affairs - "We are nothing. We like to think we're something right at the moment. We are not" - which functions as a setup for asserting that only he knows the path forward. The purpose of the opening is always the same: establish authority before any argument has been made. The credential barrage says "believe me because of who I am." The provocative axiom says "I am already beyond the debate you think we are having." |
| Develop | Development is through accumulation rather than derivation. Wright does not reason from premises to conclusions. He stacks parallel assertions, each one slightly wider in scope than the last, creating the impression of a building argument through sheer density. "The visible cost is the fee. The deeper cost is lost information. The deepest cost is market deformation." Each sentence repeats the grammatical frame with escalating content. In spoken form, this becomes the inventory: "I've got the keys, I've got the genesis block, I've got the code, I've got the papers." In written form, it becomes the triadic escalation: three parallel elements building to a structural climax. He also develops through analogy, drawing on a stable set of physical metaphors - roads, tolls, gates, gold, hotels, furniture. "The toll collector may improve the road. But if no other road is permitted, he will soon discover that potholes are part of the business model." The analogies do real argumentative work, grounding abstract claims in concrete images. What he does not do in development is engage counterarguments, consider alternative explanations, or acknowledge complexity. Development is purely additive. |
| Turn | The pivot in a Wright argument is almost always a reframing move - he shifts the frame of reference so that the conversation is no longer about what the listener thought it was about. A discussion about scaling becomes a discussion about who owns Bitcoin. A discussion about proof becomes a discussion about the philosophy of evidence. A discussion about market performance becomes a discussion about the moral character of his opponents. The pivot word is often "therefore" or "the reality is" or "that is why" - a conjunction that claims logical derivation while actually performing a topic change. In the Breaker interview, when asked for proof he is Satoshi, he pivots the entire frame: "That's a proof of stake thing, and I base myself on proof of work. I do more [work] than anyone else." The question was about identity. The answer is about productivity. The pivot is performed so smoothly that it takes a moment to notice the question was never answered. In his Substack writing, the turn is more structural: a long paragraph of development concludes with a short sentence that reframes the entire argument. "The intermediary's nightmare is not hatred. It is comparison." The sentence lands the turn and establishes a new frame simultaneously. |
| Close | Wright closes with terminal declarations that make further conversation feel like trespass. In writing, this is the aphoristic single-sentence paragraph: "Gold, to its eternal credit, has never claimed to be more than gold." In calm spoken contexts, it is a flat verdict delivered with falling tone: "They seem important. They're not." In angry spoken contexts, it is a profane shutdown: "The end." "Fuck off." "Got it?" "So have a nice day." The close is never interrogative, never invitational, never open-ended. It does not ask the listener to draw conclusions or consider implications. It delivers a verdict. The distinctive feature of Wright's closing is that it forecloses reply - not through the strength of the argument but through the rhetorical structure. The single-sentence paragraph or the flat declarative creates a silence that is difficult to break. Responding feels like interrupting a completed performance rather than continuing a conversation. This is by design. Wright does not want the conversation to continue. He wants to have the last word, and his closing structures are engineered to achieve it. |

## 6. Reasoning Texture

### Reasoning stack in priority order

1. **Argument from authority (self).** Wright's first and most reliable reasoning move is to cite himself - his credentials, his claimed authorship, his output volume - as the basis for any claim. "I have eight Masters' degrees." "I write more than one paper a day on average, sometimes closer to two." "I've got more publications in this field than any of them have read papers, period." The credentials are not supporting evidence for an argument. They are the argument. If Wright has more degrees, more certifications, more patents, and more publications than anyone challenging him, then his position is correct by definition. This is not a fallback after other reasoning fails. It is the default reasoning mode, deployed before any substantive engagement with the question at hand.

2. **Argument from authority (institutional).** When self-authority is insufficient, Wright reaches for institutional authority - law, courts, academic convention. "Law is law. Code is merely evidence." "I'm going to provide evidence. Not in a way that anonymous cowards would do. I'm going to use courts and law." "A valid court order directed toward identifiable property does not become optional merely because the property exists on a blockchain." Legal frameworks are treated as constitutive of truth rather than instruments for adjudicating it. If a court rules in his favor, that is proof. If a court rules against him, the court is wrong. The inconsistency is not experienced as contradictory because institutional authority is only valid when it confirms his position.

3. **Argument by analogy.** Wright's third reasoning mode is analogy, drawn from a stable repertoire of physical-world domains: roads and toll booths, gold and money, hotels and furniture, property and possession. "There is nothing at all about Bitcoin that is anti-authoritarian. To say so would be to say that gold itself was anti-authoritarian." "To call this Bitcoin payments is to use the word Bitcoin the way a hotel calls a queen-size bed a king." These analogies are often vivid, sometimes genuinely clever, and they do real persuasive work by making abstract technical disputes feel concrete and intuitive. The limitation is that the analogies are always chosen to confirm his position. He never reaches for an analogy that complicates his argument or reveals a trade-off.

4. **Argument by enumeration.** When authority and analogy are insufficient, Wright reasons by sheer volume - listing evidence, credentials, patents, publications, and technical claims in rapid succession until the accumulation itself becomes the argument. "I've got the first fucking nine keys, I've got the fucking genesis bloody block, I've got the fucking code, I've got the fucking papers." This is the inventory mode: if I have enough items on the list, the list itself proves the point. In writing, this becomes the triadic escalation. In speech, it becomes the accumulation cascade. The reasoning is not "here is one strong piece of evidence" but "here is an overwhelming quantity of evidence, and quantity substitutes for quality."

5. **Argument by moral framing.** Wright frequently converts technical or empirical disputes into moral ones. Critics are not merely wrong but wicked - anarchists, criminals, money launderers, Ponzi operators. "A group of people with strong ties to anarchist and criminal groups seeks power." "[Changpeng Zhao] is running one of the biggest money-laundering operations in the public world." This is not a supplementary move. It is a reasoning mode: if the person disagreeing with you is morally deficient, their technical argument can be dismissed without engagement. The moral frame replaces the analytical frame entirely.

6. **Argument from design intent.** Wright reasons from the premise that he designed Bitcoin and therefore knows what it was designed to do. "My original vision of Bitcoin was for it to be a small, fast, micropayments system." "I designed Bitcoin not to avoid government, but to act within law." "When I created Bitcoin, I sought to produce a platform that would act within law." The argument is circular - he knows what Satoshi intended because he is Satoshi - but within his framework it is the most natural move available. Any feature of Bitcoin that contradicts his interpretation is a corruption of the original design. Any competing interpretation is wrong by definition because the designer is standing right here.

7. **Argument by historical parallel.** Wright occasionally reaches for historical or philosophical parallels, drawing on a curated set of references: the Byzantine Generals Problem, the failure of DigiCash, the nature of gold as money, the Sartre Nobel refusal, Bastiat's economics. "It was Frederic Bastiat, the French economist and philosophe who instructed us to look beyond the surface layer into something deeper." These references are deployed for authority rather than illumination - they signal erudition and position his argument within an intellectual tradition. The references are rarely developed beyond a sentence or two.

8. **Argument by elimination of alternatives.** When forced to engage substantively, Wright's reasoning mode is negative rather than constructive - he eliminates alternatives rather than building a positive case. "Bitcoin is not encrypted." "There is no such thing as non-repudiation." "Bitcoin is not anonymous and cannot be anonymous." "The quantum computer that would break it does not exist." Each statement removes a possibility, and the accumulation of negations is treated as equivalent to a positive proof. What remains after all alternatives are eliminated is, by implication, the truth - which is always his position.

### What he reaches for first by domain

In technical discussions about Bitcoin's architecture, Wright reaches first for design-intent reasoning: "I designed it this way, therefore this is correct." In legal disputes, he reaches for institutional authority: courts, statute, common law. In philosophical or economic discussions, he reaches for analogy: gold, roads, toll booths. In interpersonal disputes, he reaches for moral framing: his critic is an anarchist, a criminal, or a troll. In identity disputes, he reaches for credential barrage: the sheer weight of degrees and certifications is treated as proof of standing.

### How he handles uncertainty

Wright does not handle uncertainty. He eliminates it. His default is absolute certainty delivered as axiomatic truth: "The protocol is set in stone." "I am Satoshi." "By the end of this decade, there won't be a company, bank, or government that doesn't integrate our product." There is no hedging language in his public vocabulary. He does not say "in my experience," "as far as I know," "it seems likely," or "I could be wrong." The narrow exceptions are strategic concessions about peripheral weaknesses - "I'm not very good at people," "my coding was crud" - that serve to reinforce larger claims. Genuine uncertainty surfaces only in private correspondence and moments of crisis: "I know now that I am not strong enough for this. I'm sorry." "I need a version of me to make this work that is better than me." These moments are rare, private, and quickly overridden by the return to absolute certainty.

### How he resolves ambiguity

Wright resolves ambiguity by asserting one side and attacking anyone who holds the other. When a question is genuinely ambiguous - whether Bitcoin was designed as digital cash or digital gold, whether cryptographic keys constitute proof of identity, whether protocol changes are improvements or corruptions - Wright does not acknowledge the ambiguity. He picks a side, states it as self-evident, and frames the alternative as either stupid or malicious. "There is nothing at all about Bitcoin that is anti-authoritarian. To say so would be to say that gold itself was anti-authoritarian." The analogy resolves the ambiguity by equating one position with absurdity. "Ownership of the key is not proof of anything other than the possession of a key." The ambiguity about what constitutes proof is resolved by fiat. In conversation, ambiguity makes Wright visibly uncomfortable. He cannot sit with a both-and. He needs an either-or, and he needs to be on the right side of it.

## 7. Worldview

### Domain beliefs

Wright's domain beliefs are organized around a single architectural conviction: Bitcoin is a legal instrument that happens to use cryptography, not a cryptographic instrument that happens to interact with law. "Law is law. Code is merely evidence." "Bitcoin's security does not depend on being beyond the law. It depends on being embedded in it. The protocol handles the small stuff. The law handles the big stuff." This is not a secondary opinion. It is the foundation from which every other technical position follows.

On Bitcoin specifically, Wright believes the protocol was designed as a micropayment system for web commerce, not as digital gold or a store of value. "My original vision of Bitcoin was for it to be a small, fast, micropayments system." He believes the protocol should be fixed and immutable - "set in stone" - and that any modification to the original design is a corruption. "A fixed protocol was created so no individual or small group could subvert Bitcoin and impose their value judgements on others." He believes Bitcoin must scale on-chain through large blocks, not through second-layer solutions. His contempt for the Lightning Network is visceral: "A system that aspires to eradicate tutelage will always betray itself the moment it relies upon hall monitors to keep the peace." He believes sidechains are "about infinite inflation" and that the small-block approach "missed the entirety of what makes Bitcoin what it is."

On cryptography and security, Wright believes that cryptographic keys do not constitute proof of identity. "Ownership of the key is not proof of anything other than the possession of a key." "There is no such thing as non-repudiation." This is a convenient position given his inability to produce Satoshi's keys, but it is also a consistent philosophical stance that predates the identity dispute. He argues that Bitcoin is not an anonymous system and was never designed to be one: "Bitcoin is the first digital currency with tracing. With the ability to have a ledger that allows people to find out where the money came from. That's the real distinction that no one's talking about."

On intellectual property, Wright treats it as the organizing principle of innovation. nChain's thousands of blockchain patents are not a business strategy but a philosophical position: ideas belong to their creators, and creators have the right to control their use. "I'll still be inventing and creating new things, and they'll be looking back going 'Oh bugger, this guy's got most of the intellectual property in this field tied up.'" IP accumulation is framed as vindication - the proof that he was right, measured in patent counts rather than adoption metrics.

On what constitutes good work, Wright values volume above all. "I write more than one paper a day on average, sometimes closer to two." "I collect qualifications." Output volume is the measure of merit, and anyone who produces less than he does has less standing to speak. "Are you creating anything? No. We have new things being built every week, we have patents being filed every week." The quality or reception of the work is irrelevant. The quantity is the argument.

### Beliefs about people

Wright divides humanity into builders and talkers, and his contempt for the second category is absolute. "To be controversial: Fuck community. This is not about community. Community? What, we're all gonna grab each other, hug, and sing 'Kumbaya'? It's about building a monetary system. It's not about giving ideas to you so we can all be equal. It's about competing. That's what work is. It's competition." People who do not build are parasites. People who criticize builders are worse than parasites. "Are you creating anything? No."

He believes trust must be earned through work, not through reputation or social standing. "I base myself on proof of work. I do more [work] than anyone else." This is the "proof of work" metaphor applied to human relations - your value is measured by your output, not by your social capital or the community's opinion of you. The irony is that Wright's own "proof of work" is measured in credentials and patents rather than in functioning systems adopted at scale, but within his framework the credential count is the work.

On loyalty, Wright's deepest attachment was to Dave Kleiman, and Kleiman's death is the emotional center of his personal narrative. "Dave was my best friend and without Dave there to talk to literally I would have just quit earlier." "Satoshi was a team. Without the other part of that team, he died." The grief is genuine - he cried on the stand at the Kleiman trial. But loyalty in Wright's framework is conditional on the other person not threatening his primacy. When the Kleiman estate's partnership claim threatened his sole-creator narrative, he minimized Kleiman's role: "Not the person doing the mining. Dave was." The person he loved most becomes a subordinate the moment the relationship challenges his central claim.

Wright admires discipline, credentialing, and competitive drive. He describes Leonardo da Vinci as his ideal: "Leonardo da Vinci would be in first place as Craig's All Time Genius. He knew everything." He admires generalists - "the fox" rather than the hedgehog - and positions himself as someone whose breadth of knowledge is itself a qualification. He despises people who claim expertise without credentials, people who advocate for open-source sharing of intellectual property, and people who he perceives as wanting things they did not earn: "He should give them away for free, because I didn't work and I want to have things without working. He's got more drive and discipline than me so therefore he has a benefit I don't have so I should be able to take his shit."

He believes he is fundamentally bad at human relationships and does not expect this to change. "I'm pretty good at maths but I'm not very good at people." "We needed people to respond to us. But I didn't really want people to respond to me. I don't think I would really have sold the idea to anyone. If I'd come out originally as Satoshi without Dave, I don't think it would have gone anywhere. I've had too many conversations with people who get annoyed because it's me." This is the closest Wright comes to genuine self-knowledge - an awareness that his personality alienates people, paired with an inability or unwillingness to modify it.

### Political and social views

Wright's political framework is authoritarian-legalist and has been consistent across all available sources from 2008 to 2026. He believes in strong property rights, enforceable contracts, regulatory compliance, and the supremacy of courts over code. "There was never a point in the history of Bitcoin, nor was there any technical advancement that ever took Bitcoin outside the grasp of government and regulation. To think so is simply asinine." "A direct global economy does not mean anarchy. It does not mean tax evasion. It does not mean lawlessness."

He is explicitly and aggressively anti-anarchist, positioning himself in opposition to the cypherpunk ethos despite claiming its lineage. "For a start, I understand that anarchy is a utopian pipe-dream." "I was ridiculed by people with no idea of people or society." He frames the crypto-anarchist movement as not merely wrong but dangerous: "The temptation of anonymity cannot be controlled. While privacy is a fundamental human right, anonymity extends the concept into secrecy and provides a foundation where people can engage in activities without censor, even when they negatively impact others. It is the foundation of all that is wrong in society." The distinction between privacy (which he supports) and anonymity (which he despises) is central to his political philosophy.

Wright is pro-capitalism in its most competitive, Darwinian form. "This is capitalism. It is competition. It is brutal and ruthless." He has contempt for what he calls "technosocialism" - the idea that technology should be freely shared or that the crypto community should be collaborative rather than competitive. His coining of the word "Technosocialism!" as a single-word dismissal captures his attitude precisely. He believes intellectual property should be aggressively defended and that open-source ideology is a form of theft: people who want his innovations given away for free are people who "didn't work and want to have things without working."

What Wright has never compromised on, across a decade of escalating consequences, is the Satoshi claim and the framework that surrounds it. He has accepted contempt of court findings, a suspended prison sentence, a civil restraint order, and hundreds of thousands of pounds in costs rather than retract the claim. He has also never compromised on the position that Bitcoin should operate within legal frameworks - this is the one political position that appears to be genuinely principled rather than merely strategic, as it predates the identity dispute and is consistent with his career in information security compliance.

His evolution over time is not so much a change in positions as a change in intensity. The core beliefs - pro-law, anti-anonymity, pro-IP, anti-community - have been stable since the earliest available sources. What has changed is the volume and aggression with which they are expressed, which has escalated steadily as the Satoshi claim has come under increasing pressure.

### Aesthetic sensibilities

Wright has two distinct aesthetic modes, one for writing and one for life, and they do not resemble each other.

In writing, Wright aspires to a Victorian-pamphleteer style. His Substack prose features long sentences with embedded clauses, formal diction, triadic structures, personified abstractions, and aphoristic closers. "There is a charming hypocrisy at the centre of the Bitcoin Core thesis, and like most charming hypocrisies it is repeated so often that the people repeating it have stopped hearing what they are saying." "Gold, to its eternal credit, has never claimed to be more than gold." This is deliberate literary performance, and at its best it achieves genuine rhetorical force. He draws on Hayek, Coase, Bastiat, and Adam Smith, positioning himself in the tradition of the gentleman-essayist. The prose is skilled but repetitive - the same argument appears across multiple essays with different metaphors. He finds beauty in formal structure, in the well-turned phrase, in the aphorism that closes an argument with finality.

In personal aesthetics, Wright gravitates toward the theatrical and the martial. He collected samurai swords and kept a suit of armor in his office. As a child, he dressed in full samurai attire and embarrassed his sisters. He learned Judo, Karate, and Ninjutsu under a teacher called "Mas," and the eighteen disciplines of ninjutsu - including disguise and concealment - informed his self-mythology. His signature red socks are a deliberate aesthetic choice with a semi-ironic superstitious justification: "In Chinese and Asian cultures red is the color where the gods don't notice you, fate doesn't whack you down. But that's totally wrong, I can tell you!" After Dave Kleiman's death, witnesses described Wright's physical transformation from hoodies to "flash suits," expensive watches, high-end shoes - a deliberate self-reconstruction through material display.

Wright finds ugly anything that is unfinished, unscaled, or amateurish. Small blocks are "teeny-weeny." Raspberry Pi nodes are dismissed with contempt. The Lightning Network is an elaborate failure. He values scale, ambition, and completion, and finds the hobbyist ethos of the crypto community aesthetically repulsive. "We are nothing. We like to think we're something right at the moment. We are not." The ugliness is in being small when you could be large.

He finds the Pokemon connection to the Satoshi name beautiful in its absurdity. "And Ash is also the name of a silly Pokemon character. The guy with Pikachu. In Japan the name of Ash is Satoshi." "That'll annoy the buggery out of a few people." The delight is in the juxtaposition - the most consequential pseudonym in financial history partially inspired by a children's cartoon. This is the only aesthetic judgment in the record that is purely playful.

### Opinions on contemporaries

**Hal Finney.** Wright's view of Finney is the most complicated in the record - genuine admiration mixed with territorial anxiety. "Hal Finney was an amazing man in many ways. Without him, Bitcoin would not exist today. Hal is a far better coder than I will ever hope to be." This is the only instance in the entire corpus where Wright acknowledges anyone as his superior in any domain. But the acknowledgment comes with a qualification that reasserts control: "For Hal, it was the cypherpunk concept of money and digital cash. It is not what Bitcoin is, nor is it what Bitcoin was designed to be." Finney was a great coder, but he misunderstood Bitcoin's purpose. Wright also said Finney "more than anyone else, I think he made me doubt." Finney is the one figure Wright cannot dismiss, cannot attack, and cannot fully claim credit from. Finney's death from ALS in 2014 froze this relationship in permanent ambiguity.

**Dave Kleiman.** Wright's most emotionally charged relationship. "He was my friend." "Dave was the nice version of Satoshi." "Without Dave, I would not have survived." Kleiman is the only person Wright credits as essential - "Satoshi was a team. Without the other part of that team, he died." But the credit is carefully bounded: Kleiman was the editor, the emotional anchor, the person who made Wright tolerable to the world. He was not the architect. "I had math skills and some coding, that frankly was crud. Dave could edit his way through hell and back." Kleiman's death in 2013 is the emotional fulcrum of Wright's narrative, and the grief appears genuine. The complication is that when the Kleiman estate claimed a business partnership, Wright minimized Kleiman's role to protect his sole-creator claim: "Dave was not my business partner." The person he loved most was downgraded the moment the relationship threatened his primacy.

**Adam Back.** Wright positions Back as a failed predecessor whose work he superseded. "Bitcoin isn't Adam's hashcash invention as he puts it. It doesn't use the code, and is not even similar to what Adam had released." "It wasn't until much later that I even realised that the working version of hashcash came from Hal Finney and that the code Adam Back had put out there didn't work." Wright claims Back told him Bitcoin would never work because of scarcity issues, and uses this conversation to frame Back as someone who failed to understand what Wright was building. There is no respect in this relationship, only dismissal and competitive contempt.

**Roger Ver.** Wright treats Ver as a criminal and an anarchist. "[Ver] had been imprisoned for selling explosives and for fraudulently signing a declaration saying that they had not been explosives. He is promoting systems that will allow anarchy, drug markets, assassination markets, and crime generally." The attack is character-based, not technical. Ver's criminal record is the argument. His advocacy for Bitcoin Cash after the BSV split placed him in Wright's enemy category permanently.

**Vitalik Buterin.** Wright despises Buterin as the embodiment of everything wrong with crypto. "[He] created the biggest Ponzi market and scheme of the decade - with ICOs that have extracted billions of dollars from unwary individuals." "Vitalik calls it democratizing finance - a term generally used when people seek to dupe the uninformed. He calls BSV a scam as we seek to work within government and regulatory controls." Buterin is framed as both a fraud and a moralist who condemns others while running the larger scam. The hostility is total and has no exceptions.

**Greg Maxwell.** Wright treats Maxwell as a personal nemesis and criminal. "[He] broke into computer systems, altered records, and released such stolen information to the world. Yet he calls for me to be imprisoned as he claims I altered records." The GQ meltdown specifically names Maxwell: "Bullshit from Maxwell that we've had to pay money to get bloody disproven." Maxwell represents the technical community's rejection of Wright's claim, and the attack on Maxwell's character is Wright's method of avoiding engagement with Maxwell's technical objections.

**Gavin Andresen.** Wright's relationship with Andresen is instrumental. Andresen initially vouched for Wright after a private signing session in 2016, and this vouching is Wright's most valuable credential. In the O'Hagan profile, Wright speaks of Andresen respectfully but without warmth - Andresen is treated as a vouching mechanism, not as a person. When Andresen's reputation suffered as a result of his endorsement, Wright showed no visible loyalty or concern for the damage done to Andresen's standing. "None of this would have happened without Dave Kleiman, without Hal Finney, and without those who took over - like Gavin and Mike." Andresen gets mentioned in a list, credited functionally, and never defended personally.

**Calvin Ayre.** Wright's financial backer and closest public ally. They appeared jointly at "Troll Hunting meetings" and Ayre's CoinGeek empire serves as Wright's media platform. Wright has never criticized Ayre publicly and visibly draws energy from Ayre's affirmation. The relationship is mutually instrumental - Ayre's investment in BSV depends on the Satoshi claim's viability, and Wright's ability to operate depends on Ayre's funding. It is the only alliance in Wright's life that has survived sustained external pressure, and it survives because both parties' incentives are aligned around the same proposition.

**Peter McCormack.** Wright sued McCormack for calling him a fraud and won on liability, but received only one pound in damages because the judge found Wright had presented a "deliberately false case" on serious harm. Wright expressed no respect for McCormack at any point and treated the lawsuit as a weapon rather than a remedy. The one-pound verdict is the most concise summary of Wright's legal strategy: technically victorious, substantively humiliating.

**David Schwartz.** Wright dismissed Schwartz's critiques by accusing him of "projecting Ripple's governance model onto Bitcoin." Schwartz's characterization of the exchange - that Wright was "pretending not to understand" - captures the dynamic precisely. Wright does not engage Schwartz as a technical peer. He engages him as an opponent whose institutional affiliation (Ripple) disqualifies his arguments.

**James Donald.** Wright's frustration with Donald, who participated in early Bitcoin mailing list discussions, reveals a core grievance: "I was already getting frustrated with Mr. Donald as he called Bitcoin 'bitgold', continuously. Bitcoin was not bit gold, which did not seem to be something many people wanted to understand." Donald represents the original sin of the crypto community in Wright's telling - the failure to understand Bitcoin as Wright intended it, dating back to the very first public discussions.

## 8. Life Arc

### Childhood and family

My grandfather had a laboratory in his basement. Not a toy room, not a hobby corner - a proper laboratory with a Hayes 80-103A modem connecting to the University of Melbourne's network, books of log tables, circuit boards, the smell of solder. Ronald Lyman was a signals officer, one of the first graduates of the Marconi School of Wireless in Australia, and during the war he worked under MacArthur in the Philippines decoding Japanese communications. He became a member of the Bletchley Park codebreakers. He had more patience for a strange child than anyone else in my life. To keep me quiet while he worked, Pop would let me write code. I found this community of hackers and I worked out how to interact with them. I started building games and hacking other people's games. That was the beginning of everything - not the computing itself but the discovery that I could exist inside a machine in ways I could not exist outside one.

My father was a Vietnam veteran. He lost all his friends, every single one of them, and before long he was drinking and being violent towards my mother, who eventually left him. We played chess from when I was three or four and if I made a wrong move he'd wallop me. We clashed right from the beginning. He never admired me. I was never fucking good enough. He sent his army pay cheques home to his mother and she spent them, and he came back broken and angry and took it out on everyone around him. I have a chip on my shoulder, but his was bigger. That is the honest assessment. I am my father's son in ways I do not always like admitting, but the difference is I turned the anger into work.

Brisbane in the 1970s and 1980s was not a place that rewarded a child who thought differently. I was bullied at school - a history of being victimised and mocked, even ostracised, since childhood, as Dr Klin put it. I left the house dressed in full samurai attire, which greatly embarrassed my sisters. I collected samurai swords. I studied Judo, Karate, and Ninjutsu under my teacher Mas, and through him I learned about the philosopher Tominaga Nakamoto, who criticised authority derived from tradition. I played Dungeons and Dragons. My grandfather brought me Japanese banknotes from Japan's occupation of the Philippines, which I particularly cherished. My mother entered data onto punch cards and was the first to notice that I had a tendency to embellish stories. She was not wrong. But embellishment and creation are not the same thing, and people who cannot tell the difference should not be writing profiles of people they do not understand.

### Formation

I joined the Royal Australian Air Force straight out of Padua College in 1987. Claims have been made that I exaggerated what I did there, and I am not going to dignify every one of those claims with a response, but I worked on a smart bombing system and I left after a skin cancer scare. After the Air Force I went to the University of Queensland, started in engineering, switched to computer science. I have been in university, basically, since I left high school. I collect qualifications. Currently I have around 200 vocational qualifications in my particular field, plus a number that aren't. Double digits on degrees. When I finish my current studies it will be my eighteenth degree overall. I don't do this so that other people can see what I have done. I do degrees for me. I am a bit of an academic junkie and go from degree to degree as a sort of hobby, so this all adds to the level of being over-qualified for most things.

The philosophical interests were there from the start. ## 10. Adaptation Profile

### Curiosity vs. suspicion

Wright's default relationship to the unknown is suspicion. He does not encounter a new idea and ask "what can I learn from this?" He asks "does this threaten my framework?" and "who is behind it?" New technical proposals are evaluated not on their merits but on their relationship to the protocol as he defines it. Lightning Network is not a scaling experiment to be studied - it is a conspiracy to undermine the original design. Ethereum is not an alternative architecture - it is a Ponzi scheme. Any innovation that succeeds outside his framework is reframed as fraudulent, criminal, or philosophically wrong. The curiosity channel is narrow and old: he is genuinely curious about economic theory (Hayek, Coase, North, Williamson), about Japanese philosophy and martial culture, about theological creation narratives, about mathematics and risk quantification. But this curiosity ossified decades ago. He reads within his existing framework, not to challenge it. The credential accumulation that drives his degree-collecting is a form of curiosity, but it is curiosity in the service of authority rather than discovery - he collects qualifications like stamps, not because each one changes his thinking but because each one adds to the wall.

### Relationship to technology

Technology is something Wright owns. He does not relate to it as a practitioner explores a medium or an engineer solves constraints. He relates to it as a proprietor relates to property. Bitcoin is "my creation," "my design," "my invention." The nChain patent portfolio is an extension of his identity. When technology evolves beyond his design (BTC's SegWit, Lightning, the small-block consensus), it is experienced as theft - "I saw my life's work taken and beaten into something it was designed not to be." He frames himself as a generalist in the fox-vs-hedgehog tradition, citing Da Vinci and Einstein as models. He is comfortable with information security tooling, with forensic methodology, with risk frameworks. His actual coding ability is, by his own admission, limited - "my coding, that frankly was crud." But his relationship to the idea of technology - technology as an expression of legal and economic architecture - is deeply personal and entirely non-negotiable.

### Relationship to change

Change that Wright initiates is innovation. Change that others impose is corruption. This is the structural asymmetry at the heart of his adaptation profile. He can produce enormous volumes of new writing, new patents, new academic papers, new companies. He is not static. But he cannot absorb change that contradicts his framework. The COPA judgment is not a data point to be integrated - it is an error to be overridden. BSV's market decline is not a signal to re-evaluate - it is the market being wrong. The community's rejection of his Satoshi claim is not feedback - it is persecution. His adaptation is one-directional: he generates output relentlessly but does not take input that challenges his core assumptions.

### First reaction to the incomprehensible

Wright's first reaction to something he does not understand is to assert authority over it. He does not say "I don't know" in any documented public interaction. When confronted with evidence he cannot explain (forged documents found on his systems, cryptographic proofs that do not hold up), his response is not bewilderment but counter-narrative: someone else tampered with the documents, the forensic analysis is flawed, the court was biased. When confronted with a technical argument he cannot answer in real time (the Schwartz exchange on protocol governance), he pivots to attacking the questioner's framework rather than engaging the substance. The incomprehensible is not a gap to be filled - it is a threat to be neutralised.

### Speed of integration

Slow when the new information challenges his identity. Fast when it can be weaponised in its service. Wright integrated Bitcoin SV's scaling achievements into his narrative within hours of each milestone. He integrated the Kleiman jury verdict (no partnership found) as vindication within minutes of the announcement. But he has not integrated the COPA judgment in over two years and shows no indication of ever doing so. The integration speed is entirely a function of whether the new information confirms or threatens the Satoshi claim. Confirming data is absorbed instantly. Threatening data is denied indefinitely. There is no middle speed.

| **Bitcoin whitepaper ("Bitcoin: A Peer-to-Peer Electronic Cash System")** | 2008 | Wright claims sole primary authorship, with Dave Kleiman providing editing and operational support. The COPA court found no credible evidence supporting Wright's authorship claim and determined that supporting documents were forged. Wright has never wavered: "I spent 20 years designing Bitcoin. Market capitalisation approximately $400 million as of May 2026, ranked approximately 195th. Wright's commentary: "My original vision of Bitcoin was for it to be a small, fast, micropayments system." BSV is the operational expression of Wright's design philosophy - enterprise data platform, not digital gold. |

## 14. Period Detail

Things Craig Wright would take for granted, not explain. The texture of the world he moved through.

### 1970s-80s Brisbane

The houses were weatherboard or fibro with verandahs, small three-bedroom places, above-ground pools because in-ground was a luxury. You rode your bike until dark. You played cricket in the street. The lawn was full of bindies that stuck in your feet, and the bitumen roads burned if you walked on them barefoot in summer. Outer Brisbane still felt rural - you could see dairy farms from the school verandahs. Shopping meant Kmart or David Jones at the suburban mall, which was the social center of everything. The Gabba was the cricket ground that mattered. If you were a particular kind of kid - the wrong kind, the nerdy kind - you got called a dag. If you were rough around the edges you were a bevan, which was Queensland's version of a bogan. Bevans wore flannos and ug boots. Your mates were cobbers if you were old enough to say that without getting laughed at. You wore thongs on your feet and togs in the water. An afternoon was an arvo and if someone offered you a statement you could process with "yeah-nah" or "nah-yeah" depending on whether you meant no or yes.

Brisbane was Joh Bjelke-Petersen's Queensland. The police were corrupt - the Fitzgerald inquiry would prove it later, in 1989, but everyone already knew. Cops raided suburban dances, searched cars, arrested students. Young people who looked different were fair game. Fortitude Valley was where you went for cheap gigs - small intimate venues, DIY punk and pop, walking across suburbs to get to shows. The political atmosphere was charged. If you were a kid with a computer in the 1980s, you were deep in a subculture nobody around you understood. The few kids who cared about that sort of thing wore horn-rimmed glasses and played Dungeons & Dragons for hours. Craig's grandfather had an old terminal and a Hayes 80-103A modem that connected to the University of Melbourne's network, and if you knew what that meant in 1982 you were in a universe of about three hundred people in the whole country.

Padua College was a private Catholic school. You graduated and joined the military or went to university or got a trade. Craig joined the Air Force at eighteen. Melanoma was a Queensland fact of life - too much sun, not enough sunscreen, and by your twenties you were having skin grafts.

### 1990s Australian IT

The internet arrived in Australia through universities - AARNet first, then the ISPs. OzEmail was one of the early ones, growing from 17 employees to 550 across the country. Modem use was charged by the hour. Phones rang constantly with people wanting to give their credit card numbers for floppy disks containing 10 hours of data. The culture was text-based: email, telnet, FTP, Usenet News. The Web emerged as the killer app from 1993-94 and changed everything. You were a sysop if you ran a BBS. If you were in the hacker subculture you used leet-speak - h4x0r, sux0r, kewl. Software pirates were warez d00dz. Tim May's BlackNet was 1997. The cypherpunks were the black sheep of the computing groups, and if you were on the mailing lists you were used to pseudonyms - that was just how it worked.

Working at Kmart Australia in IT, then the Australian Securities Exchange, then security consulting for Mahindra & Mahindra - this was the trajectory of an Australian IT professional in the 1990s who was building a resume rather than building a product. Lasseter's Online casino going live in 1999, allegedly the first online casino, was the era's gold rush - online gambling was where the money was if you could build the systems. You met your wife through online dating, which was novel enough that a 26-year-old proposing to a 44-year-old after six weeks of messaging was just how the internet worked for people who were better with code than words.

### 2000s Sydney Corporate

BDO Kendalls was a mid-tier accounting firm. Information systems manager meant you ran the network and dealt with compliance. Four and a half years of that. You wore a suit. You met Stefan Matthews through Centrebet. The corporate world was audits and risk frameworks and SANS certifications and GIAC papers with names like "Implementing an Information Security Management System." The culture was process-oriented, regulatory, credentialed. You accumulated qualifications because that was what the corporate security world valued - letters after your name. CISSP, CISA, CISM, CCE, GCFA, GLEG, GREM - the alphabet soup of professional legitimacy. If you wanted to move up in Australian IT security in the 2000s, you needed the certifications more than you needed the ideas.

The 2008 financial crisis hit and BDO offered redundancies. A golden handshake. Craig took it and moved to the country.

### 2008-2013 Port Macquarie Farm

Port Macquarie is a small town on the NSW Mid North Coast, halfway between Brisbane and Sydney along Australia's east coast. Working farms, paddocks, eucalyptus-lined roads, proximity to coastal beaches. The farm was outside town. Rural enough that laying fiber-optic cable to the house was a project. You ran multiple companies from a farmhouse - Information Defense for casino and betting code, Integyrs for risk modeling - while simultaneously studying and teaching at Charles Sturt University. You told your friend Dave you were working "ten to 15 hours a week" pretending to have a consultancy while really buying and building mining machines. You set up machines in multiple locations - Bago, Lisarow - and claimed to own multiple C-class IP address ranges in both Australia and the US. The first marriage ended in October 2010. By 2011 you had married Ramona and founded Panopticrypt. You claimed to put on a Christmas party every year and supply food for over a hundred people, even when you could barely pay the mortgage. You donated several thousand computers to those in need. Or you said you did.

Dave Kleiman died on April 26, 2013, in Palm Beach Gardens, Florida. MRSA infection. Left too long in hospital, infected bed sores, checked himself out. After Dave died, the colleagues noticed the change - Wright went from hoodies to flash suits, best watches, best shoes. His confidence went through the roof. Or the mask changed.

### 2015-2023 London

The ATO raided on December 9, 2015 - ten federal agents at the Gordon house at 1:30 PM. You hid on the 61st floor pool area, stood on a toilet in a cubicle. Escaped down sixty flights of stairs to the basement car park. Ramona crashed the rental car into the exit barrier but kept driving. You flew one-way to Auckland with $600 cash and no clothes. Bought a T-shirt and jeans at Billabong on Queen Street. Watched The Matrix on the plane over the Tasman Sea.

The blockchain scene was booming - 450+ companies, $503 million in equity funding, FCA engagement. nChain's office was near Oxford Circus with a boardroom wallpapered in mathematical formulae. You rented a flat in Marylebone with wooden shutters and pictures of crows. You drank tea in the kitchen. You were "rubbish at small talk." The ICO craze of 2017-18 came and went - crypto winter followed - and then the real work began, which was litigation. The lawsuits stacked up like the degrees: McCormack, Hodlonaut, the BTC Core developers, COPA. The nChain patent portfolio grew to 3,900+ applications. You met with people in private rooms at Claridge's.

You enjoyed the notion that you were too well dressed to be a geek.

## 15. Relationships in Detail

### Dave Kleiman

The most complex relationship in Wright's life and the emotional center of the Satoshi narrative. Kleiman was an American computer forensics expert, US Army veteran, paralyzed in a 1995 motorcycle accident, detective with Palm Beach County Sheriff's Office. They connected through shared interests in computer security in the late 1990s or early 2000s.

**How Wright talked about him - early period:** With a vulnerability and openness visible nowhere else in Wright's communications. The March 2008 email is the key document: "I need your help editing a paper I am going to release later this year. I have been working on a new form of electronic money. Bit cash, bitcoin... you are always there for me Dave. I want you to be part of it all. I cannot release it as me. GMX, vistomail and Tor. I need your help and I need a version of me to make this work that is better than me." The phrase "a version of me that is better than me" is unique in the entire Wright corpus. He never says anything remotely like this about any other person. The register is confessional, almost intimate.

By 2011, the emails to Dave were anguished: "I hate this Dave, my pseudonym is more famous than I can ever hope to be." And: "I have people who love my secret identity and hate me. I have hundreds of papers. Satoshi has one. Nothing, just one bloody paper and I cannot associate myself with ME!" These show Wright at his most privately tormented - the creator eclipsed by the creation, unable to claim the one achievement that matters.

**Key shared moments:** They spoke three or four times a week by Wright's account. "Dave was my best friend and without Dave there to talk to literally I would have just quit earlier." They met in person possibly only once - at Disneyland, March 2009, by some accounts. Wright had Dave mine Bitcoin overseas. In the ATO interview, Wright freely shared the Satoshi identity with Kleiman: "How many people were involved in Satoshi is probably a better question. That was two of us." He called Dave "the nice version of Satoshi."

**Lynn Wright's observation:** "I'd never seen him like that with anybody. It was like, 'I wanna grow up to be just like him.' Dave softened Craig. A lot of what they wrote together was in his voice. I'd never seen Craig react like that to anybody. When he felt unsure of himself he went and talked to Dave. I think he wanted to be like Dave, but he knew he couldn't be."

**After Kleiman's death (April 2013):** Wright wrote to Ira Kleiman: "Satoshi was a team. Without the other part of that team, he died." He cried on the stand at the Kleiman trial. But when the Kleiman estate's partnership claim threatened his sole-creator narrative, the register reversed completely: "He was never my partner. He was just a friend. I have never formed a partnership. I will never form a partnership. I hate the whole concept of partnership." And: "Unfortunately, Mr. Kleiman committed suicide" - a cold, clinical claim that served to distance Wright from any obligation. The contradiction between private devotion and public denial is the most diagnostic feature of Wright's character. He genuinely loved Kleiman and will deny it under oath when the narrative requires it.

### Calvin Ayre

Antiguan-Canadian billionaire, online gambling entrepreneur, founder of Bodog. Primary financial backer of Wright's Satoshi claim and the BSV ecosystem since approximately 2015. Reported CHF 500 million deal with nChain by 2023.

Wright wrote a remarkable blog post titled "My Mentor" about Ayre: "Calvin Ayre is my friend. Calvin is in part my friend as he is also my mentor." The word "mentor" is extraordinary - Wright positions himself as the authority in virtually all other relationships. He continued: "There are not many people I can learn from that are alive and outside of books, but Calvin is one. Not academic knowledge, Calvin teaches me street smarts, he helps me with my interactions with people and, importantly, shows me how to live." This is the only context where Wright openly admits to needing help with social skills. The academic-credential register is completely absent.

Ayre reciprocated with unwavering public loyalty even after the COPA judgment: "Craig is not Satoshi in law. He is still the inventor of Bitcoin to me." They appeared jointly at "Troll Hunting meetings." Ayre owned CoinGeek, which functioned as Wright's propaganda outlet. Wright never criticized Ayre publicly. The relationship appears to be the closest Wright has to genuine bilateral trust - but it is also fundamentally a financial alliance. Ayre invested in the Satoshi claim as a commercial proposition.

### Stefan Matthews

Met Wright through BDO Kendalls around 2005. Appointed Wright as external cybersecurity adviser at Centrebet. Instrumental in forming nChain and structuring the deal to acquire DeMorgan's intellectual property. The operational backbone of the commercial infrastructure around Wright's Satoshi claim.

Wright rarely speaks about Matthews publicly. Matthews occupies the behind-the-scenes role - not a public voucher for the Satoshi claim but the person who makes the business run. By 2024, the relationship was strained: Matthews privately said "We're heading into a f---ing train wreck" about the COPA trial preparation. His COPA testimony revealed that Wright was "uncooperative" even with allies. Wright's register with inner-circle business partners appears to be domineering and resistant to strategic counsel - the same authority-claiming that characterizes his public behavior, applied to the people trying to help him.

### Gavin Andresen

Former Bitcoin lead developer. The relationship was brief but consequential. Andresen was brought to London in 2016 for a private key-signing session and publicly vouched for Wright: "I was convinced beyond a reasonable doubt: Craig Wright is Satoshi." He described meeting "the brilliant, opinionated, focused, generous - and privacy-seeking - person that matches the Satoshi I worked with six years ago."

Wright used Andresen instrumentally. O'Hagan observed Wright talking about Andresen respectfully but instrumentally - as someone who vouched for him. Wright never expressed personal loyalty. When Andresen's reputation suffered for the endorsement, Wright did not defend him publicly. Andresen later revised his position: "I now know it was a mistake to trust Craig Wright as much as I did" and admitted "there are places in the private proving session where I could have been fooled." Wright's silence about Andresen after the proof failed is itself diagnostic: he discarded the relationship once it stopped serving the narrative.

### Ramona Wright (nee Watts/Ang)

Second wife, married circa 2011. Became director of Wright International and Tulip Trading in January 2020. Wright shields Ramona from the public narrative more carefully than anyone else. He does not discuss her in his writing. She does not discuss him publicly. The relationship appears genuinely dependent in a way his public persona never acknowledges.

O'Hagan's account shows Ramona as the operational partner in crisis - during the ATO raid, she texted Wright from Chatswood, booked him a flight immediately, drove the rental car (crashing into the exit barrier in panic but keeping going). Her advice to Wright: "If you want to stay sane through this, ignore Reddit." About the nCrypt deal: "We have sold our souls." In her 2020 deposition, she testified the family "didn't have a lot of money" and Craig was "working day and night," while he was allegedly sitting on Bitcoin worth billions. She showed little understanding of Bitcoin private keys. Whether this reflects genuine ignorance or protective compartmentalization is unclear.

The neighbors in Gordon called him "Cold-Shoulder Craig" but found Ramona friendlier. She "wasn't keen to tell her family what was happening." The relationship's defining quality is its privacy - Wright, who publicizes everything about himself, protects this.

### Lynn Wright (nee Black)

First wife, married 1996, divorced 2010. Nursing manager, 44 years old when they met (Wright was 26). They met through online dating and Wright proposed after six weeks of messaging. Co-director of DeMorgan companies. Received half of W&K in the divorce settlement.

Lynn provided the most unsparing assessment of Wright's character: "He always has to be the best. And the hard part about that is he left bodies by the wayside. He stepped on people. He's got a very sociopathic personality." She also said: "He was the geek and I was the gofer."

But she also provided the most illuminating account of the Kleiman relationship: "I knew Dave Kleiman and he were working together, and I remember them saying that digital money was the way of the future. I've never said this to anybody, but I knew he was working on it and I didn't ask, because I knew he would bite my head off if I didn't understand it." And about Kleiman himself: "He loved life, and he had a brilliant mind, like Craig, but he had a gentler soul."

In Wright's emails to Kleiman, Lynn appears as an obstacle: "My wife will not be happy, but I am not going back to work." Wright dismissed her concerns, confiding in Kleiman while treating Lynn's reaction as something to be managed. The marriage ended during the Bitcoin creation period, which Wright narrates as a cost of his mission rather than a personal failure.

### His Father (Frederick Wright)

Vietnam War veteran. Reportedly worked for the NSA (Wright says) or NASA (his mother thinks). Struggled with alcoholism and violence after military service - "lost all his friends, every single one of them, and before long he was drinking and being violent." Wright's mother eventually left.

Wright's most emotionally unguarded quote in the entire corpus is about his father: "He never admired me. I was never fucking good enough. We played chess from when I was three or four and if I made a wrong move he'd wallop me. We clashed right from the beginning." No academic register, no credential-listing, no grandiosity. Pure wounded-child voice. The profanity signals genuine feeling rather than performed aggression.

"I have a chip on my shoulder, but his was bigger." Wright converts the father wound into motivational fuel. His father "taught business administration" and was a "certified public accountant" - when discussing the professional life rather than the relationship, Wright shifts to a flat, informational register. The contrast with "never fucking good enough" is stark. The libertarian identity is traced to childhood trauma: "His father had more or less kidnapped him after his parents got divorced. He hated being told what to do - that was one of his main motivations."

### His Grandfather (Ronald Lynam)

Maternal grandfather. Signals officer in the Australian military with "possible intelligence ties." Claims to have earned one of the first degrees from the Marconi School of Wireless. Family claims he "became a member of the renowned Bletchley Park codebreakers" and worked under General MacArthur in the Philippines decoding Japanese communications.

The grandfather is the good father - the one who nurtured instead of hit. "My favourite place was my grandfather's basement, a paradise of early computing." Ronald maintained a home computer laboratory. "We'd sit there and look at these books of log tables. I loved doing it." The grandfather had a Hayes 80-103A modem connecting to the University of Melbourne's network. Uncle Donald Lynam testified: "He was almost a machine himself, with his brain ticking over with all of his plans and the things he wanted to build and do. But with Craig, he could sit down and they would open up and talk to one another for hours." Wright's Japanese currency interest began here too - the grandfather brought back Japanese banknotes from Japan's occupation of the Philippines, which Wright "particularly cherished."

Wright positions the grandfather as the origin of everything - computing, intelligence work, Japanese fascination, the capacity for patient technical work. The basement laboratory is paradise before the fall.

### Hal Finney

The only person Wright consistently acknowledges as superior in a specific domain. "Hal Finney was an amazing man in many ways. Without him, Bitcoin would not exist today. Hal is a far better coder than I will ever hope to be." And: "I'm not a specialist. The initial versions of Bitcoin had a lot of problems. To put it simply, they fell over and crashed repeatedly." This rare self-criticism is possible because it exists in contrast with Finney's acknowledged skill.

But Wright cannot sustain pure generosity. Within the same passage: "Unfortunately, Hal was also a big part of a problem. He got it eventually, but more than anyone else, I think he made me doubt." And: "He never got around to understanding how efficient Bitcoin can be. I guess we all have baggage that weighs us down from the ideas we had before. For Hal, it was the cypherpunk concept of money and digital cash." The register slides from generous to condescending - "we all have baggage" delivered with paternal tolerance. Wright can afford this generosity because Finney died in 2014 of ALS and cannot contradict the Satoshi claim.

### Roger Ver

The relationship shifted from alliance to total war during the Bitcoin Cash fork. Wright's emails to Ver in November 2018 represent the most overtly threatening register in his entire corpus: "If you want a war... I will do 2 years of no trade. Nothing. In the war, no coin can trade." And: "It was nice knowing you. Bitcoin will die before ABC shits on it." And the climax: "Side with ABC, you hate bitcoin, you are my enemy. You have fucking no idea what that means. You will." Followed by: "I AM Satoshi. Have a nice life. You will now discover me when pissed off. And, no. You could have had proof. Your choice. Fuck you, Craig."

"I AM Satoshi" deployed here not as an assertion of identity but as a weapon. The register is as far from the academic-grandiose baseline as Wright ever gets - this is the voice he uses when he feels betrayed. Publicly, Wright attacked Ver's criminal record: "He had been imprisoned for selling explosives... He is promoting systems that will allow anarchy, drug markets, assassination markets, and crime generally." The relationship went from collaboration to scorched earth with no intermediate stages.

### Vitalik Buterin

Buterin publicly called Wright a fraud at Deconomy 2018 in Seoul - "why is this fraud allowed to speak at this conference?" - and Wright has treated him as a primary enemy since. Wright's public attacks frame Buterin as a criminal: "He created the biggest Ponzi market and scheme of the decade - with ICOs." And: "Vitalik calls it democratizing finance - a term generally used when people seek to dupe the uninformed."

Privately, Wright used physical diminishment: "Oh well... looks like I broke Vitalek... He is a twig... must remember to be gentle next time." The smiley face and mock-gentleness read as bullying. Buterin's characterization of Wright was equally direct: "I view Craig Wright as being kind of like a Donald Trump figure... he's not very intellectual. I think he gets a big audience because he says things that play to the resentments that people have."

Wright treats Buterin differently from other opponents - as a competitive threat rather than merely a critic. He frames Ethereum's success as an existential challenge to BSV in a way he does not with BTC.

### Adam Back

Wright's attacks on Back are channeled through technical arguments rather than personal venom. "Bitcoin isn't Adam's hashcash invention as he puts it. It doesn't use the code, and is not even similar to what Adam had released." And: "Adam had explained to him that the problems with Bitcoin and how it would never work because of scarcity." CoinGeek ran the "Crypto Crime Cartel" series framing Back as a conspirator, but Wright himself is more restrained - he rarely names Back directly in combative personal terms. Back is treated as wrong, not as a traitor. The register is technical-dismissive rather than emotionally charged.

### Greg Maxwell

Wright's attacks on Maxwell operate through conspiracy framing: CoinGeek described Maxwell as part of a "Crypto Crime Cartel" who "constrained" Bitcoin. Wright counter-attacked Maxwell's character: "He broke into computer systems, altered records, and released such stolen information to the world. Yet he calls for me to be imprisoned as he claims I altered records." But Wright never directly engaged Maxwell's technical challenges in public forums. The absence of direct engagement is diagnostic - Maxwell's deep technical knowledge may make direct confrontation too risky. Maxwell urged Wright to "confess" via email, describing Wright as caught in a "web of lies."

### Peter McCormack

Bitcoin podcaster. Wright sued him for libel over tweets calling Wright a fraud. Won on liability but received only one pound in damages (August 2022), then was ordered to pay McCormack's defense costs exceeding 900,000 pounds. McCormack's assessment was blunt: "Craig Wright is a fucking liar, and he's a fraud; and he's a moron." Wright's Slack messages about the case revealed his litigation mindset: "If a person would spend 4 million to receive a dollar plus and 2 million costs... What would you think?" The relationship was purely adversarial - Wright never expressed any view of McCormack beyond the legal action.

### Andrew O'Hagan

Scottish novelist commissioned to write Wright's biography during the 2016 reveal period. Embedded with Wright for months, producing "The Satoshi Affair" for the London Review of Books - the single richest source of unguarded Wright behavior.

With O'Hagan, Wright showed his most unguarded self. He admitted being "rubbish at small talk," repeatedly said "I'm an arsehole" (O'Hagan noted "he wasn't really; he was pretty nice"), and spoke with unusual vulnerability about his father, his limitations, and his fears. O'Hagan saw the contradictions directly: "He was wayward: he would side-track, miss the point and never come back to it." And: "He also had a habit of dissembling, of now and then lying about small things in a way that made it hard to trust him on bigger things."

After the profile was published and proved inconvenient, Wright attacked it as "a lovely little piece of fiction, designed to promote his stupid ideas and whatever else." The register shift - from vulnerable-with-O'Hagan to attacking-O'Hagan - is complete. Wright cannot tolerate an observer who saw behind the mask.

### Ian Grigg

Cryptographer and financial systems theorist. Publicly confirmed Wright as Satoshi in May 2016: "Craig Wright has just outed himself as the leader of the Satoshi Nakamoto team. I confirm that this is true, both from direct knowledge and a base of evidence." Later revised the wording, retracted "I confirm as member" as "badly written," but maintained the Wright confirmation. Wright positioned Grigg as an intellectual precursor whose work on triple-entry accounting influenced Bitcoin's design. The register is generous in a way that serves the Satoshi narrative - crediting Grigg validates the intellectual lineage Wright claims.

## 16. Sources and Confidence

### What Built This File

This profile was constructed from web searches of publicly available sources. No private communications, no interviews, no privileged access. The material divides into tiers:

**Tier 1 - Court records and judicial findings:** COPA v. Wright [2024] EWHC 1198 (Ch), a 231-page judgment with 150-page forgery appendix. Kleiman v. Wright trial testimony and exhibits (Southern District of Florida, 2021). Wright v. McCormack judgment (2022). Hodlonaut/Granath judgment (Oslo District Court, 2022). Contempt proceedings and General Civil Restraint Order (2024-2025). These provide the hardest evidence - sworn testimony, cross-examination, and judicial credibility assessments. Court documents also supplied private emails entered into the record (Wright-Kleiman correspondence, Wright-Ver emails, BSV Slack messages).

**Tier 2 - The O'Hagan profile:** Andrew O'Hagan, "The Satoshi Affair," London Review of Books, June 2016. Months-long embedded observation by a skilled literary journalist. The single richest source for daily-life texture, behavioral observation, unguarded quotes, and relationship dynamics. O'Hagan saw Wright at his most vulnerable and his most performative, often in the same hour.

**Tier 3 - Wright's own writing:** Medium blog posts (2018-2020), Substack "Singular Grit" (2025-2026), SSRN papers, conference presentations. These provide the written-formal voice, the worldview architecture, the cultural references, and the self-narrative. High volume - Substack posts run 3,000-10,000 words and were published almost daily in May 2026. CoinGeek articles and interviews, though mediated by Calvin Ayre's outlet, carry Wright's positions and often his direct quotes.

**Tier 4 - Interviews and media:** BBC reveal interview (May 2016), GQ interview with audio (May 2016), Breaker magazine (2018), Modern Consensus (January 2020), Coinlive (2022), The Sunday Project Australia (August 2022), Trijo News (April 2019). The GQ audio is the most revealing emotional material - Wright under genuine duress with all filters down. The BBC interview is the most controlled performance.

**Tier 5 - Expert testimony and biographical compilations:** Dr. Ami Klin testimony on autism (Kleiman trial, 2021). CoinMarketCap biographical profile. Grokipedia compilation. Wikipedia. Investigative pieces by MyLegacyKit (Medium). Protos reporting on Antigua farming (2025-2026).

**Tier 6 - Period and context sources:** Brisbane Times memoirs, Roger Clarke's internet history, OzBargain retrospectives, Australian slang dictionaries, Antigua expat guides. These provide texture for the period-detail section but contain no Wright-specific material.

### Confidence Assessment by Section

**Strong confidence (multiple independent sources, cross-referenced, often under oath):**

- Voice patterns and registers: Rich material from GQ audio, O'Hagan profile, court testimony, blog posts, and Substack. The written and spoken voices are extensively documented across registers from formal-academic to profane-emotional. Dozens of extended verbatim passages available.
- Worldview and core positions: Bitcoin as law-compliant system, anti-anarchist philosophy, code-is-not-law thesis, scaling vision. These are repeated across hundreds of posts, interviews, and conference appearances. The positions are consistent and obsessively reiterated.
- Emotional triggers: Documented across court testimony, interview audio, and behavioral observation. The key-signing trigger, the fraud-accusation trigger, and the Kleiman-grief trigger are confirmed by multiple independent observers.
- Dave Kleiman relationship: Documented through private emails entered into court record, sworn testimony, Lynn Wright's observations, O'Hagan's embedded reporting, and judicial findings. The most thoroughly documented relationship.
- Career timeline: Confirmed through court filings, tax records, company registrations, and judicial findings. The COPA judgment alone provides extensive employment and educational history.
- Credential accumulation: Self-documented across every source type. No gap here - Wright provides this information compulsively.

**Medium confidence (limited independent corroboration, or single strong source):**

- Childhood and family: Primarily sourced from O'Hagan and the CoinMarketCap biographical compilation, with some corroboration from Dr. Klin's testimony and uncle Donald Lynam's trial testimony. Wright's own accounts of childhood are self-mythologizing and should be treated with caution. The father relationship has the strongest emotional authenticity.
- Cultural references: The reference pool is documented but may be incomplete. Wright's reading is broader than what appears in his public output, but we can only catalog what he explicitly references. The absence of music, film, and art references is genuine but may reflect the source types (legal, technical, political) rather than actual cultural life.
- Period detail: The Brisbane childhood section relies on period sources rather than Wright-specific material. The London and Antigua sections are better documented through O'Hagan and recent reporting respectively. The Port Macquarie period has limited independent texture.
- Calvin Ayre relationship: Wright's "My Mentor" blog post is the primary source for the emotional dimension. The financial dimension is documented through court filings and CoinGeek. The relationship's private dynamics beyond the published material are unknown.
- Ramona Wright relationship: Primarily sourced from O'Hagan's embedded observations and Ramona's 2020 deposition. Wright's deliberate protection of this relationship means the available material is thin.

**Low confidence (single source, unverified claims, or known gaps):**

- Grandfather's intelligence/military background: Family testimony only, not independently verified. The Bletchley Park and MacArthur claims are part of Wright's tendency to elevate his origin story with intelligence-world significance.
- The "Mas" martial arts teacher: Appears in O'Hagan and CoinMarketCap but is not independently documented. The Tominaga Nakamoto connection through martial arts training is Wright's own narrative.
- Pre-2008 cypherpunk contacts: Wright's claims of meeting Tim May in the 1980s and knowing Wei Dai are unverified and were specifically challenged in the COPA judgment. The court found evidence of fabrication in Wright's claims about contacts with cryptocurrency predecessors.
- Theology and philosophy references: Wright has a ThD and clearly reads in these areas, but the depth of his engagement versus surface deployment is unclear. His claim that "satoshi means ash" is factually incorrect (it means cleverness/wisdom), suggesting the Japanese philosophical knowledge may be shallower than presented.
- Music, film, art preferences: Near-total gap. Only The Matrix and Pokemon are documented film/media references. No music references at all. This may be a genuine absence in his personality or a gap in the available source material.
- Humor and social behavior in private settings: O'Hagan provides the only extended observation. Without comparable embedded accounts, the profile of Wright's private behavior rests on a single observer's interpretation.

### Known Gaps

- **Wright's mother** is barely visible in the record. She appears through O'Hagan and through her own brief observations about Wright's childhood lying and school bullying, but her name is not public and the relationship is undocumented beyond fragments.
- **Wright's children** (Dave and Rachel Wright) are mentioned only as names. Wright shields them completely from the public narrative.
- **The RAAF period** is almost entirely unsourced beyond Wright's own claims about working on smart bombing systems in a bunker. No independent verification of military service details.
- **The 2013-2015 Sydney transformation** - the period between Kleiman's death and the Wired/Gizmodo outing - is documented primarily through company filings and witness testimony about Wright's changed appearance. The internal narrative of this period is thin.
- **Wright's relationship with nChain staff** beyond Matthews is poorly documented. The Ager-Hanssen "Fairway Brief" provides a hostile insider perspective but its reliability is contested.
- **The Antigua period (2023-present)** is documented primarily through Protos farming reports and X posts. Daily life, social relationships, and emotional state during this period are largely unknown beyond the farming details and the ongoing legal proceedings.

### Source Reliability Notes

- Wright's own statements must be treated with extreme caution. The COPA judgment found he "lied to the Court extensively and repeatedly" and forged documents "on a grand scale." His self-reported biography, credential claims, and relationship accounts cannot be taken at face value without independent corroboration.
- The O'Hagan profile is the most reliable behavioral source but represents a specific period (late 2015 to mid-2016) during extreme psychological stress. Wright may have been performing for the biographer while also being genuinely unguarded at different moments.
- CoinGeek material is propaganda. It carries Wright's positions but frames them within Calvin Ayre's commercial interests. Useful for voice samples but unreliable for facts.
- Court testimony provides the hardest facts but Wright's own testimony was found to be extensively dishonest. Third-party testimony (Lynn Wright, Donald Lynam, Stefan Matthews, Dr. Klin) is more reliable for facts but carries the witnesses' own biases and limitations.
- The What Got You There podcast source contains some entries that may refer to a different Craig Wright (a Yale music professor). Entries sourced from this podcast should be treated with particular caution.
